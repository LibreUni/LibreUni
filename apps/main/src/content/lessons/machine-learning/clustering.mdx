# Finding Structure in Unlabeled Data

Clustering is an unsupervised learning task where the objective is to partition a dataset into groups (clusters) such that data points within a group are more similar to each other than to those in other groups. This is a journey of discovery: we don't have target labels to guide the model; instead, the model must uncover the underlying geometry of the feature space.

## The Taxonomy of Clustering Algorithms

Clustering approaches are generally divided by their logic:
1. **Centroid-based**: Groups points around a central mean or medoid (e.g., K-Means).
2. **Density-based**: Connects points in regions of high point density (e.g., DBSCAN).
3. **Distribution-based**: Assumes data is generated from a mixture of statistical distributions (e.g., Gaussian Mixture Models).
4. **Connectivity-based**: Builds a hierarchy of nested clusters (e.g., Hierarchical Clustering).

## K-Means: The Centroid Archetype

K-Means is the most ubiquitous clustering algorithm due to its efficiency and simplicity. It is an iterative algorithm that seeks to minimize the distance between points and their respective cluster centers.

### The Algorithm: Lloyd's Method
1. **Initialize**: Randomly choose $K$ points to be the initial centroids.
2. **Assignment Step**: Assign each data point $x^{(i)}$ to the nearest centroid $\mu_j$ based on Euclidean distance:
   $$ c^{(i)} = \arg\min_j ||x^{(i)} - \mu_j||^2 $$
3. **Update Step**: Calculate the new centroids by taking the mean of all points assigned to that cluster:
   $$ \mu_j = \frac{1}{|C_j|} \sum_{i \in C_j} x^{(i)} $$
4. **Convergence**: Repeat until centroids stop shifting or a maximum number of iterations is reached.

### Objective Function: Inertia
K-Means minimizes the **Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS)**, also known as Inertia:
$$ J = \sum_{j=1}^{K} \sum_{i \in C_j} ||x^{(i)} - \mu_j||^2 $$

### Challenges and Solutions
- **Standardization**: Since K-Means relies on Euclidean distance, features with larger scales will dominate. **Always scale your data.**
- **Local Minima**: K-Means is sensitive to the initial placement of centroids. The **K-Means++** initialization algorithm improves this by spreading initial centroids far apart.
- **Specifying K**: How do we know how many clusters exist? 
  - **The Elbow Method**: Plot WCSS vs $K$ and look for the "elbow" where the rate of improvement drops.
  - **Silhouette Analysis**: Measures how close each point in one cluster is to points in the neighboring clusters.

## DBSCAN: Clustering by Density

K-Means fails miserably when clusters have non-spherical shapes (like crescents or rings) or when the data contains many outliers. **DBSCAN** (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) solves this by focusing on point density.

### Logic
1. A point is a **Core Point** if it has at least `MinPts` neighbors within a radius of $\epsilon$ (Epsilon).
2. A point is a **Border Point** if it is not a Core point but is reachable from a Core point.
3. Everything else is **Noise**.

### Why use DBSCAN?
- **Arbitrary Shapes**: It can find clusters that snake through the feature space.
- **Outlier Detection**: It explicitly identifies points that don't belong to any cluster as -1 (Noise).
- **Number of Clusters**: It discovers the number of clusters automatically.

## Hierarchical Clustering: The Tree of Life

Instead of choosing $K$ upfront, Hierarchical Clustering builds a hierarchy of relationships.
- **Agglomerative (Bottom-Up)**: Every point starts as a cluster. The two closest clusters are merged iteratively until only one cluster remains.
- **Divisive (Top-Down)**: All points start in one cluster. The cluster is split iteratively until every point is its own cluster.

### Linkage Criteria
How do we define the "distance" between two *clusters*?
- **Single Linkage**: Distance between the two closest points in the clusters.
- **Complete Linkage**: Distance between the two farthest points.
- **Ward's Linkage**: Minimizes the variance of the clusters being merged (most common).

## Evaluating Performance without Labels

Since there is no "ground truth", we use internal validation metrics:

### 1. Silhouette Coefficient
Ranges from -1 to 1. 
- $1$: Point is very far from neighboring clusters.
- $0$: Point is on the boundary between two clusters.
- $-1$: Point is likely in the wrong cluster.

### 2. Calinski-Harabasz Index
The ratio of the sum of between-clusters dispersion and of within-cluster dispersion. A higher score signifies better separation.

## Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM): Soft Clustering

Standard K-Means is a "hard clustering" algorithm—every point belongs to exactly one cluster. **Gaussian Mixture Models** provide "soft clustering". They assume that the data is generated from a mixture of $K$ Gaussian distributions.

For every point, GMM provides a probability distribution across all clusters (e.g., 80% Cluster A, 20% Cluster B). This is valuable for handling overlapping groups and provides information about the uncertainty of the cluster assignments.

## Practical Use Cases

Clustering is the engine behind many real-world systems:
- **Customer Segmentation**: Identifying groups of customers with similar buying behaviors for targeted marketing.
- **Image Compression**: Using K-Means to reduce the number of colors in an image (Color Quantization).
- **Anomaly Detection**: Identifying fraudulent transactions by finding points that are far from any existing dense cluster.
- **Biological Taxonomy**: Grouping genes with similar expression patterns to understand biological functions.

## Conclusion

Clustering allows us to make sense of data where labels are expensive or impossible to obtain. By choosing between centroid, density, or distribution-based methods, and carefully validating results using silhouette scores, we can transform a chaotic cloud of points into actionable insights about the latent structure of our world.
