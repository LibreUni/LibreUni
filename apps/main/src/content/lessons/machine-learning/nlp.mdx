# Language as Data

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the computational study of human language. The primary challenge is that language is discrete, symbolic, and ambiguous, while machine learning models operate on continuous numerical vectors. The goal of NLP pipelines is to transform raw text into high-dimensional representations that capture semantic meaning.

## 1. Text Preprocessing and Tokenization

Before a model can "read" text, the raw string must be cleaned and broken into units called **tokens**.

- **Tokenization**: Splitting text into words, characters, or sub-words (e.g., Byte-Pair Encoding).
- **Stop-word Removal**: Removing frequent but low-information words like "the", "is", "at".
- **Stemming/Lemmatization**: Reducing words to their base form (e.g., "running" $\rightarrow$ "run").
- **Normalization**: Converting to lowercase, removing punctuation, and handling special characters/emojis.

## 2. Statistical Vectorization (Sparse Embeddings)

### Bag-of-Words (BoW)
In BoW, we represent a document as a vector of word counts. If our vocabulary has $V$ unique words, each document becomes a $V$-dimensional vector.
*Problem*: BoW loses all information about word order and gives too much weight to frequent words.

### TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency)
TF-IDF re-weights word counts to emphasize words that are important to a specific document but rare across the entire corpus.

$$ \text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \text{TF}(t, d) \times \log\left(\frac{N}{DF(t)}\right) $$

where $TF$ is the frequency of term $t$ in document $d$, $N$ is the total number of documents, and $DF$ is the number of documents containing $t$.

## 3. Distributed Representations (Word Embeddings)

Traditional vectorization (BoW/TF-IDF) results in "sparse" vectors where the distance between "King" and "Royal" is the same as between "King" and "Apple". **Word Embeddings** solve this by mapping words to dense, low-dimensional vectors in a continuous space.

### Word2Vec: The Distributional Hypothesis
Word2Vec is based on the idea that "a word is characterized by the company it keeps" (Firth, 1957).
- **Skip-gram**: Given a target word, predict the context words.
- **CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)**: Given context words, predict the target word.

The model uses a shallow neural network to learn weights. Once trained, the internal weights represent the word embeddings.

### Algebraic Properties
Word embeddings capture semantic analogies through vector arithmetic:
$$ Vector(\text{"King"}) - Vector(\text{"Man"}) + Vector(\text{"Woman"}) \approx Vector(\text{"Queen"}) $$

## 4. Sequence Modeling: RNNs and LSTMs

Language is inherently sequential; the meaning of a word depends on what came before it. 

### Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)
RNNs process sequences word-by-word, maintaining a "hidden state" $h_t$ that acts as a memory:
$$ h_t = \sigma(W_{hh} h_{t-1} + W_{xh} x_t + b) $$
*The Vanishing Gradient Problem*: As sequences get longer, the gradients used to update the beginning of the sequence during backpropagation shrink to zero, causing the model to "forget" long-term dependencies.

### LSTMs and GRUs
Gated units like **Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)** introduced "gates" (input, forget, and output) to control the flow of information. The "Cell State" acts as a highway, allowing gradients to flow back through long sequences without vanishing.

## 5. The Bottleneck of RNNs

Despite the success of LSTMs, they suffer from two major flaws:
1. **Sequential Nature**: They cannot be parallelized easily since $h_t$ depends on $h_{t-1}$.
2. **Contextual Limitation**: They struggle to capture relationships between distant words (e.g., the subject of a very long sentence determining the verb conjugation).

These limitations led to the development of the **Attention Mechanism** and eventually the **Transformer**, which we explore in the next module.

## Implementation Summary
- **Use NLTK/SpaCy** for fast preprocessing (tokenization, lemmatization).
- **Use Scikit-learn** for statistical models (TF-IDF, Naive Bayes).
- **Use Gensim** for Word2Vec or GloVe embeddings.
- **Use PyTorch/TensorFlow** for sequence modeling with LSTMs or Transformers.
