# Derivatives: Rates of Change

The derivative is the mathematical tool for measuring **change**. If limits are about "approaching," derivatives are about "moving." 

## 1. The Instantaneous Rate of Change

We define the derivative $f'(x)$ as the limit of the average rate of change as the interval goes to zero:
$$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} $$

Instead of jumping to rules, let's calculate the slope of $f(x) = x^2$ at $x=1$ by making $h$ smaller and smaller. This is exactly what your computer does when it performs **Numerical Differentiation**.

## 2. Linear Approximation

Around a point $a$, we can approximate a complex function $f(x)$ with a simple line defined by the derivative:
$$ f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) $$

This is why derivatives are so powerful: they turn complex, curvy problems into simple, linear ones.

<CodeRunner 
  client:load 
  code={`import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def f(x): return np.sin(x)
def df(x): return np.cos(x) # Derivative of sin is cos

a = 1.0 # Point of approximation
x_range = np.linspace(0, 2, 100)

# Linear approx: L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)
linear_approx = f(a) + df(a) * (x_range - a)

plt.plot(x_range, f(x_range), label='True sin(x)', linewidth=2)
plt.plot(x_range, linear_approx, '--', label='Linear Approx', color='r')
plt.scatter([a], [f(a)], color='black', zorder=5)
plt.title("Linear Approximation (Tangent Line) at x=1")
plt.legend()
plt.show()`} 
/>

## 3. The Rules of Calculus

To avoid numerical limits, we use rules. The **Chain Rule** is particularly vital for modern AI, as it powers backpropagation.
$$ \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) $$

## 4. Optimization: Finding Extremes

If a smooth function reaches a peak or valley, its slope must be **zero**. These are **Critical Points**.

  client:load
