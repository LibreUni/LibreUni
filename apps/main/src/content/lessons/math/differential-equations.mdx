# Differential Equations

In basic calculus, we learn how to find the derivative of a function. In the real world, we often encounter the reverse problem: we know how a system **changes**, and we want to find the function that describes the system's **state**. These are **Differential Equations (DEs)**.

A DE is an equation that relates a function $y$ to its derivatives. For example:
$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = ky $$
This says that the rate of change of $y$ is proportional to its current value. This is the model for population growth, radioactive decay, and continuously compounded interest.

## Separation of Variables

The simplest way to solve a first-order DE is to "separate" the variables so that all $y$'s are on one side and all $x$'s are on the other.
Example: Solve $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy$.
1. $\frac{1}{y} dy = x dx$
2. $\int \frac{1}{y} dy = \int x dx$
3. $\ln|y| = \frac{1}{2}x^2 + C$
4. $y = Ae^{x^2/2}$ (where $A = e^C$)

## Modeling: Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of change of the temperature $T$ of an object is proportional to the difference between its temperature and the ambient temperature $T_a$:
$$ \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a) $$

This equation tells us that a hot cup of coffee cools down quickly at first (when the difference is large) and then slows down as it approaches room temperature.

## Linear Systems and Matrices

When we have multiple interdependent variables (like a predator and its prey, or a set of connected water tanks), we use **Systems of Differential Equations**.
$$ \mathbf{x}'(t) = A\mathbf{x}(t) $$
The solution to this system is deeply connected to the **Eigenvalues** and **Eigenvectors** of the matrix $A$. In fact, the general solution is based on the matrix exponential $e^{At}$.
