Differential forms provide a unified framework for the study of integration, Stokes' theorem, and the geometry of manifolds.

## Exterior Algebra and Wedge Products

At each point of a manifold, a **differential $k$-form** is a purely antisymmetric $(0, k)$-tensor. The **wedge product** $\wedge$ allows us to combine forms:
$$\omega \wedge \eta = (-1)^{p q} \eta \wedge \omega$$
where $\omega$ is a $p$-form and $\eta$ is a $q$-form.

## The Exterior Derivative

The exterior derivative $d$ is a operator that transforms a $k$-form into a $(k+1)$-form. It generalizes the concepts of gradient, curl, and divergence from vector calculus.
- If $f$ is a 0-form (function), $df$ is its gradient.
- $d(d\omega) = 0$ for any form $\omega$.

## Generalized Stokes' Theorem

The fundamental theorem of calculus, Green's theorem, and the divergence theorem are all special cases of the **generalized Stokes' theorem**:
$$\int_{\Omega} d\omega = \int_{\partial \Omega} \omega$$
where $\partial \Omega$ is the boundary of $\Omega$.

## Cohomology

The study of closed forms ($d\omega = 0$) that are not exact ($\omega \neq d\eta$) leads to **De Rham Cohomology**, which reveals topological information about the underlying space (e.g., the presence of holes).
