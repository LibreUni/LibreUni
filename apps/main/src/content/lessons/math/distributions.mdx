# Distributions and Generalized Functions

In many physical applications, we encounter objects like the Dirac delta function that are not "functions" in the classical sense. Distribution theory provides a rigorous mathematical framework for these entities.

## The Problem with Classical Functions

A classical function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ has a value at every point. However, an idealized point mass or a sudden impulse has a "value" of infinity at a point and zero elsewhere, yet its integral is finite.

## Distributions as Linear Functionals

A **distribution** $T$ is a continuous linear functional on the space of **test functions** $D$ (smooth functions with compact support). We denote the action of a distribution $T$ on a test function $\phi$ as $\langle T, \phi \rangle$.

## The Dirac Delta Distribution

The Dirac delta $\delta$ is defined by its effect under an integral (or pairing):
$$\langle \delta, \phi \rangle = \phi(0)$$
For any test function $\phi$.

## Weak Derivatives

Distribution theory allows us to define the derivative of any locally integrable function. If $f$ is a distribution, its **weak derivative** $f'$ is defined by:
$$\langle f', \phi \rangle = -\langle f, \phi' \rangle$$
This formula is inspired by the integration by parts formula where boundary terms vanish for test functions.

## Sobolev Spaces

Sobolev spaces are spaces of functions whose weak derivatives exist and belong to $L^p$ spaces. They are essential in the study of partial differential equations.
