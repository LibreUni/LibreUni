# Galois Theory and Solvability by Radicals

Why is there a simple formula to solve quadratic equations ($x^2 + bx + c = 0$), and yet no universal formula using radicals exists for equations of degree 5 or higher? 

To understand this, we must examine the history of algebra and a tragic story of a young mathematician who solved a centuries-old riddle.

---

## The Quest for the Quintic

In the Renaissance, Italian mathematicians engaged in public duels to solve algebraic equations. 
- In **1515**, **Scipione del Ferro** discovered a method to solve "depressed cubics" ($x^3 + cx = d$), which was independently rediscovered by **Niccolò Fontana "Tartaglia"** in **1535**.
- In **1545**, **Gerolamo Cardano** published these methods in his landmark book *Ars Magna*, alongside his student **Lodovico Ferrari's** solution to the general quartic (degree 4) equation.
- For the next 280 years, mathematicians attempted to find a "quintic formula" (degree 5). 
- In **1824**, the Norwegian genius **Niels Henrik Abel** published the first complete proof that a general formula for the quintic was impossible.
- In **1832**, the French revolutionary **Évariste Galois** (writing his ideas down the night before he was killed in a political duel at age 20) completed the picture. He showed that the solvability of an equation is dictated by the symmetry group of its roots.

---

## Field Symmetries

Let $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$. How can we map this field to itself without altering the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$? An **automorphism** of $L$ is a bijective ring homomorphism $\sigma: L \to L$. 

Since $\sigma$ must preserve arithmetic:
$$\sigma(\sqrt{2})^2 = \sigma(\sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{2}) = \sigma(\sqrt{2}) \cdot \sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \sigma(2) = 2$$

So $\sigma(\sqrt{2})$ must be a number in $L$ whose square is $2$. This leaves only two possibilities:
$$\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \sqrt{2} \quad \text{or} \quad \sigma(\sqrt{2}) = -\sqrt{2}$$

These two actions form a group under composition: the identity $\sigma_1$, and the conjugation automorphism $\sigma_2$ (which maps $a + b\sqrt{2} \to a - b\sqrt{2}$). This is the **Galois group** of the extension.

## Galois Groups and Galois Extensions

An extension $L/K$ is a **Galois extension** if it is both normal (a splitting field) and separable.
- The **Galois Group** of a Galois extension $L/K$ (denoted $\text{Gal}(L/K)$) is the group of all field automorphisms of $L$ that fix $K$ pointwise:
  $$\text{Gal}(L/K) = \{ \sigma: L \to L \mid \sigma \text{ is an automorphism and } \sigma(k) = k \text{ for all } k \in K \}$$
- **Theorem:** If $L/K$ is a finite Galois extension, the order of the Galois group equals the degree of the extension:
  $$|\text{Gal}(L/K)| = [L : K]$$

---

## The Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

The core result of Galois theory is the **Galois Correspondence**, which maps the structure of a field extension to the structure of a group:

> **Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory):** Let $L/K$ be a finite Galois extension with Galois group $G = \text{Gal}(L/K)$. There is a bijection between:
> 1. The intermediate fields $E$ (where $K \subseteq E \subseteq L$).
> 2. The subgroups $H$ of $G$ (where $\{e\} \le H \le G$).
>
> This bijection is given by:
> - **Subgroup to Field:** $H \mapsto L^H = \{ x \in L \mid \sigma(x) = x \text{ for all } \sigma \in H \}$ (the fixed field of $H$).
> - **Field to Subgroup:** $E \mapsto \text{Gal}(L/E)$ (the automorphisms fixing $E$).
>
> Furthermore, this correspondence reverses inclusions: $E_1 \subseteq E_2 \iff \text{Gal}(L/E_2) \le \text{Gal}(L/E_1)$.

## Mapping the Galois Correspondence

Let's study the Galois correspondence for bi-quadratic extensions.

{/* Pedagogical step: I Do */}
### Worked Example

Let $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})$. This is the splitting field of $(x^2 - 2)(x^2 - 3)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.
- The degree of the extension is $[L:\mathbb{Q}] = 4$.
- The Galois group $G = \text{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ has order 4. Its elements are determined by where they send $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$:
  - $e$: $\sqrt{2} \to \sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{3} \to \sqrt{3}$ (Identity)
  - $\sigma$: $\sqrt{2} \to -\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{3} \to \sqrt{3}$
  - $\tau$: $\sqrt{2} \to \sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{3} \to -\sqrt{3}$
  - $\sigma\tau$: $\sqrt{2} \to -\sqrt{2}$, $\sqrt{3} \to -\sqrt{3}$
- This group is isomorphic to the Klein four-group $V_4 = \{e, \sigma, \tau, \sigma\tau\}$.
- Let's find the fixed field of the subgroup $H_1 = \{e, \sigma\}$:
  - An element $x = a + b\sqrt{2} + c\sqrt{3} + d\sqrt{6} \in L$ is fixed by $\sigma$ if $\sigma(x) = x$:
    $$a - b\sqrt{2} + c\sqrt{3} - d\sqrt{6} = a + b\sqrt{2} + c\sqrt{3} + d\sqrt{6} \implies b = 0, d = 0$$
  - So the fixed field is $\{ a + c\sqrt{3} \} = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3})$.

{/* Pedagogical step: We Do */}
### Subgroup Correspondence

Now let's find the fixed field of the subgroup $H_2 = \{e, \sigma\tau\}$ together.
- The automorphism $\sigma\tau$ maps $\sqrt{2} \to -\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3} \to -\sqrt{3}$.
- What does it do to $\sqrt{6} = \sqrt{2}\sqrt{3}$?
  $$\sigma\tau(\sqrt{6}) = \sigma\tau(\sqrt{2})\sigma\tau(\sqrt{3}) = (-\sqrt{2})(-\sqrt{3}) = \sqrt{6}$$
- So $\sqrt{6}$ is left fixed by $\sigma\tau$.
- Let's check which elements of the form $x = a + b\sqrt{2} + c\sqrt{3} + d\sqrt{6}$ are fixed:
  $$\sigma\tau(x) = a - b\sqrt{2} - c\sqrt{3} + d\sqrt{6} = a + b\sqrt{2} + c\sqrt{3} + d\sqrt{6}$$
  This forces $b = 0$ and $c = 0$.

## Solvability by Radicals and the Abel-Ruffini Theorem

Galois proved that a polynomial equation is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group is a **solvable group** (meaning it can be broken down into Abelian steps).

For a general polynomial of degree $n$, the Galois group of its splitting field is the symmetric group $S_n$.
- For $n \le 4$, the symmetric groups $S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4$ are solvable.
- For $n \ge 5$, $S_n$ is **not solvable** because its only non-trivial normal subgroup is $A_n$, which is simple and non-Abelian.
- This explains why the general quintic polynomial $x^5 - 4x + 2$ cannot be solved by radicals.

## Boundaries and Non-Galois Extensions

- **Solvable Group:** $S_3$ is solvable (normal subgroup chain $\{e\} \trianglelefteq A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3$ where $|S_3/A_3| = 2$ and $|A_3| = 3$ are cyclic and hence Abelian).
- **Simple Non-Abelian Group:** $A_5$ (order 60) is the smallest simple non-Abelian group. Because it has no non-trivial normal subgroups, it cannot be broken down further.
- **Edge Case (Trivial Galois Group):** For the extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$, the Galois group is trivial ($\{e\}$) because the extension is not normal. The Galois correspondence only works for Galois extensions!
