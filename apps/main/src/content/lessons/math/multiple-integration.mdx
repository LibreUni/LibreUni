In 1D calculus, we integrate over a line to find an area. In **Multivariable Calculus**, we integrate over a 2D region (Double Integral) to find a volume, or over a 3D region (Triple Integral) to find a total mass or charge.

## Double Integrals: Volumes under Surfaces

The integral $\iint_R f(x, y) dA$ represents the volume between the $xy$-plane and the surface $z = f(x, y)$ over the region $R$.

**Fubini's Theorem**: If the function is nice, you can calculate the double integral by doing two "nested" single integrals in any order:
$$\int_a^b \int_c^d f(x, y) dy \, dx = \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x, y) dx \, dy$$

## The Jacobian: Scaling Space

When we change variables (e.g., from Cartesian $(x, y)$ to Polar $(r, \theta)$), the "infinitesimal area" $dA$ changes. We use the **Jacobian** to account for this stretching.
- **Cartesian**: $dA = dx \, dy$
- **Polar**: $dA = r \, dr \, d\theta$

If you forget the $r$ in polar coordinates, your areas and volumes will be wrong!

## Triple Integrals and Density

If $\rho(x, y, z)$ represents the density of an object at point $(x, y, z)$, the total mass of the object is the triple integral of the density over its volume $V$:

$$M = \iiint_V \rho(x, y, z) dV$$
