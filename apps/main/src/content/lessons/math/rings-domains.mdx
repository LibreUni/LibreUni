# Rings and Integral Domains

While group theory analyzes systems with a single binary operation, many of our most familiar mathematical structures—like integers, real numbers, and matrices—possess two operations: addition and multiplication. 

When we have two operations, how do they interact? And why does the simple act of division fail in some algebraic systems while working perfectly in others? To study this, we introduce **rings**.

## The Motivating Problem: Cancellation in Algebra

In school algebra, if we have an equation:
$$a \cdot b = a \cdot c$$
we often cancel the $a$ from both sides to conclude that $b = c$. We justify this by dividing both sides by $a$ (assuming $a \neq 0$).

But does this always work? Let's look at arithmetic modulo 6. Let $a = 2$, $b = 3$, and $c = 0$.
- Under multiplication modulo 6:
  $$a \cdot b = 2 \cdot 3 \pmod 6 = 0$$
  $$a \cdot c = 2 \cdot 0 \pmod 6 = 0$$
So $2 \cdot 3 = 2 \cdot 0$. But we cannot cancel the $2$ to conclude that $3 = 0$! 

Why does cancellation fail here, and under what conditions can we guarantee it works?

### Try It Yourself: Finding Zero-Divisors

In modulo 6 arithmetic, the non-zero elements $2$ and $3$ multiply together to give $0$. We call such elements **zero-divisors**.

## Formalization: Definitions of Rings and Domains

A **ring** is a set $R$ equipped with two binary operations, addition ($+$) and multiplication ($\cdot$), satisfying the following axioms:

1. **$(R, +)$ is an Abelian group:**
   - Addition is associative and commutative.
   - There exists an additive identity, denoted $0$.
   - Every element $a \in R$ has an additive inverse $-a \in R$.
2. **$(R, \cdot)$ is a Monoid:**
   - Multiplication is associative.
   - There exists a multiplicative identity, denoted $1$ (with $1 \neq 0$).
3. **Distributivity:** Multiplication distributes over addition from both sides:
   $$a \cdot (b + c) = (a \cdot b) + (a \cdot c) \quad \text{and} \quad (b + c) \cdot a = (b \cdot a) + (c \cdot a)$$

If multiplication is also commutative ($a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ for all $a, b \in R$), we call it a **commutative ring**.

### Units and Zero-Divisors

- An element $u \in R$ is a **unit** (or invertible element) if there exists some $v \in R$ such that $u \cdot v = v \cdot u = 1$. The set of all units in $R$ forms a group under multiplication, denoted $R^\times$.
- A non-zero element $a \in R$ is a **zero-divisor** if there exists a non-zero element $b \in R$ such that $a \cdot b = 0$ or $b \cdot a = 0$.

### Integral Domains and Fields

- An **integral domain** is a commutative ring that has no zero-divisors. In an integral domain, the cancellation law holds: if $a \cdot b = a \cdot c$ and $a \neq 0$, then $b = c$.
- A **field** is a commutative ring in which every non-zero element is a unit (meaning we can divide by any non-zero element). 
  *Corollary:* Every field is an integral domain, but the converse is not true (e.g., $\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain but not a field).

## Characteristic of a Ring

The **characteristic** of a ring $R$ (denoted $\text{char}(R)$) is the smallest positive integer $n$ such that:
$$n \cdot 1 = \underbrace{1 + 1 + \dots + 1}_{n \text{ times}} = 0$$
If no such positive integer exists, the ring has characteristic $0$.
- **Theorem:** If $R$ is an integral domain, then $\text{char}(R)$ is either $0$ or a prime number $p$.

## Worked Example: Units and Zero-Divisors Modulo $N$

In the ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ of integers modulo $n$:
- An element $a$ is a unit if and only if $\gcd(a, n) = 1$.
- An element $a \neq 0$ is a zero-divisor if and only if $\gcd(a, n) > 1$.

Let's verify this using Python for $\mathbb{Z}/10\mathbb{Z}$.

## Contrast: Ring, Domain, and Field

- **$\mathbb{Z}$ (Integers):** A commutative ring and an integral domain, but not a field. The only units are $1$ and $-1$.
- **$\mathbb{Q}$ (Rationals):** A field (and thus also an integral domain). Every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.
- **$\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ (Integers mod 6):** A commutative ring, but **not** an integral domain (due to zero-divisors $\{2, 3, 4\}$) and not a field.
