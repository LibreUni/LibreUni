# Splitting Fields and Separability

When we adjoin a root of a polynomial to a field, we do not always obtain all of its other roots. For example, if we adjoin the real root $\sqrt[3]{2}$ to the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$, we get the field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$. But the polynomial $x^3 - 2$ has two other roots in the complex numbers:
$$\sqrt[3]{2}\omega \quad \text{and} \quad \sqrt[3]{2}\omega^2 \quad \text{where} \quad \omega = e^{2\pi i / 3} = \frac{-1 + i\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

These roots are complex, so they do not lie in the real field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$. To perform complete algebraic calculations, we need a field that contains *all* the roots of our polynomial. Such a field is called a **splitting field**.

## The Motivating Problem: Splitting $x^3 - 2$

To find a field where $x^3 - 2$ splits completely into linear factors, we must adjoin both the real root $\sqrt[3]{2}$ and the complex cube root of unity $\omega$. This gives the field:
$$L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$$

What is the degree of this extension over $\mathbb{Q}$? We can compute this using the Tower Rule by building the extensions step-by-step:
1. First adjoin $\sqrt[3]{2}$: the degree is $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 3$ (since the minimal polynomial $x^3 - 2$ is irreducible and has degree 3).
2. Now adjoin $\omega$ to $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$: $\omega$ is a root of the quadratic polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$. Since $\omega$ is complex, it does not lie in the real field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$. Thus, its minimal polynomial over this intermediate field is still $x^2 + x + 1$, which has degree 2. 
   So $[L : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})] = 2$.
3. By the Tower Rule:
   $$[L : \mathbb{Q}] = [L : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 3 = 6$$

This splitting field is a 6-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$.

## Formalization: Splitting Fields and Normal Extensions

Let $K$ be a field and $f(x) \in K[x]$. An extension field $L/K$ is a **splitting field** for $f(x)$ over $K$ if:
1. $f(x)$ factors into linear factors in $L[x]$:
   $$f(x) = c(x - \alpha_1)(x - \alpha_2)\dots(x - \alpha_n) \quad \text{for some } \alpha_i \in L, c \in K$$
2. $L$ is generated over $K$ by the roots: $L = K(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dots, \alpha_n)$.

> **Theorem (Uniqueness):** Any two splitting fields for a polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$ are isomorphic over $K$.

An extension $L/K$ is called a **normal extension** if it is the splitting field of some family of polynomials in $K[x]$. Geometrically, if a normal extension contains one root of an irreducible polynomial in $K[x]$, it must contain *all* of its roots.

## Separability and Multiple Roots

A polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$ is **separable** if it has no multiple roots in its splitting field. 
- Over fields of characteristic 0 (like $\mathbb{Q}$ or $\mathbb{R}$) or finite fields, every irreducible polynomial is separable. Fields with this property are called **perfect fields**.
- Inseparability only occurs in infinite fields of prime characteristic $p$ (e.g., $x^p - t$ over the rational function field $\mathbb{F}_p(t)$).

### Testing for Multiple Roots

A polynomial $f(x)$ has a multiple root in its splitting field if and only if $f(x)$ and its formal derivative $f'(x)$ share a common factor of degree $\ge 1$. That is:
$$\gcd(f(x), f'(x)) \neq 1$$

## Contrast: Separable vs Inseparable Extension

- **Separable Extension:** The extension $L/K$ is separable if the minimal polynomial of every element in $L$ is separable. Over $\mathbb{Q}$, **every algebraic extension is separable** because $\mathbb{Q}$ has characteristic 0 (making it a perfect field).
- **Inseparable Extension:** Only exists in characteristic $p$. In the extension $\mathbb{F}_p(t^{1/p}) / \mathbb{F}_p(t)$, the minimal polynomial of $t^{1/p}$ is $x^p - t = (x - t^{1/p})^p$. Since the root $t^{1/p}$ has multiplicity $p$, the polynomial is inseparable.
