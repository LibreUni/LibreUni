## The Birth of Rational Inquiry

Before the 6th century BC, the ancient Greeks relied on mythology to explain the world. Natural phenomena like storms, seasons, and disease were attributed to the direct actions of the gods. The "Pre-Socratic" philosophers (thinkers who lived before Socrates) sparked a massive intellectual revolution by abandoning mythological explanations in favor of rational, naturalistic observation. 

{/* Source tracking: Synthesized from Wikipedia article on Pre-Socratic philosophy: the shift from myth to rational explanation. */}

This period marks the true beginning of Western philosophy and science. These early thinkers were primarily concerned with **cosmology** (the study of the universe's origin and structure) and the search for the **arche**—the single, fundamental substance or principle from which everything in the universe is derived.

## Major Pre-Socratic Thinkers

{/* Source tracking: Synthesized from Wikipedia article on Pre-Socratic philosophy: notable schools and thinkers. */}

1.  **The Milesians:** The very first philosophers came from the city of Miletus. Thales famously proposed that the fundamental substance (*arche*) of all things was water. His successor Anaximander argued it was the *apeiron* (the infinite or boundless), while Anaximenes believed it was air, proposing that differing densities of air created different materials.
2.  **Heraclitus and Parmenides:** These two philosophers engaged in a profound debate over the nature of change. Heraclitus argued that change is the fundamental essence of the universe—famously stating that you cannot step into the same river twice, as everything "flows." Parmenides, using strict logical deduction, argued the exact opposite: that "Being" is one, eternal, and unchanging, and therefore all change we see is merely a sensory illusion.
3.  **The Atomists:** Thinkers like Leucippus and Democritus attempted to reconcile permanence and change by proposing that the universe consists entirely of tiny, indivisible, unchanging particles called "atoms" moving aimlessly through an infinite void.

## Analytical Worked Example

A strong philosophical analysis does not merely name a doctrine; it tests what the doctrine can explain, what it must assume, and where it becomes costly. Consider a student evaluating **The Pre-Socratics** through an ordinary disagreement. One person claims the position is attractive because it gives a clear standard for judgment. Another objects that the same standard may oversimplify human experience. The useful response is not to choose a side immediately, but to reconstruct the argument in stages: define the central claim, identify the reasons offered for it, state the strongest objection, and then decide whether the theory can answer that objection without changing into a different theory.

{/* Source tracking: Philosophical method in this worked example is synthesized from OpenStax Introduction to Philosophy on argument analysis, critical reasoning, and the thematic organization of philosophy; wording and scenario are original. */}

For this topic, that means asking three questions. First, what is the theory treating as basic: reality, knowledge, duty, happiness, virtue, language, power, experience, or social order? Second, what does it count as evidence: logical consistency, perception, historical explanation, practical success, reflective equilibrium, or lived experience? Third, what would falsify or seriously weaken the view? A doctrine that cannot name its own pressure points is not yet a philosophical position; it is only a slogan.

A good revision also distinguishes exposition from evaluation. Exposition asks what the view says and why its defenders found it compelling. Evaluation asks whether the view survives comparison with rival accounts. Keeping those tasks separate prevents two common errors: rejecting a theory before understanding its internal logic, and summarizing a theory so sympathetically that no critical judgment remains.
