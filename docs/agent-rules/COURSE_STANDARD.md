# LibreUni Course Standard (“the bible”)

This document is mandatory reading for every agent or contributor who creates, reviews, researches, repairs, restructures, or otherwise modifies a LibreUni course, module, lesson, course component, manifest, or course assessment. It applies regardless of host harness. `AGENTS.md` routes course work here before the task-specific rules.

## Standard of work

Write to the standard of a demanding upper-division course at MIT, Stanford, Cambridge, or ETH Zürich. Courses are public scholarly work and will be reviewed by professors who teach the subject. Treat superficial coverage, unjustified claims, missing prerequisites, and fake exercises as publication-blocking defects.

The course must be competitive with the strongest openly available university courses on its subject. Reconstruct its expected content before design or major revision from 3–5 respected university syllabi, 2–3 canonical textbooks, and relevant professional or curricular standards where applicable. Record the research in a curriculum crosswalk containing:

- universally expected material;
- institution-dependent material;
- advanced extensions; and
- deliberate LibreUni omissions.

The target is durable conceptual mastery, not apparent completeness: mathematical maturity includes proofs and abstraction; programming maturity includes independent implementation of nontrivial systems; the assessment ceiling includes demanding final-exam problems; and the expected study time is 150–200 hours. This is not a bootcamp, reference sheet, interview-cram guide, or overview.

Do not maximize lesson count, heading count, word count, topic coverage, component count, or repository metrics. A clean mechanical audit is necessary but never establishes educational quality.

## Non-negotiable learning contract

Every stated learning outcome must be demonstrated by an assessment that cannot be passed through keyword recognition or reading comprehension alone. A learner completing the course must be able to solve unseen problems, explain why methods work, compare alternatives, recognize failure conditions, reconstruct forgotten formulas from internal structure, and read primary or advanced secondary literature in the area. Require transfer to unfamiliar cases.

Every acceptable lesson contains, at the appropriate scale:

1. a motivating problem that makes the concept necessary;
2. precise definitions, assumptions, and boundary cases;
3. a central explanation, derivation, proof, or justified mechanism;
4. at least two substantially different worked examples;
5. a counterexample, failure mode, or limitation;
6. connections to prerequisites and subsequent concepts;
7. exercises at recall, application, transfer, and synthesis levels;
8. complete solutions or usable grading criteria;
9. sources supporting material claims; and
10. an explicit account of what was deliberately omitted.

For each major concept, check that the draft answers:

- Why does it exist?
- What is its formal or precise statement?
- What mechanism makes it work?
- How is it derived or justified?
- What is a canonical example?
- What is a non-example or counterexample?
- What are its boundary conditions?
- What misconception is most likely?
- How does it relate to adjacent concepts?
- What problem requires transfer rather than recall?

These are quality obligations, not a demand for repetitive headings. Combine them naturally when that preserves reasoning; do not omit them merely to make a lesson shorter.

## Scope discipline and intellectual honesty

Every topic must earn its place because it is prerequisite knowledge for a stated outcome, resolves an important conceptual difficulty, enables a meaningful class of problems, or is necessary for disciplinary literacy. If the available scope cannot teach a topic adequately, allocate sufficient space, explicitly defer it, or remove it. A named-but-untaught topic is negative quality.

Do not simulate rigor with terminology, citations, equations, long enumerations, or advanced names. Do not replace explanation with lists, reduce difficult material to a short summary merely to mark it covered, or create exercises copied directly from preceding prose. Do not declare a lesson complete because its headings exist, and do not quietly lower the intended level when the work becomes large.

Before finishing, perform an adversarial self-review: identify the most intellectually dishonest shortcuts in the draft—hidden assumptions, skipped derivations, decorative terminology, unsupported claims, shallow examples, copied-answer exercises, and unacknowledged omissions—and repair them or disclose them. If a claim cannot be supported or taught at the intended level, remove it or defer it explicitly.

## Evidence and handoff

Course work must leave enough evidence for another reviewer to verify the design: stated outcomes and prerequisites, the curriculum crosswalk for course-scale work, source-backed content, meaningful assessment alignment, deliberate omissions, and an authoring-surface audit. Report mechanical checks separately from pedagogical judgments. Never claim a check passed unless it was run.
