# Approximation Review

For an intractable optimization model, compare an exact solver on small instances, a provable approximation, and a heuristic. State feasibility, quality guarantee, runtime, and the kinds of instances on which the guarantee says nothing.

## What an approximation guarantee says

For a minimization problem, an algorithm is an alpha-approximation if it always returns a feasible solution with cost at most alpha times the optimum, where alpha is at least one. For maximization, the usual statement is that the returned value is at least OPT divided by alpha, or equivalently at least a specified fraction of OPT. Always state the convention; “within two” is ambiguous.

The guarantee has three parts: feasibility, quality, and computational cost. A solution that violates a constraint is not a poor approximation; it is not a solution to the stated problem. A ratio applies to every legal instance, including pathological ones, unless the theorem explicitly assumes metric distances, bounded weights, a special graph class, or another restriction. The ratio may be loose on typical data and still be valuable because it is auditable under worst-case conditions.

An additive guarantee has a different meaning. A schedule may have cost at most OPT plus an additive constant, which can be excellent for large instances but useless when OPT is small. A multiplicative ratio is scale-free but can be unstable when the optimum is near zero. Choose the measure that reflects the application and prove the exact statement used in the decision.

## Canonical proof patterns

The simplest approximation proofs compare the algorithm with a lower bound on OPT. In weighted vertex cover, take a maximal matching M and select both endpoints of every edge in M. The selected set covers every edge, so it is feasible. Any vertex cover must contain at least one endpoint of each matching edge, and matching edges are disjoint, so OPT is at least the size of M. The algorithm selects 2|M| vertices and is therefore a 2-approximation.

The proof is a reusable template: construct a certificate of cost L that OPT must pay for, then show the algorithm costs at most alpha L. Matching, packing, cuts, and dual solutions frequently provide such certificates. A reviewer should ask exactly where the lower bound is established and whether the objects charged to it overlap.

For metric traveling salesman, a minimum spanning tree has weight at most OPT because removing one edge from an optimal tour leaves a spanning tree. Doubling tree edges gives an Eulerian walk of weight twice the tree. Shortcutting repeated vertices does not increase length under the triangle inequality, producing a tour of weight at most 2OPT. The metric assumption is essential: without triangle inequality, shortcutting can be more expensive and the proof fails.

For set cover with n elements, the greedy algorithm repeatedly chooses the set covering the largest number of uncovered elements per unit cost. A harmonic-number analysis shows a ratio at most H(n), approximately 1 plus the natural logarithm of n. The charging proof assigns each newly covered element a marginal price and compares the total prices with the cost of an optimal set cover. This is not a constant ratio independent of n, and saying merely “greedy is good” hides the theorem’s scale.

## Relaxation and rounding

A powerful strategy formulates an integer program, drops integrality to obtain a linear-programming relaxation, solves or approximately solves the relaxation, and rounds the fractional solution. The relaxation value is a lower bound for minimization because every integer feasible solution is also feasible in the relaxed problem. The rounding analysis must show both feasibility and expected or deterministic cost.

Randomized rounding sets a variable to one with probability proportional to its fractional value. Linearity of expectation controls expected cost, while concentration bounds control constraint violations or repeated sampling. Alteration can repair an infeasible sampled solution. If the theorem says expected cost, do not silently claim that every run satisfies the ratio. To obtain high probability, state the repetition, alteration, and failure analysis.

The integrality gap measures how far the relaxation can be from the integer optimum. A gap example is not merely a technical curiosity: it tells us that no rounding method based only on that relaxation can beat the gap in general. Strengthening the relaxation with valid inequalities can improve the gap, but may increase solver cost. Approximation design is often a trade between a lower bound that is easy to compute and a relaxation strong enough to guide rounding.

## Local search and exchange arguments

Local search starts with a feasible solution and repeatedly applies an improving neighborhood move. Termination is immediate if the objective is integral and each move improves it, but termination alone says nothing about quality. The proof examines a locally optimal solution and an optimum, then maps optimal objects to local objects so that the local cost is bounded.

The neighborhood size is part of the theorem. A 1-swap local optimum may have a weak ratio while a k-swap neighborhood supports a stronger result at greater running time. If approximate local improvement is used, account for tolerance and the possibility of cycling. A practical implementation needs a stopping rule, tie handling, and a way to avoid repeatedly scanning unchanged neighborhoods.

## Hardness, lower bounds, and limits

An approximation algorithm is meaningful only relative to what is known to be possible. A reduction can show that improving a ratio beyond a threshold would imply an unlikely complexity collapse. Such a statement is conditional, so name the assumption. A polynomial-time heuristic with no guarantee may outperform a 2-approximation on a data set, while a 2-approximation remains the only method with a universal certificate.

Approximation schemes deserve careful distinction. A PTAS gives, for every fixed epsilon greater than zero, a solution within 1+epsilon of optimum in time polynomial in input size, but the exponent or hidden dependence may grow with 1/epsilon. An FPTAS is polynomial in both input size and 1/epsilon. A scheme that is practical for epsilon=.1 may be unusable for epsilon=.001; complexity notation exposes that trade.

## How to compare methods experimentally

Use exact optimization only where it can finish or provide a certificate on small instances. For larger instances, compare the heuristic or approximation value with lower bounds from relaxations, matchings, spanning trees, or dual feasible solutions. The gap between an upper bound U and lower bound L gives a certified interval for OPT. If U/L is close to one, an apparently heuristic answer may already be nearly certified.

Report feasibility violations separately from objective value. Include instance families, size, random seed, time limit, preprocessing, solver version, and whether a timeout returns the best incumbent. A timeout is not an exact result. Compare wall time and memory as well as ratio, because a mathematically stronger method may be inappropriate under a strict service budget.

## Exercises

1. Prove the maximal-matching 2-approximation for vertex cover and identify every use of maximality.
2. Give a metric counterexample showing why MST shortcutting needs triangle inequality.
3. State a valid approximation theorem for a randomized rounding algorithm, including its probability qualifier.
4. Construct a relaxation lower bound and an integer feasible upper bound for a small covering instance.
5. Explain the difference between PTAS and FPTAS using the dependence on epsilon.
6. Design a local-search neighborhood and name the invariant needed for termination.
7. A method returns `U=120` while a dual certificate gives `L=100`. What can be certified about OPT and the method’s ratio relative to OPT?

### Diagnostic solutions

1. A maximal matching has no edge with both endpoints outside its endpoints, so selecting both endpoints covers every edge. Every cover contains an endpoint of each disjoint matching edge, giving OPT at least |M|; the selected cover has 2|M| vertices.
2. Let a tree contain two vertices joined by a cheap path while the direct shortcut edge is arbitrarily expensive. The Euler walk can use the path, but replacing a repeated-vertex detour by the direct edge is not safe without metric inequality.
3. One acceptable form is: the algorithm is feasible with probability at least 1-delta and has expected cost at most alpha OPT, or, if separately proved, cost at most alpha OPT with probability at least 1-delta. The two claims are not interchangeable.
4. Any feasible dual solution is a lower bound L by weak duality; any integral selected collection gives an upper bound U. Hence `L  at most  OPT  at most  U`, and `U/L` certifies the returned solution relative to optimum.
5. A PTAS is polynomial for each fixed epsilon but may be exponential or otherwise severe in 1/epsilon; an FPTAS is polynomial in both n and 1/epsilon.
6. Choose a finite neighborhood and a strictly improving integer potential, or use a lexicographic potential with a tolerance rule. Then prove no infinite sequence of accepted moves.
7. OPT lies in [100,120]. The returned solution has ratio at most 120/100=1.2 relative to OPT’s lower bound, and its exact ratio is at most 120/OPT; if the returned solution is feasible, it is certified within 20 percent using the available bound.

**Exercise.** Explain why a heuristic’s excellent average result is not a replacement for a worst-case approximation proof.

## Synthesis checkpoint

Before accepting an approximation claim, write the feasible region, objective convention, lower or upper certificate, and quantifier over instances. Then distinguish a theorem from a measured result: a strong average benchmark does not imply a worst-case ratio, and a ratio does not imply acceptable memory or latency. This checklist is useful when comparing exact solvers, schemes, and heuristics in the same report.
