# Dynamic Programming

**Prerequisites.** Proof by induction, directed acyclic graphs (DAGs), big-O notation, and the definition of a recurrence relation.

Richard Bellman at the RAND Corporation (1950s) studied *sequential decision processes*: at each stage an action is chosen, incurring a cost or reward, and transitioning to a new state. The goal is a *policy* specifying the optimal action for every possible state. The naive approach enumerates all policy sequences. For $S$ states and $A$ actions per state over $T$ stages, that is $A^{S \cdot T}$ possibilities — the **curse of dimensionality** [Bellman, 1957].

Bellman's insight: the future cost from a given state depends only on the state itself, not on the history of how you reached it — the *Principle of Optimality*. This transforms the exponential search into a polynomial-time recurrence: $V(s) = \min_a [\text{cost}(s,a) + V(\text{succ}(s,a))]$. Bellman coined the deliberately bland term "dynamic programming" — "programming" in the sense of planning (its military meaning), and "dynamic" because it sounded impressive while concealing the underlying mathematics from his Air Force reviewers [Bellman, 1957].

## Principle of Optimality

<MathStatement id="optimality-principle" kind="definition" title="Principle of optimality">
An optimal policy has the property that whatever the initial state and initial decision are, the remaining decisions must constitute an optimal policy with regard to the state resulting from the first decision.
</MathStatement>

Let $s$ be a state, $d(s)$ the set of feasible decisions, $\text{cost}(s, a)$ the immediate cost of taking action $a$ in state $s$, and $\text{succ}(s, a)$ the resulting state. Define $V(s)$ as the minimum total cost from $s$ to a terminal state. Then:

$$
V(s) = \min_{a \in d(s)} \left[ \text{cost}(s, a) + V(\text{succ}(s, a)) \right].
$$

<MathProof id="optimality-principle-proof" for="optimality-principle" title="Justification of the recurrence" collapsible>
Let $\pi$ be an optimal policy. For state $s$, let $a^* = \pi(s)$ be the optimal action and $s' = \text{succ}(s, a^*)$. The total cost is $\text{cost}(s, a^*) + V_{\pi}(s')$, where $V_{\pi}(s')$ is the cost of following $\pi$ from $s'$. If $V_{\pi}(s') > V(s')$ (the true minimum from $s'$), then there exists a policy $\pi'$ with cost $V(s')$ from $s'$. Replacing the suffix of $\pi$ with $\pi'$ yields a strictly lower total cost from $s$, contradicting the optimality of $\pi$. Therefore $V_{\pi}(s') = V(s')$, and the recurrence $V(s) = \min_{a \in d(s)} [\text{cost}(s,a) + V(\text{succ}(s,a))]$ holds.
</MathProof>

The recurrence is valid iff the cost-to-go $V$ depends only on the resulting state, not on how it was reached. This is the *Markov property* [Bellman, 1957, Ch. 1]. The principle fails when past decisions affect future feasibility beyond the state descriptor — a *non-Markovian* situation that demands state augmentation.

## Optimal Substructure

<MathStatement id="optimal-substructure" kind="theorem" title="Optimal substructure (cut-and-paste)">
If an optimal solution to problem $P$ of size $n$ makes a decision that decomposes $P$ into subproblems $P_1, \ldots, P_k$, then the solutions to $P_1,\ldots,P_k$ used within the optimal solution must themselves be optimal.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="optimal-substructure-proof" for="optimal-substructure" title="Proof by cut-and-paste" collapsible open>
Suppose an optimal solution to $P$ uses a suboptimal solution to $P_i$ with cost $C_i$. Let $C_i^* < C_i$ be the optimal cost for $P_i$. Replacing $P_i$'s solution with the optimal one reduces the total cost of $P$, contradicting optimality of the original solution, provided the replacement does not affect the feasibility of other subproblem solutions.

The "provided" clause is the critical constraint — it fails when subproblem solutions interact (e.g., scheduling with shared resources). DP therefore requires *independent subproblems* after the decision.
</MathProof>

## The State DAG Formulation

Every DP defines a directed acyclic graph (DAG) over states. Vertices are state descriptors; edges correspond to transitions with costs. Solving the DP is equivalent to computing shortest (or longest) paths in this DAG.

<MathStatement id="dag-dp" kind="theorem" title="DP as DAG shortest path">
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a DAG with edge weights $w(u,v)$. Define $\text{dist}(v)$ as the minimum-weight path from source to $v$. Then:

$$
\text{dist}(v) = \min_{(u,v) \in E} \left[ \text{dist}(u) + w(u,v) \right],
$$

and $\text{dist}$ can be computed in $O(V+E)$ time by processing vertices in topological order.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="dag-dp-proof" for="dag-dp" title="Proof by induction on topological order" collapsible>
Every path to $v$ ends with an edge $(u,v)$, where $u$ appears earlier in topological order (since the graph is acyclic). By induction on the topological order, $\text{dist}(u)$ is finalized when $u$ is processed. Considering all incoming edges yields the minimum over all paths.
</MathProof>

This perspective unifies all DP algorithms [Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani, 2006, Ch. 6]: the choice of state determines the vertex set; the transition function determines edges; the base case is the source; the queries ask for distances to target vertices. If the state graph has cycles, the recurrence becomes a system of equations solvable by value iteration or Bellman–Ford.

## When Optimal Substructure Fails

Not every natural-looking recurrence satisfies the cut-and-paste condition. Three common failure modes:

**Failure mode 1: Interacting subproblems.** The optimal solution to a subproblem may depend on which other subproblems were solved, violating the independence assumption.

> **Example: Shortest path with vertex colors.** Find the shortest path from $s$ to $t$ that visits at most 2 red vertices. A natural state is (vertex, reds_used). But two paths reaching the same vertex with equal reds_used may have visited *different* red vertices, leading to different future feasibility. The cut-and-paste fails because the suffix solution may need a specific red vertex that was already used. Fix: augment the state with the set of red vertices visited, increasing the state space exponentially.

**Failure mode 2: Non-Markovian cost structure.** The cost of a transition depends on the entire history, not just the current state.

> **Example: Job scheduling with setup times.** Each job has a setup cost that depends on the *previous* job (traveling salesman-like). If the state is just the set of completed jobs, the optimal cost to reach a state depends on which job was last completed — the state is insufficient. Fix: augment state with the last completed job, yielding the Held–Karp-like DP.

**Failure mode 3: Non-local constraints.** The global constraint cannot be expressed as a per-transition cost bound.

> **Example: Knapsack with conflicts.** Items belong to groups; at most one item per group may be selected. The standard DP state (item_index, remaining_capacity) is insufficient because the optimal solution must also track which groups have been used. The constraint couples the choices across items that are not adjacent in the ordering. Fix: reorder items so group members are contiguous, or augment state with a bitmask of used groups.

## Canonical DP: Edit Distance

<MathStatement id="edit-distance" kind="definition" title="Edit distance (Levenshtein)">
Given strings $X[1..m]$, $Y[1..n]$, the minimum number of insertions, deletions, and substitutions to convert $X$ to $Y$ is:

$$
D[i][j] = \begin{cases}
j & \text{if } i = 0 \\
i & \text{if } j = 0 \\
D[i-1][j-1] & \text{if } X[i] = Y[j] \\
1 + \min\{D[i-1][j], D[i][j-1], D[i-1][j-1]\} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

where $D[i][j]$ is the edit distance between prefixes $X[1..i]$ and $Y[1..j]$.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="edit-distance-proof" for="edit-distance" title="Proof of recurrence" collapsible open>
Consider the last operation performed on $X[1..i]$. If it is a deletion of $X[i]$, the remaining cost is $D[i-1][j]$. If it is an insertion of $Y[j]$, the remaining cost is $D[i][j-1]$. If it is a substitution of $X[i]$ with $Y[j]$, the remaining cost is $D[i-1][j-1]$, plus 1 if the characters differ. If $X[i] = Y[j]$, no operation on the final character is needed.

The three cases are exhaustive and mutually exclusive for an optimal sequence: any optimal edit sequence can be rearranged so that operations on the final characters occur last. **Complexity:** $O(mn)$ time, $O(mn)$ space, compressible to $O(\min(m,n))$ space [CLRS, 4th ed., Ch. 14].
</MathProof>

## Matrix Chain Multiplication

**Problem.** Given matrices $A_1 \times A_2 \times \cdots \times A_n$ with dimensions $d_0 \times d_1, d_1 \times d_2, \ldots, d_{n-1} \times d_n$, find the parenthesization minimizing scalar multiplications.

**State.** $C[i][j]$ = minimum cost to multiply $A_i \cdots A_j$.

**Recurrence.** $C[i][i] = 0$. For $i < j$:

$$
C[i][j] = \min_{i \le k < j} \left[ C[i][k] + C[k+1][j] + d_{i-1} d_k d_j \right].
$$

**Proof of optimal substructure.** Any optimal parenthesization of $A_i \cdots A_j$ has a final multiplication at some split $k$, producing $B = A_i \cdots A_k$ and $C = A_{k+1} \cdots A_j$. The cost is $\text{cost}(B) + \text{cost}(C) + d_{i-1} d_k d_j$. By the cut-and-paste argument, $\text{cost}(B)$ must be optimal for the subproblem $A_i \cdots A_k$, and similarly for $C$. If either were suboptimal, replacing it with the optimal parenthesization would reduce the total cost.

**Complexity.** $O(n^3)$ time, $O(n^2)$ space [CLRS, 4th ed., Ch. 14].

## 0/1 Knapsack: Proof and Pseudo-Polynomiality

<MathStatement id="knapsack-dp" kind="theorem" title="0/1 knapsack optimal substructure">
Let $DP[i][w]$ be the maximum value achievable from the first $i$ items with capacity $w$. Then:

$$
DP[i][w] = \begin{cases}
DP[i-1][w] & \text{if } w_i > w \\
\max(DP[i-1][w], v_i + DP[i-1][w-w_i]) & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

with base $DP[0][w] = 0$ and $DP[i][0] = 0$.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="knapsack-proof" for="knapsack-dp" title="Proof by case analysis" collapsible open>
Let $S$ be an optimal subset from items $1..i$ with total weight $\le w$. Either $i \notin S$ or $i \in S$.

- If $i \notin S$, then $S$ is an optimal subset from items $1..(i-1)$ with capacity $w$ (by cut-and-paste).
- If $i \in S$, then $S \setminus \{i\}$ is a subset from items $1..(i-1)$ with capacity $w - w_i$, and must be optimal for that subproblem (otherwise $S$ could be improved).

The recurrence takes the maximum of these two possibilities.
</MathProof>

**Pseudo-polynomiality.** The running time $O(nW)$ is polynomial in $n$ and the numeric value $W$, but exponential in $\log W$, the bit length of the input. Knapsack is NP-hard, so no truly polynomial algorithm exists unless P = NP. The DP is therefore a *pseudo-polynomial* algorithm [Garey and Johnson, 1979], useful when $W$ is moderate.

**FPTAS for knapsack.** Round each value $v_i$ to $\lfloor v_i / K \rfloor$ where $K = \epsilon V_{\max} / n$. Solve the scaled instance in $O(n^2 / \epsilon)$ time. ## Weighted Interval Scheduling

<MathStatement id="weighted-interval" kind="definition" title="Weighted interval scheduling recurrence">
Given $n$ intervals $[s_i, f_i)$ with values $v_i$, let $OPT[j]$ be the maximum value using intervals from sorted finish times $[1..j]$. Then:

$$
OPT[j] = \max(OPT[j-1], v_j + OPT[p(j)])
$$

where $p(j)$ is the largest index $i < j$ with $f_i \le s_j$, and $OPT[0] = 0$.
</MathStatement>

**Proof.** For interval $j$, either it is excluded (giving $OPT[j-1]$) or included. If included, all intervals overlapping $j$ are excluded, and the remaining problem is $OPT[p(j)]$ by cut-and-paste [Kleinberg and Tardos, 2005, Ch. 6].

**Complexity.** $O(n \log n)$ for sorting and binary searching $p(j)$, plus $O(n)$ DP.

## DP with Bitmasks: Held–Karp for TSP

<MathStatement id="held-karp" kind="theorem" title="Held–Karp DP for TSP">
Given complete graph $G = (V, E)$ with edge weights $w(i,j)$, let $DP[S][v]$ be the minimum cost of a path starting at vertex 0, visiting exactly the vertex set $S$, and ending at $v$, where $0 \in S$.

$$
DP[S][v] = \min_{u \in S \setminus \{v\}} \left[ DP[S \setminus \{v\}][u] + w(u,v) \right],
$$

with $DP[\{0\}][0] = 0$ and $|S| > 1$. The optimal tour cost is:

$$
\min_{v \neq 0} \left[ DP[V][v] + w(v, 0) \right].
$$
</MathStatement>

**Complexity.** $O(n^2 2^n)$ time, $O(n 2^n)$ space. This is still exponential but far better than $(n-1)!$ brute force [Held and Karp, 1962].

## Convex Hull Trick (CHT)

For DP recurrences of the form $DP[i] = \min_{j < i} \left( DP[j] + a_i \cdot b_j \right)$ where $a_i$ is monotone and $b_j$ determines the slope, the convex hull trick reduces transition computation from $O(n)$ to $O(\log n)$ by maintaining the lower envelope of lines $y = b_j x + DP[j]$ [CLRS, 4th ed., Ch. 15].

**Example:** $DP[i] = \min_{j < i} \left[ DP[j] + (x_i - x_j)^2 \right] = x_i^2 + \min_{j < i} \left[ DP[j] + x_j^2 - 2 x_i x_j \right]$. The inner expression is a line with slope $-2x_j$ and intercept $DP[j] + x_j^2$ evaluated at $x_i$.

1. **State completeness:** Do two histories with the same state always have the same optimal future? If not, the state needs augmentation.
2. **Base cases:** Are all minimal states explicitly defined and correct?
3. **Transition validity:** Does every transition map to a strictly smaller state (in a well-founded sense)?
4. **Optimal substructure:** Can an optimal solution be decomposed into optimal solutions to subproblems? Prove by cut-and-paste.
5. **Dependency order:** Is the evaluation order a topological sorting of the state DAG?
6. **Reconstruction:** Can a witness be recovered from the table or stored decisions?

## Exercises

1. **Optimal BST.** Given frequencies $f_1, \ldots, f_n$ for keys $k_1 < \cdots < k_n$, find the BST minimizing expected search cost. Define $C[i][j] = \min_{i \le r \le j} \left[ C[i][r-1] + C[r+1][j] + \sum_{t=i}^j f_t \right]$. Prove optimal substructure and derive $O(n^3)$ time.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Try each possible root $r$ for the interval $[i,j]$.">

   For a BST with root $k_r$, the left subtree is an optimal BST for keys $k_i..k_{r-1}$ and the right for $k_{r+1}..k_j$. The sum $\sum_{t=i}^j f_t$ accounts for the depth increase of every node when attached to the root. Cut-and-paste: if the left subtree were suboptimal, replacing it with the optimal left BST reduces total search cost.
   </ExerciseSolution>

2. **Dominance pruning.** For the unbounded knapsack problem, prove that if item $a$ has $v_a \ge v_b$ and $w_a \le w_b$, then item $b$ can be discarded without affecting optimality. Construct a case where this dominance does not hold for 0/1 knapsack.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Consider replacing item $b$ with item $a$ in an optimal solution.">

   For any feasible solution using item $b$, replace each copy of $b$ with a copy of $a$: value does not decrease, weight does not increase. Feasibility is preserved. For 0/1 knapsack, replacing $b$ with $a$ may exceed the capacity if $w_a < w_b$, but the replacement is valid only if $b$ was included and $a$ was not. Dominance holds in 0/1 only if $w_a \le w_b$ and both items can be swapped without exceeding capacity; a counterexample: $v_a=10, w_a=9$, $v_b=9, w_b=5$, with capacity 10. Item $b$ is part of the optimal solution (value 9 with item $b$ alone fits), while item $a$ alone also fits but swapping $b\to a$ changes the solution.
   </ExerciseSolution>

3. **Held–Karp reconstruction.** Augment the TSP DP to reconstruct the optimal tour. Show that storing the optimal predecessor per state adds $O(n 2^n)$ space and does not change the asymptotic time.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   Maintain $next[S][v]$ = the vertex that precedes $v$ in the optimal path for state $(S,v)$. When computing $DP[S][v] = DP[S\setminus\{v\}][u] + w(u,v)$, record $next[S][v] = u$. After finding the optimal final edge, follow $next$ pointers in reverse to reconstruct the tour.
   </ExerciseSolution>

4. **Longest increasing subsequence.** Derive the $O(n \log n)$ DP using patience sorting. Prove that the algorithm correctly computes LIS length by maintaining the smallest possible tail value for each subsequence length.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   Maintain array $tails[k]$ = the minimum possible tail value of an increasing subsequence of length $k$. For each $x$ in the input, binary search for the largest $k$ such that $tails[k] < x$, and set $tails[k+1] = x$. The length of $tails$ after processing all elements is the LIS length. Proof invariant: after processing $i$ elements, $tails[k]$ is the minimum tail of any increasing subsequence of length $k$ that uses only the first $i$ elements.
   </ExerciseSolution>

5. **Coin change (minimum coins).** Given coin denominations $d_1, \ldots, d_k$, find the minimum number of coins to make amount $A$. State the recurrence. Prove it produces the minimum when coins are unlimited (unbounded knapsack). Show that a greedy strategy fails for non-canonical coin systems.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   $DP[a] = \min_{i: d_i \le a} (1 + DP[a - d_i])$ with $DP[0] = 0$. The recurrence is correct by cut-and-paste: in an optimal coin set for amount $a$, remove one coin to get an optimal set for $a - d_i$. Greedy fails for denominations 1, 3, 4: the optimal for 6 is (3,3) = 2 coins, but greedy picks 4 + 1 + 1 = 3 coins.
   </ExerciseSolution>

6. **Optimal substructure failure.** Give a concrete problem where a "natural" DP state seems correct but fails the cut-and-paste argument because subproblem solutions interact. Show the interaction explicitly.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   **Shortest path with vertex colors.** Find the shortest path from $s$ to $t$ that visits at most 2 red vertices. A natural state is (vertex, reds_used). But two paths reaching the same vertex with equal reds_used may have different sets of visited red vertices, leading to different future feasibility. The cut-and-paste fails because the suffix solution may need a specific red vertex that was already used. Fix: augment state with the set of red vertices visited.
   </ExerciseSolution>

7. **Transfer: Design a DP for a novel problem.** You are given a sequence of $n$ daily stock prices $p_1, \ldots, p_n$ and a transaction cost $c$ per trade. You may hold at most one share at a time. Design a DP that computes the maximum profit achievable, where each transaction (buy then sell) incurs the cost $c$. State the state, recurrence, base cases, and complexity. Prove optimal substructure.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   State: $DP[i][h]$ = maximum profit after processing prices $1..i$ where $h \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates whether you hold a share. Recurrence: $DP[i][0] = \max(DP[i-1][0], DP[i-1][1] + p_i)$ (sell), $DP[i][1] = \max(DP[i-1][1], DP[i-1][0] - p_i - c)$ (buy, paying transaction cost). Base: $DP[0][0] = 0$, $DP[0][1] = -\infty$. Answer: $DP[n][0]$. Optimal substructure: any optimal sequence of decisions for $i$ prices either does nothing on day $i$ (inheriting optimal from $i-1$) or executes a trade (the remaining problem reduces to $i-1$ with the opposite holding state). Cut-and-paste applies because the two cases are independent.
   </ExerciseSolution>

8. **Synthesis: Compare DP, greedy, and divide-and-conquer.** Identify a problem that can be solved by all three paradigms (e.g., computing the maximum subarray sum). For each paradigm, write the recurrence (or decision rule), analyze the complexity, and explain why the other paradigms would be suboptimal for a different problem that fits only one paradigm.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   **Maximum subarray sum.** DP: $DP[i] = \max(DP[i-1] + A[i], A[i])$ with $DP[0] = A[0]$, answer $\max_i DP[i]$, $O(n)$. Greedy: maintain running sum, reset to 0 when negative — same as Kadane's algorithm, produces the same result, but the "greedy choice" (keep or reset) is justified by the DP recurrence. Divide-and-conquer: split at midpoint, recursively compute max subarray in left, right, and crossing the midpoint — $O(n \log n)$.

   **Why DP is needed for other problems:** For edit distance or knapsack, the greedy choice is not locally optimal (the exchange argument fails), and divide-and-conquer cannot efficiently handle overlapping subproblems. DP's systematic table-filling is essential when subproblems overlap and the optimal choice depends on multiple future outcomes.
   </ExerciseSolution>

9. **Transfer: DP on a novel graph structure.** You are given a rooted tree with $n$ nodes, each with a value $v_i \in \mathbb{Z}$. Select a subset of nodes such that no two selected nodes are adjacent (connected by an edge). Design a DP that computes the maximum total value of a valid subset. State the state, recurrence, and complexity. Prove optimal substructure.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="For each node, the DP must consider two cases: selected or not selected.">
   State: $DP[u][0]$ = max value in subtree of $u$ when $u$ is not selected; $DP[u][1]$ = max value when $u$ is selected. Recurrence: $DP[u][0] = \sum_{c \in \text{children}(u)} \max(DP[c][0], DP[c][1])$; $DP[u][1] = v_u + \sum_{c \in \text{children}(u)} DP[c][0]$. Base: leaf nodes have $DP[leaf][0] = 0$, $DP[leaf][1] = v_{leaf}$. Answer: $\max(DP[root][0], DP[root][1])$. Complexity: $O(n)$. Optimal substructure: If $u$ is selected, none of its children can be selected, so the optimal value from each child subtree is $DP[c][0]$. If $u$ is not selected, children may be selected or not, and the optimal choice per child is independent — the cut-and-paste argument applies to each child subtree independently.
   </ExerciseSolution>

10. **Synthesis: Decompose a problem into DP building blocks.** A company must schedule $n$ jobs on a single machine. Each job $j$ has a processing time $p_j$, a deadline $d_j$, and a profit $v_j$ (earned only if completed by its deadline). The machine can process at most one job at a time. (a) Prove that the problem of maximizing profit is NP-hard (hint: reduction from subset sum or knapsack). (b) Suppose all $p_j = 1$ (unit processing times). Design a DP that solves the problem in $O(n \max d_j)$ time. (c) Explain why the DP in (b) fails when $p_j$ are arbitrary, and identify the structural property that makes the unit-time case tractable.

    <ExerciseSolution>
    (a) Reduction from knapsack: each item $(w_i, v_i)$ maps to a job with $p_i = w_i$, $d_i = W$ (capacity), profit $v_i$. A feasible schedule with profit $\ge K$ corresponds to a knapsack solution with value $\ge K$. (b) Sort jobs by deadline. DP state: $DP[t]$ = max profit achievable by time $t$. Process jobs in deadline order: $DP[t] = \max(DP[t-1], \max_{j: d_j = t} (v_j + DP[t-1]))$. Since all $p_j = 1$, including a job at time $t$ uses the entire time slot. (c) With arbitrary $p_j$, the decision is not just which job to schedule at time $t$ but how to pack variable-length jobs into the timeline — this is a scheduling problem equivalent to knapsack. The unit-time case is tractable because the time dimension becomes a count of slots rather than a continuous resource.
    </ExerciseSolution>

## Deliberately Omitted

- **DP on tree decompositions and bounded treewidth** — Dynamic programming on graphs with bounded treewidth is a powerful technique for NP-hard problems on almost-trees; it is covered in the parameterized algorithms module.
- **Policy iteration for Markov decision processes** — This lesson presents value iteration for MDPs but omits policy iteration, which often converges in fewer iterations; both methods are covered in the reinforcement learning module.
- **DP with monotone queue optimization (sliding window)** — Optimizing DP transitions that depend on a sliding window of previous states (e.g., $DP[i] = \min_{j \in [i-k, i-1]} DP[j] + f(i,j)$) via monotone queues is a competitive-programming technique rather than a core algorithmic concept.
- **DP on trees** — Tree DP (e.g., maximum independent set on a tree) is a special case of DP on DAGs where the DAG is the tree itself; it warrants a dedicated treatment in the graph algorithms module.
