# Greedy Algorithms and Exchange Arguments

A greedy algorithm makes a locally preferred choice, commits to it, and solves the remaining problem. Greedy design is attractive because it is often fast and simple, but local plausibility is not a correctness proof. The central task is to identify a structural property—an exchange argument, a cut property, or a matroid—that guarantees some optimal solution begins with the greedy choice. Without that property, a counterexample may defeat every local rule.

## Greedy-choice and optimal substructure

Optimal substructure means an optimal solution contains optimal solutions to appropriately defined residual subproblems. Greedy-choice means there exists an optimal solution compatible with the first greedy choice. The second is stronger and is where most proof work lies. Once the choice is fixed and safe, optimal substructure justifies recursion or iteration on the remainder.

Use an exchange proof: take an arbitrary optimal solution O, identify its first choice, and replace that choice with the greedy choice without worsening objective value. The resulting solution remains feasible and optimal, so an optimal solution containing the greedy choice exists. Then recurse on the reduced instance. The exchange must preserve all constraints, not merely improve a numerical score.

## Interval scheduling

Given intervals with start and finish times, choose a maximum-size set of pairwise nonoverlapping intervals. The greedy rule “select the interval with earliest finishing time, remove conflicts, repeat” is optimal. Let g be the earliest-finishing interval and O an optimal schedule. If O starts with interval o, replace o by g. Since `finish(g)  at most  finish(o)`, every interval after o in O remains compatible with g; the count is unchanged. The residual problem has the same form, proving optimality by induction.

Other intuitive rules fail: shortest duration can block many later intervals; earliest start can choose a long interval; fewest conflicts can be misleading. The exchange proof depends specifically on the boundary that leaves the largest remaining suffix of time.

**Worked example.** Intervals (1,4),(3,5),(0,6),(5,7),(3,9),(5,9),(6,10),(8,11),(8,12),(2,14),(12,16). Choose (1,4), then (5,7), then (8,11), then (12,16), obtaining four. Choosing (0,6) first prevents several compatible intervals. The rule’s proof, not the example, establishes optimality.

## Fractional knapsack and the integrality boundary

In fractional knapsack, item i has value vi, weight wi, and fractions are allowed. Sort by density vi/wi and take highest density until capacity fills. If an optimal solution takes less density item while a higher-density available fraction remains, exchange weight from the lower to higher item and improve or preserve value. Therefore density-first is optimal.

0/1 knapsack forbids fractions, and density-first is not always optimal. A high-density small item can block a combination of lower-density items with greater total value. The difference illustrates a key warning: a greedy proof may depend on divisibility or an exchange that is impossible under integral constraints. Dynamic programming solves 0/1 knapsack pseudo-polynomially in capacity, not greedily in general.

## Minimum spanning trees

For a weighted undirected graph, a spanning tree with minimum total edge weight can be built by Kruskal or Prim. The cut property says that a lightest edge crossing any cut is safe: there exists an MST containing it. For Kruskal, consider the cut separating the components of the current forest; the next edge is the lightest edge crossing that cut and can be exchanged into an optimal completion. Union-find maintains acyclicity and components.

Prim grows one connected tree. At each step choose the lightest edge from the current tree to an outside vertex. The cut property proves safety. A priority queue stores candidate edges; stale entries must be ignored when a better key has already been recorded. The greedy invariant is that the chosen edges can be extended to an MST, not that every chosen edge belongs to every MST.

## Huffman coding

Huffman coding constructs an optimal binary prefix code for symbol frequencies. Repeatedly merge the two least frequent trees into a parent whose weight is their sum; assign 0/1 branches. The sibling property and an exchange argument show that some optimal code has the two least frequent symbols as deepest siblings. Contract those siblings into one symbol, solve the smaller instance, and expand.

The objective is weighted code length `sum f_i * ell_i`, not average bit length without frequencies. Equal frequencies permit multiple optimal codes. A prefix-free representation is necessary for instantaneous decoding; a variable-length code without the prefix property may be ambiguous. Canonical Huffman encodings choose a deterministic representation among optimal length profiles.

## Matroids and greedy structure

A matroid is a ground set with an independence system satisfying heredity and the exchange axiom: if A and B are independent with `|A|  less than  |B|`, some element of `B minus A` can be added to A. For any nonnegative weights, sorting elements by decreasing weight and adding an element when independence is preserved yields a maximum-weight independent set. The exchange axiom is the abstract reason greedy works.

Spanning forests form a graphic matroid; maximum-weight independent edges give a maximum spanning forest. The family of independent sets of a uniform matroid models choosing at most k items. Many scheduling and packing constraints are not matroids, which explains why a greedy proof may fail. Recognizing a matroid is useful but not necessary; a direct exchange proof suffices.

## Counterexamples and repair

When a greedy rule is suspected, search for the smallest instance where its first choice differs from an optimal solution. Enumerate small instances, compare exact dynamic programming or brute force, and inspect the failed exchange. The repair may be a different greedy key, a richer state, dynamic programming, or approximation with a proven ratio.

Tie-breaking can matter. A proof may allow any earliest-finishing interval, but a particular implementation must consistently handle equal endpoints and closed versus half-open intervals. If intervals that finish at time t are compatible with intervals starting at t, state that convention.

## Greedy proof checklist

Before committing to a greedy algorithm, write the feasibility invariant after every choice, identify the exchange or cut that makes the choice safe, and define the residual instance. Test natural alternatives against exhaustive small cases, but treat a passing search as evidence for a conjecture, not a proof. Check whether weights can be negative, whether ties affect feasibility, and whether an item can be split. Small changes to the constraints often move a problem from greedy to dynamic programming or flow.

Greedy algorithms are often online-friendly: the next choice can be made from current information, sometimes without seeing future input. Optimality may then require a stronger assumption, such as matroid independence or interval order. If future arrivals can invalidate a committed choice, analyze competitive ratio or use a deferred/offline algorithm instead of asserting offline optimality.

## Exercises

1. Prove earliest-finish interval scheduling by exchange and induction.
2. Give a counterexample to shortest-duration scheduling.
3. Prove fractional-knapsack density ordering.
4. Explain why the proof fails for 0/1 knapsack.
5. Prove Kruskal’s cut exchange step.
6. Explain Huffman’s two-least-frequencies lemma.
7. Identify a matroid or give a non-matroid greedy failure.

### Diagnostic solutions

1. Let g finish first and O be optimal with first o. Replace o by g; every later interval compatible with o starts after `finish(o)  at least  finish(g)`, so remains compatible. The residual schedule is optimal by induction, and adding g preserves cardinality.

2. Intervals [0,10], [0,1], [1,2], [2,3], ... make shortest duration choose the many short intervals here, but adjust examples with a short interval that blocks two moderately compatible intervals; the key test is whether the selected criterion leaves maximum residual capacity. Always verify endpoints and objective when presenting a counterexample.

3. If a solution uses weight on density d2 while available capacity is assigned to lower density d1, transfer a small amount from d1 to d2. Value does not decrease. Repeating yields density order, including a final fractional item.

4. The exchange may require taking a fraction or replacing only part of an integral item, which is infeasible. A locally denser item can consume capacity needed by a combination whose total value is larger; integrality destroys the continuous exchange.

5. Kruskal’s edge e is lightest across the cut between current forest components. In an MST completion, if e is absent, the tree path between its endpoints crosses the cut at another edge f with weight at least e. Replace f by e; acyclicity and connectivity remain, and weight does not increase.

6. In an optimal prefix code, two least-frequency symbols can be chosen as deepest siblings by swapping shallower/higher-frequency leaves without increasing weighted length. Contract them to a combined symbol, solve the smaller code, and expand; induction proves Huffman optimal.

7. A graphic matroid has acyclic edge sets and satisfies exchange, so weight-sorted independent selection is greedy-correct. 0/1 knapsack’s feasible sets do not satisfy a general exchange axiom; density greedy has counterexamples.
