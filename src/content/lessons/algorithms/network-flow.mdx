# Network Flow and Cuts

During the early 1950s, the United States Air Force faced a logistical problem. The Soviet railway network had limited capacity on each rail segment between cities. How many tanks could be moved from factories in the interior to the front lines, given that each section of track could carry at most a certain number of trains per day? This was the motivation for what became the **maximum flow problem** [Ford & Fulkerson 1956](#ford-fulkerson-1956).

The first attempt to solve it is obvious: find *some* route from source (factory) to sink (front line), send as much flow as the weakest link allows, then find another route, and repeat. This greedy approach — send flow along any available path — can get stuck. You might send flow along a path that later turns out to block a better overall routing, leaving a suboptimal solution that cannot be directly improved.

The breakthrough came in 1956 from L. R. Ford Jr. and D. R. Fulkerson (Ford & Fulkerson 1956). Their key insight: when you send flow along an edge, you must also record the *possibility of undoing* that flow later. This is the **residual graph**: it contains forward edges with remaining capacity and reverse edges representing flow that can be canceled. An augmenting path in the residual graph lets you reroute previously committed flow, escaping local optima.

> **Prerequisites.** Elementary graph theory (directed graphs, adjacency, paths), breadth-first and depth-first search, and basic familiarity with asymptotic notation ($O$, $\Omega$, $\Theta$). No prior exposure to network flow is assumed.

## Flow Definitions

<MathStatement id="flow-def" kind="definition" title="Flow network and flow" proofId="flow-def-example">
A flow network is a directed graph $G = (V, E)$ with source $s$, sink $t$, and capacity $c: E \to \mathbb{R}^{\ge 0}$. A flow $f: E \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies:
1. **Capacity constraint:** $0 \le f(u,v) \le c(u,v)$ for all $(u,v) \in E$.
2. **Flow conservation:** $\sum_{(u,v) \in E} f(u,v) = \sum_{(v,w) \in E} f(v,w)$ for all $v \in V \setminus \{s,t\}$.

The **value** of $f$ is $|f| = \sum_{(s,v) \in E} f(s,v) - \sum_{(v,s) \in E} f(v,s)$.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="flow-def-example" for="flow-def" title="Small network example" collapsible open>
Consider a network with $V = \{s, u, v, t\}$ and edges $s \to u$ (capacity 10), $s \to v$ (capacity 5), $u \to v$ (capacity 15), $u \to t$ (capacity 10), $v \to t$ (capacity 10). A feasible flow sends 5 on $s \to u$, 5 on $s \to v$, 5 on $u \to v$, 10 on $u \to t$, and 10 on $v \to t$. Check conservation at $u$: inflow 5 ($s \to u$), outflow 5 ($u \to v$) + 10 ($u \to t$) → violation. Find a valid assignment: send 10 on $s \to u$, 5 on $s \to v$, 5 on $u \to v$, 5 on $u \to t$, 10 on $v \to t$. At $u$: inflow 10, outflow 5+5 = 10 ✓. At $v$: inflow 5 ($s \to v$) + 5 ($u \to v$) = 10, outflow 10 ($v \to t$) ✓. Flow value: $10+5 = 15$.
</MathProof>

<MathStatement id="cut-def" kind="definition" title="s-t cut">
An $s$-$t$ cut $(S, T)$ partitions $V$ with $s \in S$, $t \in T$. Its capacity is $c(S,T) = \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} c(u,v)$.
</MathStatement>

**Weak duality:** For any flow $f$ and any $s$-$t$ cut $(S,T)$, $|f| \le c(S,T)$. *Proof.* Sum conservation equations over $S \setminus \{s\}$; internal edges cancel, leaving $|f| = f(S,T) - f(T,S) \le f(S,T) \le c(S,T)$. □

## Residual Network and Augmenting Paths

<MathStatement id="residual" kind="definition" title="Residual network">
Given flow $f$, the residual capacity of $(u,v)$ is:
$$
c_f(u,v) = \begin{cases}
c(u,v) - f(u,v) & (u,v) \in E \\
f(v,u) & (v,u) \in E \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
The residual graph $G_f = (V, E_f)$ where $E_f = \{(u,v) : c_f(u,v) > 0\}$.
</MathStatement>

An **augmenting path** is an $s$-$t$ path in $G_f$. Augmenting by $\Delta = \min_{(u,v) \in P} c_f(u,v)$ produces a new flow $f'$ with $|f'| = |f| + \Delta$.

<MathStatement id="augment-lemma" kind="lemma" title="Augmentation preserves feasibility" proofId="augment-lemma-proof">
If $f$ is a feasible flow and $P$ is an $s$-$t$ path in $G_f$ with bottleneck $\Delta > 0$, then the flow $f'$ defined by adding $\Delta$ on forward edges of $P$ and subtracting $\Delta$ on reverse edges of $P$ is a feasible flow with value $|f| + \Delta$.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="augment-lemma-proof" for="augment-lemma">
Capacity constraints are preserved by the bottleneck choice. Conservation: every internal vertex on $P$ has one incoming and one outgoing edge on $P$ (oriented consistently), each changed by $\pm\Delta$, so net change is zero. Vertices off $P$ are unchanged. The source increases net outflow by $\Delta$, and the sink increases net inflow by $\Delta$.
</MathProof>

## Max-Flow Min-Cut Theorem

<MathStatement id="maxflow-mincut" kind="theorem" title="Max-flow min-cut theorem" proofId="maxflow-mincut-proof">
For any network, the maximum flow value equals the minimum $s$-$t$ cut capacity.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="maxflow-mincut-proof" for="maxflow-mincut">
Let $f$ be a maximum flow. Let $S$ be the set of vertices reachable from $s$ in the residual graph $G_f$. Since $f$ is maximum, there is no $s$-$t$ path in $G_f$, so $t \notin S$. Thus $(S, V \setminus S)$ is an $s$-$t$ cut.

For every $u \in S, v \notin S$: if $(u,v) \in E$, then $c_f(u,v) = c(u,v) - f(u,v)$ must be $0$ (otherwise $v$ would be in $S$), so $f(u,v) = c(u,v)$. If $(v,u) \in E$, then $c_f(v,u) = f(u,v)$ must be $0$ (otherwise $u$ could reach $v$ via a reverse edge), so $f(u,v) = 0$.

Therefore flow on $S \to T$ edges equals their capacities, and flow on $T \to S$ edges is zero. The net flow across the cut is exactly $c(S,T)$. By weak duality, $|f| = c(S,T)$ is maximal and the cut is minimal.
</MathProof>

## Ford–Fulkerson Algorithm

**Algorithm:** While an $s$-$t$ path exists in $G_f$, augment along it.

**Correctness:** If the algorithm terminates, no augmenting path exists, and by the max-flow min-cut theorem, the flow is maximal. But termination is not guaranteed for irrational capacities — pathological examples with infinite augmentations exist [Zwick 1993, *IPL* 46: 211–214].

**Complexity (integer capacities):** Each augmentation increases flow by at least 1, so at most $|f^*|$ iterations. Each iteration costs $O(E)$ (BFS or DFS). Total: $O(E|f^*|)$, which is pseudopolynomial.

  code={`def ford_fulkerson(graph, s, t):
    n = len(graph)
    flow = [[0]*n for _ in range(n)]
    parent = [-1]*n
    def dfs(u, bottleneck):
        if u == t: return bottleneck
        for v, cap, idx in graph[u]:
            if parent[v] == -1 and cap - flow[u][v] > 0:
                parent[v] = u
                aug = dfs(v, min(bottleneck, cap - flow[u][v]))
                if aug > 0:
                    flow[u][v] += aug
                    flow[v][u] -= aug
                    return aug
        return 0
    total = 0
    while True:
        parent = [-1]*n
        parent[s] = s
        aug = dfs(s, float('inf'))
        if aug == 0: break
        total += aug
    return total`}

## Edmonds–Karp Algorithm

Use BFS to find the *shortest* (fewest edges) augmenting path each iteration. This refinement was published by Edmonds and Karp in 1972 [Edmonds & Karp 1972, *J. ACM* 19: 248–264].

<MathStatement id="ek-lemma" kind="lemma" title="Edmonds–Karp monotonic distance" proofId="ek-lemma-proof">
In Edmonds–Karp, the BFS distance $d_f(v) = \text{dist}_{G_f}(s, v)$ is nondecreasing for all $v$ across augmentations.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="ek-lemma-proof" for="ek-lemma" collapsible>
Suppose an augmentation makes some $v$ closer to $s$ for the first time. Consider the first augmentation that causes any distance to decrease. Let $v$ be the vertex with minimum decreased distance. In $G_f$ after augmentation, there is a shortest path $s \to \cdots \to u \to v$ with $\text{dist}(v) = \text{dist}(u) + 1$. Before augmentation, either $(u,v)$ was not in $G_f$ or it was and $\text{dist}(v) \ge \text{dist}(u) + 1$ (since distances were nondecreasing for all vertices before $v$). The only way $(u,v)$ appears in $G_f$ is if the augmentation saturated $(v,u)$ (creating reverse capacity). But if $(v,u)$ was saturated, then in the previous BFS, $(v,u)$ was the *critical edge* — it was on a shortest augmenting path. When $(v,u)$ is saturated, the distance to $v$ in that BFS was $\text{dist}(v) = \text{dist}(u) - 1$ (since the path used $(v,u)$). Thus after saturation, $\text{dist}(v) + 1 = \text{dist}(u) \le \text{dist}(u)$ before, so $\text{dist}(v)$ did not decrease.
</MathProof>

<MathStatement id="ek-complexity" kind="theorem" title="Edmonds–Karp complexity" proofId="ek-complexity-proof">
The Edmonds–Karp algorithm performs $O(VE)$ augmentations, each in $O(E)$ time, for total $O(VE^2)$.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="ek-complexity-proof" for="ek-complexity" collapsible>
An edge $(u,v)$ becomes *critical* (bottleneck of an augmenting path). After it is saturated, it disappears from $G_f$ and reappears only when flow is sent on its reverse. Between two critical appearances of $(u,v)$, the distance to $v$ increases by at least 2 (by the monotonic lemma and the fact that the reverse path goes $u \leftarrow v$). Since distances are bounded by $V$, each edge can be critical $O(V)$ times, giving $O(VE)$ augmentations.
</MathProof>

## Dinic's Algorithm

Dinic's algorithm, published in 1970 [Dinic 1970, *Soviet Mathematics Doklady* 11: 1277–1280], introduces **layered networks**. From BFS in $G_f$, keep only edges that go from layer $l$ to layer $l+1$ (the *level graph*). Find a *blocking flow* in the level graph via DFS with dead-end pruning.

<MathStatement id="dinic-complexity" kind="theorem" title="Dinic's algorithm complexity" proofId="dinic-complexity-proof">
Dinic's algorithm runs in $O(V^2 E)$ time on general networks and $O(\min(V^{2/3}, \sqrt{E}) E)$ on unit-capacity networks.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="dinic-complexity-proof" for="dinic-complexity" collapsible>
Each BFS phase increases $d(t)$ by at least 1 (since a blocking flow saturates all level-$d(t)$ paths). There are at most $V$ phases. In each phase, finding the blocking flow via DFS spends $O(E)$ per path, and each DFS either reaches $t$ (saturating at least one edge) or backtracks (pruning a vertex). Each edge is saturated at most once per phase. Total per phase: $O(VE)$ (each vertex becomes dead end once). Multiply by $V$ phases: $O(V^2 E)$.

For unit-capacity networks, $d(t)$ grows as $O(\sqrt{E})$ or $O(V^{2/3})$, giving better bounds.
</MathProof>

## Push-Relabel Algorithm

Push-relabel, due to Goldberg and Tarjan [Goldberg & Tarjan 1988, *J. ACM* 35: 921–940], maintains a *preflow* (conservation can be violated — excess flow may accumulate at vertices) and a height labeling $h$. The operation is: while a vertex $u$ with excess exists, push flow to a neighbor $v$ with $h(u) = h(v) + 1$; if no such neighbor exists, relabel $h(u) = 1 + \min_{(u,v) \in E_f} h(v)$.

<MathStatement id="push-relabel" kind="theorem" title="Push-relabel correctness" proofId="push-relabel-proof">
The push-relabel algorithm maintains a preflow and terminates with a maximum flow in $O(V^3)$ time.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="push-relabel-proof" for="push-relabel" collapsible>
Invariant: $h$ is a valid labeling — $h(s) = V$, $h(t) = 0$, and $h(u) \le h(v) + 1$ for all $(u,v) \in E_f$. Push maintains this; relabel preserves it. When no excess remains, the preflow is a flow, and the cut defined by $\{u : h(u) \ge V\}$ has capacity 0 in $G_f$, proving maximality. Each vertex is relabeled $O(V)$ times (height bounded by $2V-1$), and each edge is saturated $O(V)$ times, giving $O(V^3)$ total pushes.

The **highest-label** variant (select the vertex with maximum height) and **gap heuristics** improve practical performance.
</MathProof>

## Integrality Theorem

<MathStatement id="integrality" kind="theorem" title="Integrality of max flow">
If all capacities are integers, there exists a maximum flow $f$ such that $f(u,v)$ is an integer for every edge.
</MathStatement>

**Proof.** Ford–Fulkerson starting from the zero flow always augments by integer amounts (the bottleneck is integer). Thus every flow encountered is integral. The limit flow is integer. For non-integer capacities, the integrality theorem does not hold. □

**Consequence for bipartite matching:** Unit-capacity edges on source/sink give integer flows that correspond to matchings.

## Counterexamples and Failure Modes

### Ford–Fulkerson can be exponential

With integer capacities, Ford–Fulkerson can take $\Theta(2^{V/2})$ augmentations on a carefully constructed graph. Consider two long parallel paths sharing a single capacity-1 edge. Each augmentation sends 1 unit, forcing the algorithm to alternate between the two paths until the cut capacity is exhausted. This demonstrates why BFS-based shortest-path selection (Edmonds–Karp) is necessary for polynomial guarantees.

### Non-termination with irrational capacities

Ford–Fulkerson using DFS for path selection may never terminate if capacities are irrational. Zwick (1993) constructed a network with four vertices and three irrational capacities where the algorithm performs an infinite sequence of augmentations converging to a flow value strictly below the true maximum. The residual graph always contains an augmenting path, but each augmentation shrinks the bottleneck geometrically without ever reaching zero.

### Greedy path selection can block optimal flow

Without reverse edges (the residual graph), a naive greedy augmenting path algorithm can produce a suboptimal flow. The classic example: a graph with edges $s \to a$ (cap 1), $s \to b$ (cap 1), $a \to b$ (cap 1), $a \to t$ (cap 1), $b \to t$ (cap 1). A greedy first choice $s \to a \to b \to t$ sends 1 unit but saturates $a \to b$, blocking any further flow. The max flow is 2 ($s \to a \to t$ and $s \to b \to t$). Reverse edges in the residual graph allow undoing the mistake.

## Circulation with Demands

A *circulation* assigns flow values to edges with no source or sink. Each vertex has a demand $d(v)$: if $d(v) > 0$, the vertex requires $d(v)$ units of net inflow; if $d(v) < 0$, it supplies $-d(v)$ units. A circulation satisfies $\sum_{(u,v) \in E} f(u,v) - \sum_{(v,w) \in E} f(v,w) = d(v)$. Feasibility is equivalent to a max-flow problem with a super-source and super-sink [CLRS 2022, Ch. 26].

## Minimum-Cost Flow

Each edge has both capacity $c(u,v)$ and cost per unit $a(u,v)$. The min-cost max-flow problem seeks a flow of value $k$ minimizing total cost $\sum a(u,v) f(u,v)$.

**Successive shortest augmenting path algorithm.** Maintain reduced costs $\tilde{a}(u,v) = a(u,v) + \phi(u) - \phi(v)$ via potentials $\phi$. Dijkstra in the residual graph with reduced costs finds the cheapest augmenting path. Potentials ensure nonnegative reduced costs after each augmentation (if no negative cycles exist initially). Complexity: $O(k E \log V)$ for $k$ units of flow.

<MathStatement id="mincost-potential" kind="theorem" title="Optimality condition for min-cost flow">
A flow $f$ is minimum-cost for its value iff the residual graph $G_f$ contains no negative-cost directed cycle.
</MathStatement>

**Proof.** If a negative cycle exists, augmenting flow around it reduces cost while preserving flow value. Conversely, if no negative cycle exists, any feasible augmentation decomposes into paths and cycles; negative cycles would allow improvement. □

## Applications

**Edge-disjoint paths.** Maximum number of edge-disjoint $s$-$t$ paths equals the max flow with unit capacities. Vertex-disjoint paths reduce to edge-disjoint via vertex splitting.

**Project selection.** Projects have profit $p_i$ (positive or negative) and prerequisites $i \to j$ (if $i$ is selected, $j$ must be selected). Model as a min-cut problem: source connects to positive-profit projects, negative-profit projects connect to sink, prerequisite edges have infinite capacity. The selected set is the sink side of the minimum cut.

**Baseball elimination.** Determine if a team can still finish first given remaining games. Formulate as a max-flow feasibility problem [Schwartz 1966, *Amer. Math. Monthly* 73: 308–309].

2. **Edmonds–Karp monotonicity.** Prove that the BFS distance $d_f(v)$ is strictly increasing for vertices on the shortest path after an edge is critical. Use this to show the $O(VE)$ bound.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Track the BFS distance to the source for vertices on the path before and after an edge becomes critical, then again when it reappears via its reverse.">

   When $(u,v)$ is critical, it is on a shortest path: $\text{dist}(v) = \text{dist}(u) + 1$. It disappears from $G_f$ after saturation. When it reappears, the path in the new BFS goes $v \to u$, so $\text{dist}(v) = \text{dist}(u) - 1$ in the new labeling. By monotonicity, $\text{dist}(u)$ in the new labeling is at least its old value, so $\text{dist}_\text{new}(v) = \text{dist}_\text{new}(u) - 1 \ge \text{dist}_\text{old}(u) - 1 = \text{dist}_\text{old}(v) + 1 - 1 = \text{dist}_\text{old}(v) + 0$. Actually, need a stronger bound: between critical appearances, $\text{dist}(v)$ increases by at least 2 because the path $v \to \cdots \to u$ after the reverse edge addition has length at least the old BFS distance to $u$ plus 1, which is at least $\text{dist}_\text{old}(v) + 2$ due to the reversal.
   </ExerciseSolution>

3. **Mincost flow reduction.** Reduce the min-cost flow problem to a sequence of shortest-path computations. Describe how potentials are maintained and how Dijkstra can be used despite negative costs.

The reduced costs remain nonnegative, so Dijkstra works.
   </ExerciseSolution>

4. **Circulation feasibility.** Given a circulation problem with demands $d(v)$ and capacities, construct a max-flow instance whose maximum flow equals a feasible circulation iff the total supply equals total demand.

Add an edge $(t, s)$ with infinite capacity to balance. The max flow saturates all supply edges iff a feasible circulation exists.
   </ExerciseSolution>

6. **Project selection min-cut.** Prove that the minimum $s$-$t$ cut in the project selection network corresponds to a maximal-profit closed set.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   The cut separates selected projects (sink side) from unselected ones (source side). If a selected project has an unselected prerequisite, the infinite capacity edge crosses the cut, making the cut capacity infinite and thus not minimal. Therefore every project in the sink set has all its prerequisites also in the sink set, forming a closed set. Minimizing cut capacity maximizes net profit.
   </ExerciseSolution>

   <ExerciseSolution hint="AND-prerequisites can be modeled by introducing an auxiliary vertex for the prerequisite pair with infinite-capacity edges enforcing both must be selected.">

   Introduce an auxiliary vertex $x$ for each AND-prerequisite $i \to (j \land k)$. Add edges $i \to x$ with infinite capacity, $x \to j$ and $x \to k$ with infinite capacity. If $i$ is selected but $j$ is not, the infinite edge $x \to j$ crosses the cut. This forces $j$ and $k$ into the selected set when $i$ is selected. The construction generalizes to any monotone Boolean prerequisite formula.
   </ExerciseSolution>

8. **Image segmentation as min cut.** An image is a grid of pixels. Each pixel $i$ has a foreground likelihood $f_i$ and background likelihood $b_i$. Adjacent pixels $i,j$ incur penalty $p_{ij}$ if assigned to different regions. Model the segmentation as a minimum $s$-$t$ cut problem. Show that the optimal segmentation minimizes $\sum_i (\text{cost of assignment}) + \sum_{(i,j)} p_{ij} \cdot [\text{boundary between } i \text{ and } j]$.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Create a source representing foreground and sink representing background. Pixel nodes connect to source with capacity $f_i$ and to sink with capacity $b_i$. Adjacent pixels connect bidirectionally with capacity $p_{ij}$.">

   Construct a network with source $s$ (foreground), sink $t$ (background), and a node for each pixel $i$. Add edge $s \to i$ with capacity $f_i$ and edge $i \to t$ with capacity $b_i$. For each adjacent pair $(i,j)$, add undirected edges $i \to j$ and $j \to i$ each with capacity $p_{ij}$. A cut $(S,T)$ places pixels in $S$ in the foreground and pixels in $T$ in the background. The cut capacity equals $\sum_{i \in T} f_i + \sum_{i \in S} b_i + \sum_{\text{boundary edges crossing the cut}} p_{ij}$, which is exactly the energy function to minimize.
   </ExerciseSolution>

### Counterexample analysis exercise

9. **Irrational capacity pathology.** Construct a network with three vertices and two edges where Ford–Fulkerson with a naive DFS path selection strategy performs an infinite number of augmentations. Explain why Edmonds–Karp (shortest path) fails to terminate here as well, and why Dinic's level-graph approach resolves the issue.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Use capacities that form a geometric series, e.g., $c(s,u) = 1$, $c(u,t) = \phi$ where $\phi = (1+\sqrt5)/2$, and $c(s,t) = 0$. Show that DFS can pick paths that shrink the remaining capacity geometrically.">

   In the Zwick construction, three vertices $s, u, t$ with capacities $c(s,u) = 1$, $c(u,t) = \phi$, $c(s,t) = 0$. A naive DFS sending flow along $s \to u \to t$ produces residual capacities that follow a recurrence $c_{k+1} = 1 - (1 - c_k)/\phi$, converging to a fixed point without reaching zero. The algorithm performs infinitely many augmentations. Edmonds–Karp does not help here because the shortest path is always $s \to u \to t$ (the only available path). Dinic resolves this because its level graph explicitly limits each phase to a single blocking flow, which saturates the path in finitely many phases (one per unit of layered distance increase).
   </ExerciseSolution>

## Deliberately Omitted

The following topics are essential to a complete understanding of network flow but are addressed in other lessons or advanced references:

- **Capacity scaling and Dinic with scaling.** The scaling technique for improving max-flow complexity to $O(E \cdot \min(V^{2/3}, \sqrt{E}) \cdot \log U)$ for integer capacities. This optimization detail builds on Dinic and is best studied after mastering the basic algorithm. See [CLRS 2022, Ch. 26].

- **Minimum-cost circulation and the network simplex algorithm.** Min-cost flow admits strongly polynomial algorithms (Tardos 1985) and specialized simplex variants. The successive shortest-augmenting-path method covered here provides the essential intuition. See [Ahuja, Magnanti & Orlin 1993, *Network Flows*].

- **Global min-cut without source-sink (Stoer–Wagner).** Finding the minimum cut of an undirected graph without a designated source-sink pair uses a contraction technique unrelated to augmenting paths. This appears in the advanced graph algorithms module.

- **Dynamic and parametric flow.** Flows that change under incremental capacity updates or parameterized edge capacities (e.g., GGT algorithm for parametric max flow) are an active research area beyond this lesson's scope.

- **Planar flow equivalence.** Maximum flow in planar graphs admits $O(V \log V)$ algorithms via dual shortest-path reductions. This is a specialized geometric technique.
