# Pumping Lemmas

The regular pumping lemma says every sufficiently long word in a regular language has a decomposition $xyz$ with $|xy|le p$, $|y| greater than 0$, and $xy^iz$ in the language for every $ige0$. To prove non-regularity, assume regularity, choose a strategic word, and show every permitted decomposition fails for some $i$.

The lemma is a necessary condition, not a characterization: satisfying it does not prove regularity.

## The regular pumping lemma precisely

If L is regular, there is a pumping length p such that every word w in L with length at least p can be written w=xyz with |xy| at most p, |y| greater than 0, and xy^iz in L for every integer i at least 0. The decomposition may depend on w, and the lemma promises existence of at least one successful decomposition. A non-regularity proof must therefore defeat every permitted decomposition, not just the decomposition that seems most convenient.

The bound |xy| at most p says the pumped segment lies among the first p symbols. This is where a carefully chosen prefix creates rigidity. For w=0^p1^p, every nonempty y in the first p positions consists only of zeros. Pumping y with i=0 leaves fewer zeros than ones; pumping with i=2 leaves more zeros than ones. Either word leaves the language. The contradiction does not assume a particular length for y, only its location and nonemptiness.

## Quantifier game for a proof

The proof order is: assume L regular; let p be its pumping length; choose one w in L with |w| at least p; consider an arbitrary legal split xyz; choose an exponent i, possibly based on the split; show xy^iz is not in L. The word is chosen after p is revealed, but before the split is selected. The exponent may respond to the split. Reversing these choices weakens the proof.

For a language with several blocks, classify the possible location of y. If y lies entirely in one block, pumping changes that block only. If y crosses a boundary, the length condition may rule it out; if not, pumping can destroy the required block order. Write the cases exhaustively. Saying “the same argument applies” is acceptable only after identifying the invariant shared by the cases.

## Myhill-Nerode as an alternative

The pumping lemma is often awkward for languages whose obstruction is not a simple count. The Myhill-Nerode theorem says L is regular exactly when the relation x equivalent-to-L y, defined by xz in L iff yz in L for every suffix z, has finitely many equivalence classes. To prove non-regularity, find infinitely many prefixes that are pairwise distinguishable: for each i and j, choose a suffix z that makes one extension belong to L and the other not.

For L=()0^n1^n(), prefixes 0^i and 0^j with i less than j are distinguished by 1^i: 0^i1^i is in L, while 0^j1^i is not. This proof directly exhibits infinitely many required states and often gives a lower bound on DFA size for finite variants. Pumping explains a repeated loop; Myhill-Nerode explains why no finite summary of the prefix can preserve every future decision.

## Context-free pumping lemma

If L is context-free, there is a pumping length p such that each sufficiently long word w in L has a decomposition w=uvxyz with |vxy| at most p, |vy| greater than 0, and uv^ixy^iz in L for every i at least 0. The two pumped pieces arise from repeated nonterminals along a path in a parse tree. Their total window is bounded, but they may occur in different positions within that window.

To prove a language is not context-free, again defeat every legal decomposition. A common strategy uses three or more blocks with linked counts, such as ()a^i b^j c^k : i=j=k(). Since v and y occupy a short window, they cannot simultaneously control all three distant blocks. Pumping changes at least one count relation. Cases must handle whether the pieces lie in one block, cross one boundary, or affect two neighboring blocks.

The context-free lemma is weaker than the regular one because the grammar can duplicate a bounded region in two places. It cannot prove every non-context-free language easily, and satisfying it does not prove context-freeness. Use closure properties, Ogden’s lemma, or a reduction when the pumped-window cases become unmanageable.

## Closure-assisted pumping arguments

If L were regular and R were regular, then L intersect R would be regular. Choose R to isolate a simpler sublanguage whose non-regularity is obvious. For example, if a proposed language has many distracting symbols, intersect with a regular language that forces a single block pattern. If the intersection is known non-regular, the original language cannot have been regular.

The same idea works with homomorphisms and inverse homomorphisms, but preserve the direction of the implication. A homomorphic image of a regular language is regular. Therefore if a homomorphic image is non-regular, the source was not regular. For context-free languages, homomorphism and inverse homomorphism closure remain available, but intersection with an arbitrary context-free language is not generally closed.

## Common invalid proofs

Choosing one decomposition and showing it fails proves nothing because the lemma promises that another decomposition might work. Pumping with i=0 only is fine if it breaks membership for every split; otherwise choose i based on the split. Assuming the pumping length equals a convenient constant is invalid; p is unknown. Showing that a particular word cannot be pumped does not refute regularity if the word is shorter than p; choose a family and use the unknown p to select a long member.

Another mistake is using the pumping lemma in the positive direction: if a language has the stated property, it need not be regular. The lemma gives a necessary condition. For positive results, build an automaton, regular expression, grammar, or closure construction.

### Diagnostic solutions


1. Assume regular with pumping length p and choose w=0^p1^p. Any legal y lies in the initial zero block and is 0^k for k greater than 0. Pump i=0 to obtain 0^(p-k)1^p, which is not in the language. This defeats every legal split.
2. The pumped segment can lie in a symmetric region, and pumping one side may be compensated by a different decomposition argument. The cases depend on the split’s position; Myhill-Nerode or a closure argument may be clearer.
3. The prefixes 0^i are pairwise distinguishable: for i less than j, suffix 1^i accepts the i-prefix extension and rejects the j-prefix extension. Infinitely many classes contradict regularity.
4. For sufficiently long w in a context-free language, w=uvxyz with a bounded middle window, nonempty combined pumped material v and y, and uv^ixy^iz in the language for every i. Two regions reflect repeated nonterminals in a parse path.
5. Choose a regular R that forces the desired block order or alphabet pattern. If L were regular, L intersect R would be regular; deriving that the intersection is non-regular contradicts the assumption.
6. The pumping length is chosen by the existential lemma before the word and decomposition. A proof cannot select a decomposition or pumping behavior before p is fixed.



The strongest short answer includes the adversarial order explicitly: choose the pumping length, choose the witness, let the decomposition be arbitrary, and then choose the exponent. This is the proof’s strategy and should appear in the written solution, not remain implicit.

## Proof audit

Before submitting a pumping proof, mark the four quantifiers in different colors: the unknown pumping length, the chosen witness word, the arbitrary legal decomposition, and the exponent selected after seeing that decomposition. Then check that the witness belongs to the language and is long enough for every possible pumping length. This mechanical audit catches the majority of invalid arguments and makes a concise proof rigorous rather than merely plausible.

**Exercise.** Use the lemma to analyze $()0^n1^n:nge0()$. Explicitly quantify over all allowed positions of $y$.

## Final proof checklist

Write the quantifier order in the margin: assume regularity, obtain p, choose w, let xyz be arbitrary, choose i, derive a contradiction. If a case split depends on where y lies, list all locations allowed by |xy| at most p. A proof that chooses a convenient decomposition or assumes p equals a fixed constant has not yet defeated the pumping lemma.

## Worked case analysis

For a language with three blocks, choose a witness with each block of length p. The regular pumping segment lies within the first p symbols, so it cannot cross beyond the first block. Pumping changes only that block and leaves the other two unchanged, breaking any equality relation. If the language also imposes a fixed order, choose an exponent that makes the segment duplicate across a boundary only when the position analysis allows it; in every case, membership must fail for at least one exponent. The proof is a quantified adversarial argument, not a claim about one convenient decomposition.

The same discipline applies to the context-free pumping lemma, except that the bounded window contains two pumped regions. Draw the window over the witness before classifying cases; this makes it clear which distant blocks cannot both be controlled by v and y.
