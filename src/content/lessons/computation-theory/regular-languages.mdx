# Regular Languages: Structure and Limits

The central question of this lesson is not how to draw one automaton. It is how to reason about all possible finite-state descriptions of a language. A regular language has only finitely many relevant distinctions among prefixes: after reading two prefixes that lead to the same state, every continuation must receive the same answer. This observation supports minimization, equivalence, and impossibility proofs.

## Continuations as meaning

For a language L and strings x,y, say x and y are distinguishable with respect to L if there exists a suffix z such that exactly one of xz and yz belongs to L. If no such z exists, they are equivalent: every continuation produces the same membership answer. This is a right-congruence because equivalence is preserved when the same symbol or suffix is appended.

Every DFA induces an equivalence: prefixes are equivalent when they reach the same state. Therefore a DFA with k states can distinguish at most k classes of prefixes. Conversely, the canonical classes of indistinguishable prefixes form the states of the minimal DFA, when there are finitely many. This is the content of the Myhill-Nerode theorem.

**Worked distinguishability proof.** Let (L=()0^n1^n:nge0()). The prefixes epsilon, 0, 00, ..., 0^m are pairwise distinguishable. For i less than j, choose suffix (1^i). Then (0^i1^iin L), while (0^j1^inotin L). Infinitely many distinguishable prefixes require infinitely many DFA states, so L is not regular. The proof does not rely on a particular machine or an intuition that a counter is needed; it counts required future distinctions.

## Myhill-Nerode theorem

Define (xequiv_L y) iff for every z, (xzin L) exactly when (yzin L). The theorem says L is regular if and only if (equiv_L) has finitely many equivalence classes. For the forward direction, any DFA maps strings to finitely many states, and equal states imply equivalence. For the reverse direction, use equivalence classes as states, start at [epsilon], transition [x] on a to [xa], and accept [x] when xin L. Congruence makes the transition well-defined, and a finite number of classes makes a finite automaton.

This theorem gives an exact notion of minimal memory. Two strings may have different textual prefixes but be indistinguishable for the particular language. For the language of strings ending in "01", prefixes with the same relevant suffix class can share a state. For a language requiring equal unbounded counts, every count creates a future distinction.

## DFA minimization

Given a complete DFA, first remove unreachable states. Mark every pair with exactly one accepting state. Repeatedly mark a pair (p,q) if some symbol sends it to an already marked pair. The unmarked pairs are equivalent and can be merged. The process terminates because there are finitely many pairs, and the resulting quotient DFA is minimal.

The table-filling algorithm is a decision procedure for equivalence of states. Its correctness follows by induction on distinguishing-string length: length zero distinguishes acceptance; if a symbol leads to a pair distinguished by a string of length k, the original pair is distinguished by that symbol followed by the string. If no pair is marked, every continuation agrees.

Minimization is not the same as simplifying a diagram visually. A state that appears redundant may be distinguished by a long suffix. Conversely, two states with different local transitions may still be equivalent if their successor behavior is equivalent. The partition-refinement view starts with accepting versus rejecting blocks and repeatedly splits blocks by transition signatures.

## Pumping lemma for regular languages

If L is regular, there exists a pumping length p such that every string win L with |w|ge p can be written (w=xyz), with |xy|le p, |y| greater than 0, and (xy^izin L) for every ige0. A DFA with p states repeats a state among the first p+1 prefixes; y labels the cycle. Pumping is a necessary property of regular languages, useful for proving nonregularity.

The quantifiers matter. To prove L is not regular, assume L regular and obtain an arbitrary pumping length p. Choose a specific w depending on p. Then show every allowed decomposition with |xy|le p and |y| greater than 0 fails for at least one i. It is not enough to exhibit one decomposition that fails; the lemma promises the existence of a good decomposition, so the contradiction must defeat all permitted decompositions.

For (L=()0^n1^n:nge0()), choose (w=0^p1^p). Any y within the first p positions consists only of 0s, so pumping y with i=0 creates fewer 0s than 1s and leaves L. Hence L is not regular. The lemma can also prove languages with modular or finite-prefix restrictions are regular only if applied carefully; failure to find a pumping contradiction does not prove regularity.

## Closure as a proof tool

Closure properties let one transform a proposed regular language into a known nonregular language. Suppose (L=()win()0,1()^*: #0(w)=#1(w)()) were regular. Intersect with the regular language (0^*1^*). The result is (()0^n1^n:nge0()), nonregular, contradicting closure under intersection. This can be shorter than a direct pumping argument.

Homomorphisms replace symbols by strings. Regular languages are closed under homomorphism and inverse homomorphism. Quotienting by a fixed string or regular language also preserves regularity in standard forms. When using closure, state the operation and the exact resulting language; “regular languages are closed under operations” is not a proof until the intersection or mapping is shown.

## Deciding and learning finite behavior

Membership for a DFA is decided by one pass through the input, using time O(n) and constant machine-state space. Equivalence of two DFAs is decided by reachable product exploration. A shortest distinguishing string can be reconstructed from predecessor pointers. These algorithms show that formal proofs can yield executable diagnostics.

The finite-state viewpoint also explains lexical analysis. A token rule such as identifiers, integer literals, or comments can often be expressed by a regular language. Combining token rules requires priority and longest-match policy, which are extra decisions beyond the language itself. A lexer cannot, in general, match arbitrarily nested parentheses with finite state alone; a parser with stack-like memory is needed.

## A complete proof workflow

For a regularity claim, give a machine or a regular-expression construction and prove both directions: every accepted string has the intended property, and every intended string has an accepting run. For a nonregularity claim, choose the proof tool whose quantifiers you can control. Myhill-Nerode is often clean when infinitely many prefixes have distinct future behavior; the pumping lemma is useful when every allowed pump visibly changes a counted structure; closure is efficient when intersection isolates a known hard language.

Do not use the pumping lemma to prove regularity, and do not conclude regularity merely because small examples fit a finite pattern. Conversely, a language can satisfy some pumping-like property and still be nonregular, so a failed contradiction is not evidence of a finite automaton. The proof must establish the theorem’s hypothesis or derive a contradiction from it.

## Exercises

1. Use distinguishability to prove that the language of binary strings with exactly n trailing 1s, for a fixed n, is regular and determine a minimal-state bound.
2. Apply Myhill-Nerode to prove (()0^n1^n()) is nonregular.
3. Minimize a DFA with states whose transition table you construct for strings divisible by 3 in binary.
4. Write a fully quantified pumping-lemma proof for (()0^n1^n()).
5. Use closure under intersection to prove that (()ww:win()0,1()^*()) is not regular or explain why your attempted closure reduction fails.
6. Explain why regular token languages do not imply that a programming-language grammar is regular.

### Diagnostic solutions


1. Track the last n symbols and a failure/shorter-suffix condition; a finite automaton exists. A minimal count depends on exact acceptance definition, but strings with different relevant suffix lengths can be distinguished by a continuation of 1s or 0, so the states cannot all collapse.
2. For each ige0, prefixes (0^i) and (0^j) with i less than j are distinguished by (1^i): the first yields (0^i1^i), the second does not. Infinitely many classes contradict finite-state recognition.
3. Track the remainder modulo 3. Reading bit b maps remainder r to ((2r+b)bmod3). Start at 0 and accept remainder 0. Test that no two remainder states can merge because suitable suffixes produce acceptance from one and rejection from another.
4. Assume a pumping length p. Choose w=(0^p1^p). For every decomposition w=xyz with |xy|le p and |y| greater than 0, y=(0^k) for k greater than 0. Pump i=0; the result has p-k zeros and p ones, not equal counts, contradiction.
5. A direct intersection with a simple regular language does not immediately isolate a known nonregular sublanguage because the equality pattern is not preserved by a one-block restriction. A correct proof can use the pumping lemma or Myhill-Nerode; closure reductions require a carefully chosen regular transduction or intersection.
6. Token boundaries may be regular while nested syntax requires unbounded matching of open and close constructs. Lexing and parsing use different memory models; a regular lexer does not imply regular recognition of complete programs.
