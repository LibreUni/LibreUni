# Theory Assessment

## Instructions

This assessment is designed as a closed-book diagnostic after completing the course. Define every machine and language used, state quantifiers explicitly, and give enough intermediate reasoning that a reader can locate a failed invariant or reduction. A final answer without a construction or proof earns little credit. When a question asks for a counterexample, one valid counterexample is enough; when it asks for non-regularity or undecidability, the argument must address every candidate machine or decider.

## Part I: finite automata

1. Construct a DFA over ()0,1() for strings whose binary value is congruent to 2 modulo 5. State the meaning of each state and prove the transition invariant.
2. Prove that the language of strings containing 010 as a substring is regular using a direct DFA or closure construction.
3. Use Myhill-Nerode to lower-bound the number of states needed for ()0^i1^j : i=j modulo 7().
4. Convert an epsilon-NFA to a DFA and explain why epsilon closure is recomputed after every symbol.

For the modular-value question, use states r in ()0,...,4(); reading bit b updates r to (2r+b) modulo 5. The invariant is that r equals the value of the consumed prefix modulo 5. This is a model answer for how a state meaning turns into a proof.

## Part II: context-free languages

5. Give a CFG and PDA for balanced parentheses and prove equivalence at the level of prefixes and stack contents.
6. Show that the grammar E - greater than  E+E | E*E | n is ambiguous by producing two trees for n+n*n. Rewrite it to encode multiplication precedence and left associativity.
7. Use the context-free pumping lemma or closure under regular intersection to argue that ()a^n b^n c^n() is not context-free.
8. Explain one language recognized by an NPDA but not naturally by a DPDA, and identify the information that must be guessed.

The grading emphasis is on the source of nondeterminism and on the distinction between a derivation and a parse tree. A PDA trace without a stack invariant is evidence of behavior, not a correctness proof.

## Part III: computability and reductions

9. Prove the halting problem undecidable by diagonal contradiction.
10. Reduce a known undecidable problem to a program-property problem and prove both directions.
11. Explain why a recognizer for a language need not decide it.
12. Give a many-one reduction and identify its computability and size obligations.

In a diagonal proof, assume a decider H for halting and define D(P) to reject if H(P,P) says halt and loop otherwise. Running D on its own source yields a contradiction. The contradiction comes from total correctness of H, not from a runtime measurement.

## Part IV: complexity

13. Give an NP verifier for a graph problem and prove polynomial certificate length.
14. Explain the direction of a reduction used to prove NP-hardness.
15. Distinguish P, NP, PSPACE, and undecidable in terms of their guarantees.
16. State one conditional consequence of a polynomial algorithm for an NP-complete problem.

## Rubric and self-check

For each construction, check all transitions or productions and test edge cases. For each impossibility proof, mark the assumed object, the adversarial choice, and the contradiction. For each reduction, draw the direction and write the decoding step. For each complexity claim, specify encoding and model. A strong submission is self-contained: a reader should not need lecture notes to infer a missing definition.

## Diagnostic solutions

1. The five-state remainder automaton uses update (2r+b) mod 5 and accepts state 2. Induction on prefix length proves the meaning.
2. Build a DFA tracking the longest suffix that is a prefix of 010, with an absorbing accepting state after occurrence.
3. Prefixes 0^i for i=0,...,6 can be distinguished with suffixes that complete one residue relation and not another; at least seven classes result.
4. A DFA subset is epsilon-closure of the current NFA set after moving on the symbol. Closure is needed because the NFA may change state without consuming input before or after that symbol.
5. Push a marker for each open parenthesis and pop for each close; the stack-height invariant proves balanced-prefix behavior. The grammar S - greater than  (S)S gives the same language by structural induction.
6. The plus-root and multiplication-root trees differ. Use E - greater than  E+T | T, T - greater than  T*F | F, F - greater than  (E) | n; left recursion encodes left associativity.
7. Intersect with a^*b^*c^* if needed, then apply the context-free pumping lemma: a bounded pumping window cannot change all three distant equal counts together.
8. Palindromes without a delimiter require guessing the midpoint; an NPDA can guess when to switch from pushing to popping, while a deterministic machine cannot generally know the midpoint from the prefix alone.
9. The diagonal machine D contradicts H on input D because H predicts exactly the opposite of D’s behavior.
10. Construct a program that simulates M(w) and exhibits the target property exactly after M halts. A target decider would decide halting.
11. A recognizer may loop forever on no instances. A decider must halt on both yes and no instances.
12. A map f is computable, preserves membership in both directions, and has output size computable in the allowed resource bound; a single target solver then decides the source.
13. For vertex cover, list at most k vertices and verify every edge has an endpoint listed in polynomial time.
14. Reduce known hard A to target B so a B solver would solve A.
15. P and NP are time-bounded decidable classes, PSPACE is space-bounded, and undecidable problems have no total decider in the model.
16. A polynomial algorithm for any NP-complete language would put every NP language in P, implying P=NP.

Define a language, select the weakest adequate model, and prove either a construction or a lower-bound claim. Your answer must state quantifiers, encoding, termination, and the exact theorem being used.

**Exercise.** Include one tempting but invalid proof and diagnose its missing implication.

## Extended grading guidance

For questions 1–4, a transition table with unexplained arrows is incomplete: attach a state invariant and prove it by induction on the prefix. For questions 5–8, distinguish generated strings from accepted strings and state whether the PDA is deterministic. For questions 9–12, mark where total termination is used. For questions 13–16, include the input encoding and certificate size.

Common deductions are: using one pumping decomposition instead of an arbitrary one; calling a recognizer a decider; reducing a target to a known hard source; claiming NP membership from a slow program; and treating a benchmark as an asymptotic lower bound. Replace each informal claim with a definition, invariant, or quantified implication.

An excellent answer also gives a smallest sanity-check instance. For a DFA, test the empty word and one-symbol words. For a grammar, test the shortest derivation and the first ambiguous-looking word. For a reduction, test k=0 and a one-element instance. Tiny examples do not prove the theorem, but they reveal whether the formal object matches the intended problem.

## Model answers and partial credit

For a construction question, full credit requires a declared invariant, a complete transition or production specification, and a two-direction correctness argument. A correct diagram with no state meaning earns only construction credit. For a lower-bound question, a single failed example earns no general contradiction unless it defeats every candidate decomposition or machine. For a reduction, the source-to-target direction, computability, output-size bound, completeness, and soundness are separate checkpoints.

When reviewing your own answer, underline every “therefore.” Ask whether the preceding statement is a definition, an induction consequence, a closure theorem, or an unstated intuition. Replace “obviously” with a case split when a transition can cross a boundary. If using a known theorem, state its hypotheses and explain why the current language or machine satisfies them.

## Additional synthesis prompts

1. Give a language that is regular and context-free, and explain why the stronger model is unnecessary.
2. Give a context-free language that is not regular and identify the memory relation requiring a stack.
3. State a recognizable but undecidable language and explain the semidecision behavior.
4. Explain why NP membership and NP-hardness must be proved independently.
5. Describe one empirical observation that cannot establish a complexity-class separation.

### Diagnostic solutions


1. The language of strings with an even number of 1s is regular via a two-state DFA and therefore context-free because every regular language is context-free.
2. The language ()a^n b^n() is context-free with a PDA that pushes a markers and pops on b, but it is not regular by pumping or distinguishability.
3. HALT is recognizable by simulation and accepting when the simulation halts, but no decider can halt correctly on every input.
4. Membership gives a verifier for the target; hardness gives a reduction from a known hard source. Neither implication supplies the other proof.
5. A slow run on one large input depends on encoding, implementation, hardware, and instance structure; it cannot prove that every algorithm requires superpolynomial time.



The assessment is passed at the level of a full course only when definitions, constructions, proofs, and limits are all present. Memorized labels such as “regular,” “NP,” or “undecidable” are not substitutes for the argument that establishes the label.

Use the references to check definitions and standard theorem statements, but make the submitted proof self-contained.
