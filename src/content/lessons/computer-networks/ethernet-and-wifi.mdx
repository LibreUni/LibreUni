# Ethernet and Wi-Fi

Local-area networks solve a different problem from the Internet layer: several nearby devices need to share a medium and deliver frames across one broadcast domain or a switched fabric. Ethernet and Wi-Fi expose similar network-layer service boundaries but have different assumptions about contention, mobility, interference, and error. Understanding the link layer requires following a frame from creation to transmission, switching, reception, and possible discard.

## Ethernet frames and identities

An Ethernet frame has destination and source MAC addresses, a type or length field, payload, and a frame check sequence. A MAC address identifies a link-layer interface in the local forwarding domain; it is not a globally sufficient host identity and is not a security credential. Broadcast and multicast destinations allow one sender to reach multiple receivers, while unicast switching can limit delivery to a selected port.

The frame check sequence is normally a CRC chosen to detect common transmission errors. A switch does not need to understand the network-layer payload to forward a frame. It learns source addresses from ingress frames and maps them to ports. On a destination hit, it forwards to the learned port; on an unknown unicast or broadcast, it floods within the permitted broadcast domain. A learned mapping ages out because devices move, links change, and addresses can be reused.

**Worked learning trace.** A switch starts with an empty table. A sends to B on port 1; the switch learns A→1 and floods because B is unknown. B replies on port 3; the switch learns B→3 and forwards to port 1. A later sends to B and the switch forwards only to port 3. If B moves to port 4, its next frame updates B→4. During the interval, frames may be flooded or sent to the old port; the protocol’s learning is eventual, not instantaneous.

## Collision domains and switching

Old shared Ethernet used carrier sense and collision detection. A station transmitted after sensing an idle medium and detected a collision while transmitting. Binary exponential backoff selected a random slot after collision, reducing repeated synchronization. Modern switched full-duplex Ethernet usually removes collisions on each point-to-point link, but the historical algorithm illustrates a general principle: when multiple senders share uncertain capacity, randomized waiting can stabilize access.

A switch separates collision domains but does not automatically separate broadcast domains. VLANs create logical Layer-2 domains over shared switching infrastructure. A tagged trunk can carry several VLANs between switches; an access port normally presents one untagged VLAN to an endpoint. Routing is required between VLANs. Misconfigured native VLANs, trunks, or allowed VLAN lists can cause isolation failures, blackholes, or unintended broadcast propagation.

Spanning Tree Protocol prevents Layer-2 loops by selecting a loop-free tree when redundant links exist. Redundancy is valuable, but a broadcast frame circulating in a loop can multiply without a hop limit. The tree may temporarily reconverge after a link change, and traffic can be interrupted during that transition. Modern designs often use routed links or controlled fabrics to reduce the size and impact of Layer-2 domains.

## Wi-Fi is a shared, variable medium

Wi-Fi stations cannot generally detect collisions while transmitting, so they use carrier sense, random backoff, acknowledgments, and retransmission. A station that senses a busy channel waits; after the channel is idle for the required interval, it chooses a contention-window slot. A successful receiver acknowledges the frame, while missing acknowledgment suggests collision, interference, or loss.

The hidden-terminal problem occurs when A and C cannot hear each other but both can reach B. Each senses an idle channel and transmits, colliding at B. RTS/CTS can reserve the medium: A requests, B clears the request, and A sends. This adds overhead and is useful for selected large or hidden-terminal-sensitive traffic, but it cannot eliminate all interference and may be disabled or ineffective in dense deployments.

Wireless capacity is not a fixed property of the access point. Rate adaptation changes modulation and coding based on observed reception. Distance, obstacles, contention, channel width, neighboring networks, and client power affect throughput. A client may show strong signal strength yet have poor throughput because interference or airtime contention dominates. Aggregate capacity must be divided among stations, and a low-rate station can consume disproportionate airtime.

## Association and mobility

A Wi-Fi station scans, authenticates, associates, obtains link parameters, and typically acquires network-layer configuration. Association does not itself guarantee Internet reachability. The access point must bridge or route traffic correctly; DHCP, neighbor discovery, DNS, and policy still matter. Roaming requires coordination between the client, access points, and authentication infrastructure, and a brief interruption may occur while state moves.

The radio identifier shown to a network observer can be randomized or changed. Applications should not treat MAC addresses as stable user identities. Security protocols bind access to cryptographic credentials and protected key exchange, not merely possession of an address. An open network can provide connectivity without providing confidentiality or peer authentication.

## Failure diagnosis

If a host cannot reach a neighbor, inspect link negotiation, VLAN membership, MAC learning, ARP/ND, and local firewall behavior. If one VLAN works but another fails, examine trunk tags and allowed lists. If wired traffic works but Wi-Fi traffic is slow, measure airtime, retries, channel utilization, rate, and client location rather than looking only at RSSI. If broadcast works but unicast fails, stale or incorrect switch learning is a candidate; if both fail, suspect domain configuration or physical reachability.

Packet captures have layer boundaries. A capture on a host may omit radio management frames and may show frames after driver processing. A monitor-mode capture reveals more Wi-Fi control and management traffic but introduces a different vantage point and can miss frames due to capture limitations. Correlate retry counters and acknowledgments with application observations.

## Airtime is the scarce wireless resource

For a frame of (L) bits sent at physical rate (R), the data-transmission time is approximately (L/R), before preambles, interframe spacing, contention, acknowledgments, and retransmissions. Two clients with different rates can therefore consume very different fractions of the same channel for equal-sized frames. A client at 6 Mb/s may occupy the medium for many times longer than a client at 54 Mb/s. “The access point supports 1 Gb/s” describes a modulation capability, not the goodput available to every station.

Channel planning must account for overlap and co-channel contention. Moving an access point to a channel with less interference can help, but neighboring cells on the same channel still share airtime rather than becoming independent capacity. Increasing channel width may improve a clean environment and make a crowded one worse by overlapping more interferers. Measurements should therefore include channel occupancy, retry rate, negotiated rates, and per-station airtime.

## Exercises

1. Trace a three-frame MAC-learning sequence and state the switch table after each frame.
2. Explain why a switch reduces collisions but does not by itself prevent a broadcast storm.
3. Construct a hidden-terminal scenario and explain when RTS/CTS helps and what it costs.
4. A Wi-Fi client has strong received signal, low throughput, and high retry count. Give three hypotheses and one measurement for each.
5. Explain why a MAC address is unsuitable as an authentication credential or permanent application identity.
6. A VLAN is reachable on one switch but not across a trunk. Give a layered troubleshooting order.

<summary>Diagnostic solutions</summary>

1. On A→B ingress port 1, learn A→1 and flood unknown B. On B→A ingress port 3, learn B→3 and forward to A’s port. On A→B again, the table contains A→1 and B→3, so the frame is unicast to port 3.
2. Broadcast frames are flooded to every port in a broadcast domain. A loop permits a broadcast to return and be replicated repeatedly. Collision isolation is not loop prevention; spanning tree or a different topology is required.
3. A and C are outside one another’s carrier-sense range but both reach B. Both transmit and collide at B. RTS/CTS lets B reserve airtime that C can hear, reducing the collision risk for the subsequent data frame; the request/clear exchange adds overhead and can itself collide.
4. Interference: inspect channel utilization and neighboring networks. Marginal rate selection or multipath: inspect negotiated modulation/rate and retry reasons. Airtime contention or a low-rate station: inspect per-station airtime and queueing. Signal strength alone cannot distinguish these.
5. Addresses can be observed, spoofed, randomized, changed by virtualization, and rewritten by bridges or NAT-like mechanisms. Authentication needs proof of possession of a secret and an agreed identity-binding protocol; application identity should survive interface changes.
6. Check physical link and tagging on both ends; verify access versus trunk mode and native VLAN assumptions; compare allowed VLAN lists; inspect MAC learning and spanning-tree state; then test gateway routing, ACLs, and MTU. Start at the first boundary where the frame disappears.
