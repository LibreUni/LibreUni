# Networks Capstone: Design, Measure, and Defend a Service

The capstone asks you to design a networked service and defend its behavior under normal load, loss, delay, failure, and attack. The deliverable is not a diagram alone. It is a set of assumptions, protocol specifications, calculations, experiments, invariants, and failure procedures that another engineer could review and implement. Every design choice should answer a concrete workload or threat.

## Project brief

Design a globally reachable document service with three regions. Clients resolve a name, establish a secure connection, read documents, and submit versioned updates. Reads should normally complete within 150 ms at the 95th percentile for a nearby region. Updates may be retried by clients and must not be applied twice. The service must tolerate one regional link failure and continue serving already-replicated documents, although updates may become temporarily unavailable if their consistency requirements cannot be met.

You may choose protocols and topology, but state them precisely. Provide an IPv4/IPv6 addressing plan, routing domains, VLAN or link-layer boundaries, MTUs, DNS behavior, transport choice, secure-channel configuration, application framing, replication semantics, observability, and rollout process. If you choose an existing protocol, identify which guarantees you rely on and which remain the application’s responsibility.

## Deliverable A: requirements and threat model

Separate functional requirements from nonfunctional requirements. Functional requirements include reading a named version, submitting an update, returning a conflict, and reporting unknown outcome after an ambiguous write. Nonfunctional requirements include latency, availability, confidentiality, integrity, authorization, replay resistance, capacity, and recovery time. Give measurable thresholds rather than adjectives such as “fast” or “secure.”

Define principals: readers, writers, service instances, routing operators, identity authority, and storage operators. Define an attacker who can observe and alter Internet traffic but cannot extract protected private keys. Consider compromised clients, replayed update requests, DNS manipulation, route leaks, denial of service, stale replicas, and operator mistakes. State exclusions, such as a fully compromised service region or a compromised root trust anchor, and explain what follows if an exclusion occurs.

## Deliverable B: topology and addressing

Draw the path from client interface to access network, edge router, load balancer, service tier, and storage. Mark Layer-2 broadcast domains and Layer-3 boundaries. Use hierarchical prefixes so aggregation is possible; do not assign a different arbitrary subnet to every host without explaining routing. Include management and data-plane separation, failure domains, and the links that are truly independent.

Specify MTU along each path and how the system behaves when a packet-too-large signal is missing or filtered. Identify whether traffic is asymmetric and whether stateful firewalls, NAT, or source validation depend on symmetry. Explain address-family selection and fallback if IPv6 and IPv4 have different reachability.

## Deliverable C: routing and convergence

Choose an interior protocol and an inter-region or provider policy. Define route origins, preferred paths, export restrictions, prefix filters, next-hop resolution, and maximum-prefix protection. Show the selected route for a client in each region. Analyze one edge-link failure, one stale withdrawal, and one accidental more-specific announcement.

Compute a small forwarding example manually using longest-prefix matching and Dijkstra or a stated path-vector policy. Define invariants: no route is exported to an unauthorized neighbor; every installed next hop resolves; critical forwarding graphs contain no cycle; a withdrawn service prefix is not retained past a bounded timer. Explain how control-plane convergence and FIB programming are observed separately.

## Deliverable D: transport and application protocol

Choose TCP, QUIC, or a justified UDP-based protocol. Explain connection establishment, flow control, congestion control, retransmission, stream or message boundaries, and behavior under reordering and loss. Calculate a window requirement using the target bandwidth-delay product. Define request IDs, deadlines, retry budgets, duplicate handling, and server behavior when a response is lost after a write commits.

Specify an application grammar with bounded lengths, canonical encoding, explicit version, error classes, and status query for unknown outcomes. Prove or argue the safety property “one update ID causes at most one state transition.” State the liveness assumptions for eventual reads and updates. Explain how backpressure prevents a slow replica or client from causing unbounded memory growth.

## Deliverable E: security architecture

Use authenticated encryption with a certificate or key binding appropriate to your principals. Explain certificate-chain and hostname/service validation, key rotation, trust-anchor deployment, session expiration, and recovery after key compromise. Bind authorization to the authenticated application identity, not a source address or port. Add replay defense and distinguish channel sequence protection from application operation IDs.

Analyze at least two attacks in detail: an active man-in-the-middle attempt, a replayed update, a forged route or DNS answer, and a resource-exhaustion attack. For each, state the attack trace, violated desired property, defense, residual assumption, and detection signal. Do not claim that encryption solves authorization or availability.

## Deliverable F: measurement and experiments

Design experiments for baseline latency, tail latency, throughput, packet loss, route convergence, TLS failure, and write ambiguity. Record vantage point, sample size, payload, address family, protocol, timestamps, concurrent load, and failure definition. Separate median from tail and goodput from link rate. Use randomized probes or workload order where periodic sampling could alias a failure.

For each experiment, list at least two competing hypotheses and the observation that would discriminate them. For example, a slow first request may be DNS, connection setup, handshake, server queueing, or a cold cache. A two-point capture can distinguish client-path loss from server processing delay more strongly than a single host trace.

## Deliverable G: failure exercise

Analyze this incident: after a policy rollout, 30% of clients receive connection timeouts; internal clients work; DNS answers are unchanged; one edge router advertises a more-specific prefix; the other edge has a valid less-specific route; packet captures show no SYN at the service edge for affected clients; rollback restores traffic in 45 seconds. Produce a timeline, forwarding graph, likely mechanism, tests, immediate repair, and preventive control. Discuss what evidence would falsify your explanation.

## Evaluation rubric

* 20% correctness of requirements, assumptions, addressing, and topology.
* 20% routing, prefix policy, convergence analysis, and manual calculation.
* 20% transport/application state machine, framing, retries, and safety proof.
* 15% threat model, authenticated identity, replay defense, and attack analysis.
* 15% measurement design, uncertainty, and causal reasoning.
* 10% failure response, rollout safety, clarity, and reproducibility.

An excellent submission does not hide uncertainty. It identifies where the design depends on an external authority, where a measurement cannot distinguish causes, and what additional observation would resolve the ambiguity. It also demonstrates that the system behaves safely during partial failure, not only when every component is healthy.

## Diagnostic project guidance

If your topology has two links that share one switch or power domain, count them as link redundancy but not as switch-failure redundancy. If your transport uses UDP, you must specify congestion behavior and reliability rather than assuming the network will provide them. If your secure channel validates a certificate chain but not the service name, the peer identity claim is incomplete. If your write retry lacks an operation ID, the at-most-once claim is false after response loss.

When a route appears correct but packets do not arrive, inspect the FIB and recursive next hop, then capture at both sides of the suspected boundary. When a client reports slow service, break the timing into DNS, connection, security, server, and transfer components. When a route or certificate change causes only partial failure, look for scope: cached records, address family, more-specific prefixes, region, or client policy. These diagnostic patterns are part of the assessed competence.

Submit raw calculations, not only final numbers. State whether a result is an upper bound, estimate, or measured value, and include the units. A reviewer should be able to reproduce the routing choice, window calculation, latency decomposition, and security claim from the report without guessing which assumptions were omitted.

Include the failure test that would change your conclusion.

That is how a design becomes reviewable rather than aspirational.
