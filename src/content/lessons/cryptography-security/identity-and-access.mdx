# Identity and Access

## Identity model

An identity system links a subject to an identifier, authenticators, attributes, sessions, and permissions. The subject may be a human, service, device, workload, or delegated actor. Authentication establishes confidence that a claimant controls an authenticator associated with the subject. Authorization evaluates whether that subject may perform an action on a resource in a context. Federation adds an issuer that makes an assertion consumed by a relying party; the relying party remains responsible for validating audience, issuer, time, signature, and policy.

Do not treat an identifier such as an email address as proof of identity. An identifier may be recycled, shared, pseudonymous, or changed. Identity proofing, authentication assurance, and authorization scope are separate dimensions. NIST’s digital identity model distinguishes assurance levels for proofing, authentication, and federation; an application should select them from risk rather than applying one global label to every account.

## Authentication factors

Factors are commonly categorized as knowledge, possession, and inherence, but the security of a factor depends on how it is bound and recovered. A password is a memorized secret vulnerable to phishing and offline guessing. A one-time code delivered by a device can be intercepted or socially engineered. A cryptographic authenticator can provide phishing resistance when it binds the origin or relying party. Biometrics are usually local activation factors, not replaceable secrets; compromise and privacy consequences differ.

Multi-factor authentication requires independent factors. Two passwords are not two factors, and a password plus a code delivered through the same compromised account may not provide meaningful independence. Enrollment, replacement, backup codes, and recovery can reduce the effective assurance to the weakest path. Require reauthentication and user notification for adding or changing authenticators.

## Sessions and tokens

A session token is a bearer credential: possession is sufficient unless the protocol adds binding. Generate it with a cryptographically secure random source, use sufficient entropy, transmit only over protected channels, and set expiration, rotation, revocation, and inactivity policy. Cookies should use Secure, HttpOnly, and appropriate SameSite attributes; browser storage and URL parameters have different leakage modes. A token’s audience, issuer, scope, and time claims must be validated server-side.

Access tokens should be short-lived and least-privileged. Refresh tokens require storage and rotation controls, reuse detection, and revocation behavior. JWT-like formats are not secure merely because they are signed: reject algorithm confusion, enforce an allowlist, validate the key and issuer, and bind claims to the service and operation. Do not accept a token minted for one audience at another service.

## Authorization models

Discretionary access control assigns permissions to subjects or groups and may allow delegation. Role-based access control assigns permissions to roles, but role explosion and stale membership can make policy difficult to audit. Attribute-based access control evaluates subject, resource, action, and environmental attributes such as tenant, time, device posture, or risk. Capability systems treat an unforgeable token as authority to a resource and operation; capability leakage then becomes a primary threat.

Authorization should be deny-by-default and fail closed for sensitive operations. Check on the server at the point of use, not only in a user interface or initial route. Enforce object-level authorization on every identifier, including bulk endpoints, exports, search filters, asynchronous jobs, and alternate content representations. Avoid trusting client-supplied roles, hidden fields, or decoded but unverified token claims.

## Delegation and federation

Delegation allows one principal to act for another within a constrained scope. The token or assertion should identify the actor, subject, issuer, audience, action, resource, time interval, and delegation chain. A service should not forward a broad user token to every downstream dependency when a narrow service credential would suffice. Confused-deputy attacks occur when a privileged service uses its authority on behalf of an untrusted caller without checking that caller’s intended resource.

Federated assertions must be protected against replay, substitution, audience confusion, and algorithm downgrade. Validate issuer trust, signature, certificate or key rotation, not-before and expiry, nonce or correlation state, and the exact redirect or callback context. Store only the attributes needed for the application; identity data creates privacy and breach impact.

## Account lifecycle

Provisioning, suspension, deletion, rename, merger, and recovery are security operations. A deleted account’s identifiers, sessions, API keys, delegated permissions, and background jobs need explicit handling. Avoid immediate identifier reuse if old links or audit records could be confused. Offboarding a workforce identity should revoke active sessions and credentials, remove group memberships, and transfer or quarantine owned resources.

Audit logs should record actor, subject, action, resource, policy decision, request correlation, and source context without logging secrets. Log both successful and denied high-risk actions. Make logs tamper-evident and restrict who can read them; a log accessible to the same compromised service may be altered or exfiltrated.

## Worked authorization example

A note service receives GET /notes/123 from a session token. The server validates token signature, issuer, audience, expiry, and session status. It maps the subject to a current account and queries the note with an owner-or-active-share predicate in the same authorization boundary. It does not first fetch the note by ID and then rely on a client-provided owner field. A revoked share must fail even if a cached permission or old capability remains; cache keys therefore include subject, resource, policy version, and expiry, with invalidation or short TTL.

An export endpoint must repeat the check for every object rather than assuming that permission to list implies permission to read contents. An asynchronous export job stores a server-side authorization snapshot or reevaluates policy at execution time according to a documented consistency rule. The audit record binds the actor, requested resource set, policy decision, and job identifier.

## Verification and abuse testing

Build tests from the policy, not only from successful user journeys. Attempt every object identifier as a different tenant, change roles during a session, replay a consumed token, alter audience or issuer, use an expired assertion, submit duplicate claims, and invoke bulk or asynchronous variants of an endpoint. Test cache invalidation after revocation and account deletion. A denial must be safe and nonrevealing, while logs should preserve enough context to investigate.

Review identity systems as state machines: registered, verified, active, suspended, recovery-pending, revoked, and deleted. Define allowed transitions, required factors, notification, and rollback behavior. A token issued in one state must not silently remain authoritative after a transition that should revoke it.

Authentication establishes an identity claim; authorization evaluates a policy over subject, action, resource, and context. Least privilege minimizes the consequence of credential misuse. Role-based access is compact, while attribute- or relationship-based rules can express richer context at policy complexity cost.

An audit record should identify actor, action, target, decision, and time while avoiding sensitive payloads. Logs are evidence only if their integrity and retention are designed.
