# Modular Arithmetic

$a\equiv b\pmod n$ means $n$ divides $a-b$. Addition and multiplication preserve congruence. A multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $n$ exists exactly when $\gcd(a,n)=1$.

The extended Euclidean algorithm finds coefficients $x,y$ with $ax+ny=\gcd(a,n)$; when the gcd is one, $x$ is an inverse modulo $n$. Fast exponentiation reduces repeated multiplication from linear to logarithmic multiplications.

## Congruence as a quotient structure

Congruence modulo $n$ partitions the integers into residue classes. The class of $a$ is the set of integers $a+kn$. Addition and multiplication are well defined on classes because replacing $a$ by $a+rn$ and $b$ by $b+sn$ changes $a+b$ and $ab$ by multiples of $n$. The resulting ring is written $mathbb{Z}/nmathbb{Z}$. When $n$ is prime, every nonzero class has a multiplicative inverse and the ring is a field; when $n$ is composite, zero divisors may exist.

For example, modulo $15$, $3$ is not invertible because $gcd(3,15)=3$, and $3\cdot 5\equiv 0\pmod{15}$. In contrast, $4$ has inverse $4$ because $4\cdot 4=16\equiv 1\pmod{15}$. The existence theorem follows from Bézout: $ax+ny=\gcd(a,n)$. A coefficient $x$ gives an inverse exactly when the gcd is $1$. The converse follows because an inverse implies $ax\equiv 1\pmod n$, so any common divisor of $a$ and $n$ divides $1$.

## Extended Euclidean algorithm

The Euclidean algorithm repeatedly replaces $(a,b)$ by $(b,a\bmod b)$. The gcd invariant is $\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,a\bmod b)$, since common divisors of $b$ and $a$ are exactly common divisors of $b$ and $a-qb$. The algorithm terminates because the second argument strictly decreases among nonnegative integers. The extended version maintains coefficients $s,t$ with the current remainder $r=sa+tb$. Back-substitution or iterative coefficient updates produce Bézout coefficients.

For $17$ and $43$: $43=2\cdot17+9$; $17=1\cdot9+8$; $9=1\cdot8+1$. Back-substitution gives $1=9-8=9-(17-9)=2(43-2(17))-17=2(43)-5(17)$. Thus $-5$ is the inverse of $17$ modulo $43$, or $38$ in the standard residue range. Verification is $17\cdot38=646$, and $646\bmod 43=1$.

## Exponentiation and group order

Binary exponentiation uses $a^{2k}=(a^k)^2$ and $a^{2k+1}=a(a^k)^2$. Process exponent bits from most significant to least significant: square the accumulator each step and multiply by $a$ when the bit is $1$. The loop performs $O(\log e)$ modular multiplications and keeps intermediate values reduced. The proof is an induction on the processed binary prefix.

For a prime $p$, Fermat’s little theorem says $a^{p-1}\equiv 1\pmod p$ for $a$ not divisible by $p$. The nonzero residues form a group of order $p-1$, and Lagrange’s theorem gives the result. Euler’s theorem gives $a^{\varphi(n)}\equiv 1\pmod n$ when $\gcd(a,n)=1$. Using a theorem without checking coprimality is a common cryptographic error.

The order of $a$ in a multiplicative group is the least positive $d$ with $a^d=1$. Exponentiation cycles modulo this order. In Diffie–Hellman, subgroup order affects security: a generator of a small subgroup permits an attacker to learn a secret exponent modulo a small number. Protocols therefore select groups and validate public elements according to a documented subgroup model.

## Chinese remainder theorem

If $m$ and $n$ are coprime, every pair of residues $(a\bmod m,b\bmod n)$ corresponds to exactly one residue modulo $mn$. Find $u$ and $v$ with $um+vn=1$. Then $x=b(um)+a(vn)$ has $x\equiv a\pmod m$ and $x\equiv b\pmod n$. Uniqueness follows because two solutions differ by a multiple of both coprime moduli and therefore by $mn$.

CRT is useful for arithmetic and RSA implementations because a computation modulo $pq$ can be split into computations modulo $p$ and $q$. It also creates a fault-analysis hazard: if one residue is faulty, a gcd of the recombination difference with the public modulus can reveal a factor. CRT is an optimization with a new attack surface, not a free speedup.

## Modular equations

The linear congruence $ax\equiv b\pmod n$ has a solution exactly when $\gcd(a,n)\mid b$. If $d\mid a$ and $d\mid n$, then $d\mid ax$ and hence $d\mid b$. Conversely, divide by $d$ to obtain a coprime coefficient modulo $n/d$ and use its inverse. There are $d$ distinct solutions modulo $n$ when a solution exists.

For example, $6x\equiv 9\pmod{15}$ has $d=3$, and $3$ divides $9$. Divide by $3$ to get $2x\equiv 3\pmod 5$; the inverse of $2$ is $3$, so $x\equiv 4\pmod 5$. The three solutions modulo $15$ are $4,9,14$. Treating $6$ as invertible modulo $15$ would incorrectly report one answer.

## Cryptographic relevance

Modular arithmetic is the algebraic substrate for RSA, Diffie–Hellman, elliptic-curve groups, and many signatures. The algebra alone does not provide security. RSA relies on a stated hardness assumption and secure padding; Diffie–Hellman relies on a discrete-log assumption in a selected group; signatures require unforgeability under adaptive message queries. A correct exponentiation routine can still be insecure through poor randomness, small subgroups, padding mistakes, key reuse, or side channels.

Implementation rules include canonical residue encoding, rejection of malformed public values, constant-time handling of secret-dependent branches where required, and tests against independent big-integer references. Never use floating point for modular cryptography. Bound intermediate products and define negative-remainder behavior.

## Further worked arithmetic

### RSA as a modular proof

Choose distinct primes $p$ and $q$, set $N=pq$ and $\varphi(N)=(p-1)(q-1)$, and choose $e$ with $\gcd(e,\varphi(N))=1$. Compute $d$ as the inverse of $e$ modulo $\varphi(N)$. For a message representative $m$ coprime to $N$, encryption is $c\equiv m^e\pmod N$ and decryption computes $c^d\equiv m^{ed}\pmod N$. Since $ed=1+k\varphi(N)$, Euler’s theorem gives $m^{ed}=m(m^{\varphi(N)})^k\equiv m\pmod N$. A complete RSA proof must handle messages not coprime to $N$ separately, usually with the Chinese remainder theorem and Fermat’s theorem modulo $p$ and $q$.

This correctness proof is not a security proof. Textbook RSA is deterministic and malleable; equal messages produce equal ciphertexts and multiplication of ciphertexts corresponds to multiplication of plaintext representatives. Modern RSA encryption uses randomized OAEP-style encoding, while signatures use a distinct encoding and verification theorem. Never use one encoding for both purposes merely because both operations involve modular exponentiation.

### Prime fields and inverses

Over a prime field, division by a nonzero element means multiplication by its inverse. A polynomial or elliptic-curve implementation must distinguish the field identity 0 from an invalid point or absent value. In an elliptic-curve group, affine point addition uses a slope involving an inverse; if the denominator is zero, the result is the point at infinity or a special doubling case. Projective coordinates avoid many inversions by representing the same affine point with several coordinates and defer one inversion until serialization.

The group law must be closed and associative under the chosen representation. Cryptographic protocols rely on a subgroup of known order rather than merely on a curve equation. If a received point is not on the curve or lies in a small subgroup, scalar multiplication can leak information about a secret scalar. Validation and domain parameters are therefore part of the mathematical protocol specification.

### Side-channel arithmetic

The number of mathematical operations is not the same as the number of observable machine operations. A square-and-multiply loop with a branch on an exponent bit may reveal the bit through timing or power. A constant-pattern ladder performs a regular sequence for every bit, but the implementation must also avoid secret-indexed memory, variable-time reduction, and compiler transformations that reintroduce branches. Constant-time is a property of a complete implementation and platform, not a comment attached to an algorithm.

Use test vectors for algebraic correctness, differential tests against a trusted library for random values, and leakage-oriented tests for timing distributions. A test cannot prove absence of a side channel, but it can detect accidental secret-dependent branches and invalid encodings. Treat secret values as tainted data throughout the call graph.
