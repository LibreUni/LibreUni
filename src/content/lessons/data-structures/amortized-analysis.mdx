# Amortized Analysis

Worst-case analysis assigns a bound to every individual operation. That is necessary for real-time guarantees, but it can be pessimistic for structures where expensive operations create state that makes future operations cheap. Amortized analysis proves a bound on every finite operation sequence without assuming random inputs. It is a deterministic accounting of work across a sequence, not an average over a probability distribution.

## Aggregate method

Suppose any sequence of m operations has total actual cost at most T(m). The aggregate amortized cost is T(m)/m, or more usefully a bound on total work. Dynamic-array append illustrates the method: ordinary appends cost one, and resizing at capacities 1,2,4,... copies a geometric sum less than m. Thus m appends cost O(m), even though one append may cost O(m).

The aggregate method is straightforward when the total cost can be counted directly. It may obscure which operations pay for which work, and it can be difficult when several kinds of state interact. Still, it is a proof for every sequence: no random or average-case assumption is involved.

**Worked binary counter.** Incrementing a k-bit binary counter flips trailing 1s to 0 and one 0 to 1. One increment can flip k bits, but across m increments, bit i flips at most (m/2^i) times. Summing over bits gives at most (m+m/2+m/4+cdots less than 2m) flips, so increment is O(1) amortized. The representation’s carry pattern creates the savings.

## Accounting method

Assign each operation an amortized charge (hat c_i), possibly larger or smaller than actual cost c_i. Store surplus as credits on objects or in a conceptual bank. The proof requires that the bank never becomes negative and that (sum hat c_igesum c_i) for every prefix. If the bank is nonnegative after each operation, the charges pay all actual work.

For a binary counter, charge two units per increment: one pays for flipping the low 0 to 1, and one credit is placed on each bit that becomes 1. When that bit later flips from 1 to 0, its credit pays for the flip. A bit cannot be charged without first receiving credit. The constant charge proves total O(m).

For a dynamic array with doubling, charge a fixed amount per append. One unit writes the new element; credits associated with earlier elements pay for moving them during a resize. The exact charge depends on growth factor and whether shrink is included. A proof must show enough credit exists at every resize, not merely assert that “copies average out.”

## Potential method

Choose a nonnegative potential (Phi(D)) for data-structure state D. Define amortized cost (hat c_i=c_i+Phi(D_i)-Phi(D_()i-1())). Summing over a sequence telescopes:

For a dynamic array that doubles, a piecewise potential based on size and capacity is needed because after a resize the array is sparse and the potential should drop enough to pay for copying. One valid shape increases as the array fills and decreases sharply at a resize. Verify the non-resize append, resize append, and possible shrink cases separately.

## Queue by two stacks

The two-stack queue has input stack I and output stack O. Let (Phi=2|I|). Enqueue costs one push and increases potential by 2, so amortized cost is 3. A dequeue from nonempty O costs one pop and does not increase potential. If O is empty and I has k elements, transfer costs 2k operations and changes potential by -2k; the amortized cost is zero plus the pop constant. Every operation has constant amortized cost, and (Phi) is nonnegative.

The potential choice reflects stored work: elements in I have not yet paid for being moved to O. An arbitrary potential can yield a false or negative bound; check initial nonnegativity and every transition. Potential may also include multiple components, such as unused capacity and number of elements in a queue.

## Dynamic tables and hysteresis

A table that grows when full and shrinks when quarter-full can be analyzed with potential tied to load. The gap between thresholds prevents repeated resize on alternating updates. If growth and shrink thresholds meet, an append/remove pair around the boundary can force linear work repeatedly, producing linear amortized cost. The structure’s policy is part of the theorem.

Amortized analysis can cover deallocation and rebuilding. A hash table rehashes all live entries when load crosses a threshold; a garbage-compacting vector or tombstone cleanup similarly performs bulk work. Charge each update a fraction of future rebuild cost. The fraction must account for updates that add, remove, or leave the relevant state unchanged.

## Limits and deamortization

Amortized O(1) does not mean every operation is short. If latency spikes are unacceptable, deamortize the resize: maintain old and new arrays and migrate a bounded number of elements on each update. During migration, lookup searches both locations, and insertion chooses a defined destination. The invariant must state that every live element is in exactly one representation or is safely discoverable in both.

Amortized analysis also does not imply expected analysis. Expected time uses a probability space, such as random hash seeds or random priorities; amortized time quantifies over all operation sequences. A randomized data structure may have both an expected bound and an amortized bound, but the proofs answer different questions.

## Choosing a potential systematically

Start by identifying what future operation will consume stored work. For a stack of deferred items, potential can be proportional to the number of deferred items. For a dynamic table, potential can be proportional to distance from a resize threshold. For a queue represented by two stacks, only the input stack contains unpaid transfer work. Then calculate the potential change for every operation category rather than adjusting the formula after seeing one convenient trace.

The potential need not equal a literal physical resource, but it must be interpretable enough to constrain the proof. A large potential may make some operations appear cheap and others expensive; that is acceptable if it remains nonnegative and the initial/final terms are handled. If the data structure can be initialized with nonzero potential, include that initial cost explicitly. If an operation can discard stored work, account for the potential decrease as a credit rather than silently dropping it.

## Adversarial sequences

Amortized proofs are robust against an adaptive adversary that chooses the next operation after observing the structure’s state, as long as the proof quantifies over every valid sequence. This is why the binary-counter and dynamic-array arguments do not need random input. A workload generator should still test adversarial patterns—alternating boundary updates, repeated deletes, and bursts—to validate that the implementation follows the policy the proof assumed.

An operation sequence can have low average cost while violating a service-level latency requirement because one resize is still large. If tail latency matters, use incremental rebuilding, bounded work per call, or a real-time data structure with worst-case guarantees. The appropriate analysis depends on what the client promises to its users.
