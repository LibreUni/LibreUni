# Amortized Analysis

A dynamic array append costs $O(n)$ worst-case when the array resizes — every element must be copied to a new allocation. If a sequence of $n$ appends triggered $O(n)$ copies on each resize, the total cost would be $O(n^2)$. Yet experience shows that dynamic arrays handle millions of appends efficiently. The resolution: resizes happen at capacities $1, 2, 4, 8, \ldots$, and the geometric sum of copy costs across all resizes is $< 2n$. Total cost is $O(n)$, not $O(n^2)$. This is amortized analysis: bounding the average cost over a sequence without probability or distributional assumptions.

Robert Tarjan formalized amortized analysis in his 1985 paper "Amortized Computational Complexity" [Tarjan 1985], introducing the potential-function method that unified the aggregate, accounting, and potential perspectives. The core insight: rather than bounding each operation in isolation, define a **potential** $\Phi$ on the data structure's state. An expensive operation that simplifies the state pays down potential; a cheap operation may increase it. The amortized cost of an operation is its actual cost plus the change in potential. Summing over a sequence telescopes, and if $\Phi$ is nonnegative, the total actual cost is bounded by the total amortized cost.

All three methods produce the same bound when applied correctly. They differ only in how they distribute the cost.

<MathStatement id="amortized-def" kind="definition" title="Amortized cost (potential method)">
Let $S_0, S_1, \ldots, S_m$ be the states of a data structure after each operation in a sequence of $m$ operations. Let $c_i$ be the actual cost of operation $i$. Given a potential function $\Phi: \text{State} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$, the amortized cost of operation $i$ is

$$\hat{c}_i = c_i + \Phi(S_i) - \Phi(S_{i-1}).$$

If $\Phi(S_0) = 0$ and $\Phi(S_i) \ge 0$ for all $i$, then for any sequence

$$\sum_{i=1}^m c_i \le \sum_{i=1}^m \hat{c}_i.$$
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="telescoping-proof" for="amortized-def" collapsible>
Summing the definition telescopes:

$$\sum_{i=1}^m \hat{c}_i = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i + \sum_{i=1}^m (\Phi(S_i) - \Phi(S_{i-1})) = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i + \Phi(S_m) - \Phi(S_0).$$

Rearranging and applying $\Phi(S_m) \ge 0$, $\Phi(S_0) = 0$:

$$\sum_{i=1}^m c_i = \sum_{i=1}^m \hat{c}_i - \Phi(S_m) + \Phi(S_0) \le \sum_{i=1}^m \hat{c}_i.$$

The final potential $\Phi(S_m)$ is a slack term that makes the bound conservative. A negative potential would let the inequality reverse — the bound would claim credit that does not exist.
</MathProof>

## Aggregate method

Directly compute the total cost of any sequence of $m$ operations. If $T(m)$ is a tight upper bound, the amortized cost per operation is $T(m)/m$.

**Worked example: binary counter.** A $k$-bit binary counter stores an integer. Increment flips trailing $1$s to $0$ and one $0$ to $1$. A single increment can flip $k$ bits (e.g., $0111 \to 1000$). Across $m$ increments, bit $i$ (the $2^i$ place) flips at most $\lfloor m / 2^i \rfloor$ times — it flips every $2^i$ increments. Total bit flips:

$$\sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \left\lfloor \frac{m}{2^i} \right\rfloor < m \sum_{i=0}^\infty \frac{1}{2^i} = 2m.$$

The total cost of $m$ increments is $O(m)$, so amortized $O(1)$ per increment.

## Accounting method

Assign each operation an amortized charge $\hat{c}_i$ that may exceed its actual cost. Store the surplus as **credits** attached to specific objects or in a conceptual bank. The invariant: the bank balance never drops below zero. The sum of charges for any prefix must cover the sum of actual costs.

**Worked example: binary counter by accounting.** Charge **two** units per increment. The first unit pays for flipping the lowest $0$ to $1$. The second unit is deposited as a credit on that newly-set $1$ bit. When that bit later flips back to $0$ during a carry, its credit pays for the flip. Every bit flip is paid for: the initial $0\to1$ by the charge, the later $1\to0$ by the stored credit. Each increment charges two units, and the bank never goes negative because no bit can be cleared without having been set (and credited) earlier. Total charge: $2m$.

**Worked example: dynamic array by accounting.** Charge **three** units per append. One pays for writing the new element at the end. Two credits are placed on the newly written element. When the array doubles, copying that element to the new array costs one credit; the second credit pays for copying the element in the *next* resize. Each element is moved at most twice (once to the first new array, once to the second). The bank never goes negative: at a resize from capacity $k$ to $2k$, each of the $k$ elements carries two credits, totalling $2k$, which covers copying all $k$ elements. After the resize, no credits remain on the moved elements — consistent with the claim that each element pays for its own next move upon insertion.

## Potential method

Define a nonnegative potential function $\Phi$ on the data structure's state. The amortized cost is $\hat{c}_i = c_i + \Delta\Phi_i$. The potential acts as stored "state energy" — it encodes prepaid future work without attaching named credits to individual objects.

**Worked example: dynamic array.** Let $\Phi = 2 \cdot \text{size} - \text{capacity}$ when $\text{size} \ge \text{capacity}/2$, and $0$ otherwise (i.e., when the array is less than half full). Verify cases:

- **Append without resize:** actual cost $1$, $\text{size}$ increases by $1$, $\Delta\Phi = 2$. Amortized cost $= 1 + 2 = 3$.
- **Append triggering resize (capacity $k \to 2k$):** actual cost $k + 1$ (copy $k$ elements + write new element). Before: $\Phi = 2k - k = k$. After: $\text{size} = k+1$, $\text{capacity} = 2k$, $\Phi = 2(k+1) - 2k = 2$. $\Delta\Phi = 2 - k = -(k-2)$. Amortized cost $= (k+1) - (k-2) = 3$.
- **Non-resize append in sparse region ($\text{size} < \text{capacity}/2$):** $\Phi = 0$, no potential change, amortized $= 1$.

Every append has amortized cost at most $3$, and $\Phi$ is always nonnegative. The telescoping sum then bounds the total actual cost by $3m$.

**Worked example: two-stack queue.** A queue implemented as input stack $I$ and output stack $O$. Enqueue pushes to $I$. Dequeue: if $O$ is nonempty, pop $O$; otherwise, pop all elements from $I$ and push them onto $O$ (reversing order), then pop $O$.

Let $\Phi = 2|I|$. 

- **Enqueue:** actual cost $1$, $\Delta\Phi = +2$, amortized $= 3$.
- **Dequeue from nonempty $O$:** actual cost $1$, $\Delta\Phi = 0$, amortized $= 1$.
- **Dequeue emptying $I$ ($|I| = k$):** transfer costs $2k$ ($k$ pops + $k$ pushes), then one pop. $\Delta\Phi = -2k$. Amortized $= (2k+1) - 2k = 1$.

Every operation has $O(1)$ amortized cost because each element is transferred from $I$ to $O$ at most once.

## Counterexample: thrashing when hysteresis is absent

<StructureDiagram kind="array" title="Hysteresis gap prevents thrashing" caption="Growth at full capacity and shrink below quarter-capacity create a gap. Without this gap, alternating insert/delete at the boundary causes $O(n)$ amortized cost." variant="capacity" />

A dynamic table that grows at full capacity and shrinks at half capacity (no hysteresis) can be forced into thrashing. Consider: insert $n/2 + 1$ elements to trigger growth to capacity $n$, then delete one element (size falls to $n/2$, triggering shrink back to $n/2$), then insert one (grow again). Each insert/delete pair in this alternating regime costs $O(n)$ copying. The amortized cost over a long enough alternating sequence becomes $O(n)$ per operation — worse than the intended $O(1)$.

The fix: grow at full capacity and shrink when load drops below $1/4$. After a shrink to capacity $c$, the size is at least $c/2$, so it takes $\Omega(c)$ operations to reach the next threshold at either end. The hysteresis gap forces a linear number of cheap operations between expensive resizes.

This failure mode is not hypothetical. Implementations of `std::vector` in early C++ standards debated the shrink policy precisely because naive symmetric thresholds caused pathological performance on mixed workloads.

## Limits and deamortization

Amortized $O(1)$ does not bound individual operation cost. A single resize still takes $O(n)$ time. For real-time systems, interactive UI, or latency-sensitive services, this tail latency is unacceptable. **Deamortization** spreads the copying work across operations: maintain both old and new arrays, migrate a fixed number $b$ of elements per update, and adjust the lookup invariant to cover both locations. Each operation then does $O(b)$ work in the worst case, where $b$ can be chosen as a small constant independent of $n$.

Amortized analysis also does not imply expected analysis or average-case analysis. Expected-time bounds (e.g., hash-table lookup) rely on a probability space over random choices or inputs. Amortized bounds hold for every sequence deterministically. A data structure may have both — a randomized splay tree has expected logarithmic search and amortized logarithmic restructuring — but the proofs answer distinct questions.

## Choosing a potential systematically

Identify the future work that must be paid for by earlier operations. For a structure with deferred work (copying during resize, reversing during transfer), the potential should grow when deferred work accumulates and shrink when it is performed. General guidelines:

- **Deferred work proportional to state size** (e.g., copying all elements during resize): potential proportional to number of elements not yet accounted for.
- **Deferred work per element** (e.g., transferring from one stack to another): potential proportional to the size of the pending queue.
- **Convex potential** (e.g., $|2 \cdot \text{size} - \text{capacity}|$): ensures large potential drops exactly when a costly operation occurs.

The potential need not correspond to a physical quantity. It must be nonnegative, zero at the initial state, and decrease enough during expensive operations to offset their actual cost. A potential that grows too fast can make amortized costs diverge; a potential that never increases may fail to accumulate enough credit.

## Adversarial sequences

Amortized proofs are robust against an adaptive adversary that chooses the next operation after observing the structure's state, as long as the proof quantifies over every valid sequence. This is why the binary-counter and dynamic-array arguments require no randomness assumptions. An adversary can always construct the worst-case sequence — but the bound must still hold.

Testing should include adversarial patterns: alternating boundary updates, repeated deletes followed by inserts, and long bursts of the same operation. An implementation that follows the policy the proof assumed will pass; one with a subtly different policy (e.g., shrink at $1/2$ instead of $1/4$) may fail catastrophically.

### Recall

1. State the three methods of amortized analysis and the proof artifact each uses.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   Aggregate: total sequence cost bounded by a function of $m$, divided by $m$. Accounting: per-operation credits stored in a nonnegative bank. Potential: state-dependent function $\Phi$ whose telescoping difference bounds actual cost.
   </ExerciseSolution>

2. Explain why a potential must be nonnegative for the standard upper-bound proof.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   From $\sum c_i = \sum \hat{c}_i - \Phi(S_m) + \Phi(S_0)$ with $\Phi(S_0) = 0$, a negative $\Phi(S_m)$ would increase the right-hand side, making $\sum \hat{c}_i$ a lower bound rather than an upper bound on actual cost. The advertised bound would pay for work with nonexistent credit.
   </ExerciseSolution>

### Application

3. Analyze $m$ binary-counter increments by the aggregate method.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Count how many times each bit position flips across $m$ increments.">
   Bit $i$ flips at most $\lfloor m/2^i \rfloor$ times. Summing over $i = 0$ through $k-1$ gives $< 2m$ flips, so total cost is $O(m)$ and amortized cost is $O(1)$.
   </ExerciseSolution>

4. Give a credit scheme for dynamic-array doubling with a charge of 3 per append.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Each element must have enough credit to cover its copy in the next resize.">
   Charge 3 per append. One unit writes the element; two credits are stored on the element. At a resize from capacity $k$ to $2k$, each of $k$ elements has 2 credits ($2k$ total), covering the $k$ copies. After the resize, each element in the new array has 0 credits, and new appends replenish them. The bank never goes negative.
   </ExerciseSolution>

5. Show how threshold hysteresis avoids grow-shrink thrashing in dynamic tables.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   Grow at full capacity, shrink at quarter-capacity. After a shrink, size is at least half the new capacity; it takes $\Omega(\text{capacity})$ updates to reach either the full-capacity growth threshold or the quarter-capacity shrink threshold. The gap forces a linear number of cheap operations between expensive resizes.
   </ExerciseSolution>

### Transfer

6. **Potential for a two-stack deque.** A deque supports push-front, push-back, pop-front, and pop-back using two stacks (left and right). Propose a potential function that yields $O(1)$ amortized cost per operation when elements are moved from one stack to the other only when a pop would otherwise fail.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Generalize the two-stack queue potential to two directions. When does work accumulate?">
   Let $\Phi = 2(|L| + |R|)$ where $L$ and $R$ are the left and right stacks. A push costs 1 actual + 2 potential = 3 amortized. A pop from a nonempty stack costs 1 actual + 0 potential = 1 amortized. When a pop encounters an empty stack, $k$ elements are transferred from the other stack, costing $2k$ actual and decreasing potential by $2k$, so amortized cost is $2k + 1 - 2k = 1$. Every operation is $O(1)$ amortized because each element is moved at most once per direction.
   </ExerciseSolution>

7. **Amortized bound on a retrying operation.** An operation may fail and retry up to $r$ times, where $r$ depends on the state. Can amortized analysis bound the total cost when $r$ varies? What condition must the retry policy satisfy?

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Consider whether the retry is a form of deferred work that can be charged to earlier operations.">
   If each retry is bounded by a constant (e.g., at most 3 attempts) then standard amortization applies. If retries depend on state (e.g., retry until a random condition holds), the bound may require expected analysis instead. Amortized analysis requires the total work across the sequence to be deterministically bounded by a function of the operation count. A retry policy that can grow with the state size (e.g., linear retries per operation) breaks the sequence bound.
   </ExerciseSolution>

### Synthesis

8. **Design and prove bounds.** Design a data structure that supports `add(x)` in $O(1)$ amortized time and `remove-any()` (remove an arbitrary element) in $O(1)$ amortized time. State the representation, the potential function, and verify all operation categories.

   <ExerciseSolution hint="Consider an unsorted dynamic array with a bitmap or a free-list for holes.">
   Use an unsorted dynamic array with a "hole" stack. Insert: if the hole stack is nonempty, write into a reclaimed slot (actual cost 1); otherwise, append (amortized cost 3 via standard doubling). Remove: do not compact — push the vacated index onto the hole stack (actual cost 1). Potential $\Phi = 2(\text{size}) - \text{capacity}$ (same as dynamic array). Each insert fills either a hole (actual 1, no potential change) or the end (amortized 3). Each remove creates a hole (actual 1, but does not increase the array's used size — the relevant size for potential is the number of live elements). The hole stack grows without bound, which is acceptable as long as we consider $\Phi$ only for the array copy cost. The hole stack itself must be bounded or amortized separately; a full proof would also account for hole-stack overhead.
   </ExerciseSolution>

9. **Compare all three methods on one problem.** Use the aggregate, accounting, and potential methods to prove that $m$ operations on a two-stack queue cost $O(m)$. Show that all three produce the same asymptotic bound but differ in the constant factor.

   <ExerciseSolution>
   Aggregate: each element is pushed at most twice (once onto I, once onto O) and popped at most twice (once from I, once from O). Total operations per element ≤ 4, so $m$ operations move at most $4m$ elements — $O(m)$. Accounting: charge 3 per enqueue, 1 per dequeue. Enqueue: write element (1), store 2 credits. Dequeue (O nonempty): pop (1), consume 0 credits. Dequeue (I→O transfer): $2k$ actual + pop (1), consume $2k$ credits. Total charge ≤ $3m$. Potential: $\Phi = 2|I|$. Enqueue amortized 3, dequeue amortized 1 (transfer case: $(2k+1) - 2k = 1$). Total amortized ≤ $3m$. All three give $O(m)$ but the constants differ (aggregate: ≤ 4$m$, accounting: ≤ 3$m$, potential: ≤ 3$m$).
   </ExerciseSolution>

### Deliberately Omitted

- **Full potential-function proof for splay trees**: the sum-of-ranks potential (rank = $\lfloor \log_2(\text{subtree size}) \rfloor$) yields $O(\log n)$ amortized cost per splay operation. The proof is more complex than the examples here and requires analyzing zig, zig-zig, and zig-zag cases separately.
- **Deamortized data structures in full generality**: incremental rebuilding with correctness invariants for multiple simultaneous structures, scheduling work across operations, and competitive analysis of rebuild strategies. Deamortization is introduced via the dynamic-array example, but a complete treatment is a separate topic.
- **Lower bounds for amortized data structures**: proving that certain trade-offs are inherent (e.g., the $\Omega(m \alpha(n))$ lower bound for union-find). These rely on adversary arguments beyond this lesson's scope.
- **Amortized analysis of garbage collection**: reference-counting, mark-sweep, and copying collectors each have amortized cost arguments that depend on the mutator allocation rate and the collector's policy. The analysis framework is the same, but the details are specific to memory management rather than data structures.
- **Non-computational potentials**: potential functions modeling lock contention, network bytes, or energy rather than computation. Extending amortized analysis to these resources changes the accounting units and is an advanced application.
