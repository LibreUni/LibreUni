# Balanced Search Trees

A BST can degenerate: inserting keys in sorted order produces a linear chain where search and update are $O(n)$ instead of $O(\log n)$. This is not a flaw in the BST invariant — it is a consequence of the invariant's weakness. The BST restricts relative ordering but places no constraint on tree shape. Balanced tree families add a **shape invariant** that forces logarithmic height while keeping restructuring local.

Two solutions emerged in the 1960s–1970s. Georgy Adelson-Velsky and Evgenii Landis (1962) showed that requiring left and right subtree heights to differ by at most one is strong enough to guarantee $O(\log n)$ height, yet weak enough to maintain with $O(1)$ local rotations after each mutation. Rudolf Bayer (1972) introduced a "colored" binary representation of B-trees, later refined by Leonidas Guibas and Robert Sedgewick (1978) into red-black trees, which enforce a black-height invariant that permits slightly more imbalance but requires fewer rotations on average.

<MathStatement
  id="rotation-invariant"
  kind="theorem"
  title="Rotation preserves BST order"
  proofId="rotation-proof"
>
A left or right rotation at any node in a BST changes local parent-child pointers but preserves the inorder sequence of keys. The dictionary abstraction (key $\mapsto$ value) is unchanged by any sequence of rotations.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="rotation-proof" for="rotation-invariant" collapsible open>
Let $y$ be a node with left child $x$. Let $A$ be the left subtree of $x$, $B$ the right subtree of $x$, and $C$ the right subtree of $y$. By the BST invariant, all keys satisfy $A < x < B < y < C$. A right rotation makes $x$ the new local root, $y$ its right child, and $B$ the new left subtree of $y$. The inorder sequence before rotation is $\text{inorder}(A), x, \text{inorder}(B), y, \text{inorder}(C)$. After rotation it is $\text{inorder}(A), x, \text{inorder}(B), y, \text{inorder}(C)$ — identical. Left rotation is symmetric. Thus the set of keys and their relative order are preserved.
</MathProof>

## BST invariant and height bound

For a node $x$ with key $k$, define lower and upper bounds inherited from ancestors. A valid BST has every descendant key within those bounds. Search compares the target with the current key and descends left or right. Insert follows the same path to an allowed empty child. Inorder traversal yields keys in sorted order — provable by induction on subtree structure.

| Balance family | Invariant | Height bound | Rotation cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| AVL | $\lvert bf(x)\rvert \le 1$ for all $x$ | $\le 1.44 \log_2 n$ | Up to $O(\log n)$ rotations on delete |
| Red-black | Black-height equal; no red-red parent-child | $\le 2 \log_2 (n+1)$ | $O(1)$ amortized on insert |
| Treap | Heap priority + BST order | $O(\log n)$ expected | Randomized shape, no deterministic cases |
| Weight-balanced (BB[$\alpha$]) | Subtree weight ratio $\le \alpha$ | $O(\log n)$ | Different rotation policy, intricate analysis |

<MathStatement
  id="avl-height-bound"
  kind="theorem"
  title="AVL height is logarithmic"
  proofId="avl-height-proof"
  dependsOn={[{ id: "rotation-invariant", label: "rotation invariant" }]}
>
An AVL tree of $n$ nodes has height $h \le \log_\phi (\sqrt{5}(n+1)) - 2 \approx 1.44 \log_2 n$, where $\phi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$ is the golden ratio.
</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="avl-height-proof" for="avl-height-bound">
Let $N(h)$ be the minimum number of nodes in an AVL tree of height $h$. The root's two subtrees must have heights differing by at most one, so at least one subtree has height $h-1$ and the other at least $h-2$. Thus $N(h) \ge 1 + N(h-1) + N(h-2)$, with $N(0) = 0$, $N(1) = 1$. This recurrence dominates the Fibonacci sequence: $N(h) \ge F_{h+2} - 1$. Since $F_k \ge (\phi^{k-2})$ for $k \ge 2$, we have $n \ge \phi^{h} / \sqrt{5} - 1$. Solving for $h$ gives $h \le \log_\phi (\sqrt{5}(n+1))$, and $\log_\phi 2 \approx 1.44$. The same recurrence shows the bound is tight: there exist AVL trees of height $h$ with $F_{h+2} - 1$ nodes.
</MathProof>

## Rotations

A rotation changes local pointers while preserving inorder sequence. A right rotation around $y$ with left child $x$ makes $x$ the new root of the local subtree, $y$ its right child, and $x$'s former right subtree $y$'s left subtree. The keys remain in sorted inorder because the middle subtree $B$ lies between $x$ and $y$. A left rotation is symmetric.

## AVL trees

An AVL tree maintains balance factor $bf(x) = \text{height}(\text{left}(x)) - \text{height}(\text{right}(x))$ in $\{-1, 0, 1\}$ for every node. Store height at each node; deriving it recursively turns an update into $O(n)$, so cached height is part of the representation. After insertion, walk from the changed leaf toward the root, update heights, and rotate the first violated node using one of four cases: left-left, right-right, left-right, right-left.

The minimum node count $N(h)$ in an AVL tree of height $h$ satisfies $N(h) \ge 1 + N(h-1) + N(h-2)$. This Fibonacci-like recurrence grows exponentially, so $h = O(\log n)$. Deletion may require rebalancing at multiple ancestors but still performs $O(\log n)$ rotations.

**Worked AVL insertion trace on [10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 25].** Each node stores height $h$ and balance factor $bf = h(\text{left}) - h(\text{right})$.

Insert 10: node (10, $h=1$, $bf=0$).

Insert 20: attach as right child of 10. Node 10: $h=2$, $bf=-1$. Node 20: $h=1$, $bf=0$. Invariant holds.

Insert 30: attach as right child of 20. Node 30: leaf. Node 20: $h=2$, $bf=-1$. Node 10: $h=3$, $bf=-2$ — violated at 10. Right-right case: left-rotate at 10. After rotation, 20 is local root, left child 10 ($h=1$, $bf=0$), right child 30 ($h=1$, $bf=0$). Height: $h(20)=2$, $bf=0$. Invariant restored.

Insert 40: attach as right child of 30. Updates: 30 $h=2$, $bf=-1$; 20 $h=3$, $bf=-1$; 10 and 20 unaffected below. Node 20: $bf=-1$, okay.

Insert 50: attach as right child of 40. Updates propagate: 40 $h=2$, $bf=-1$; 30 $h=3$, $bf=-2$ — violated at 30. Right-right case: left-rotate at 30. After rotation, 40 is right child of 20, 30 is left child of 40. Heights: 30 $h=1$, 40 $h=2$, 20 $h=3$, $bf(20)=-1$. Invariant holds.

Insert 25: attach as left child of 40. Updates: 25 $h=1$; 40 $h=2$, $bf=0$; 30 $h=2$, $bf=1$; 20 $h=4$, $bf=-2$ — violated at 20. Violation path is 20 $\to$ 30 $\to$ 25: a left-right case. Right-rotate at 30: 25 becomes right child of 20, 30 becomes right child of 25. Left-rotate at 20: 25 becomes new root, left child 20, right child 30. Heights: 20 $h=2$, 30 $h=2$, 25 $h=3$, $bf(25)=0$. Final tree is balanced.

| Step | Tree shape (level-order) | Heights | Violation | Rotation |
|------|--------------------------|---------|-----------|----------|
| After 10 | 10 | $h=1$ | none | — |
| After 20 | 10, 20 | $h=2, 1$ | none | — |
| After 30 | 20, 10, 30 | $h=2, 1, 1$ | $bf(10)=-2$ | left at 10 |
| After 40 | 20, 10, 30, -, -, -, 40 | $h=2, 1, 2, 1$ | none | — |
| After 50 | 20, 10, 40, 30, 50 | $h=2, 1, 2, 1, 1$ | $bf(30)=-2$ | left at 30 |
| After 25 | 25, 20, 40, 10, 30, -, 50 | $h=2, 1, 2, 1, 1, 1$ | $bf(20)=-2$ | right at 30, left at 20 |

This trace shows that AVL rebalancing after insertion examines only the ancestor path and applies at most one double rotation. Each rotation preserves BST order while restoring the $\{-1, 0, 1\}$ balance factor invariant.

  title="AVL tree validator"
  language="python"
  code={`
class AVLNode:
    def __init__(self, key):
        self.key = key
        self.left = None
        self.right = None
        self.height = 1

def height(node):
    return node.height if node else 0

def balance_factor(node):
    return height(node.left) - height(node.right) if node else 0

def is_avl(node):
    if not node:
        return True
    bf = balance_factor(node)
    if abs(bf) > 1:
        return False
    return is_avl(node.left) and is_avl(node.right)

def insert(root, key):
    if not root:
        return AVLNode(key)
    if key < root.key:
        root.left = insert(root.left, key)
    else:
        root.right = insert(root.right, key)
    root.height = 1 + max(height(root.left), height(root.right))
    return root

# Build and validate
root = None
for k in [10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 25]:
    root = insert(root, k)
print("Height:", root.height if root else 0)
print("AVL valid after all inserts:", is_avl(root))
  `}
  output={`
Height: 3
AVL valid after all inserts: True
  `}

## Red-black tree insertion: worked example

Insert [10, 20, 30, 15] into an initially empty red-black tree. Standard convention: new nodes are red.

Insert 10: becomes root, color it black. No violation.

Insert 20: red node attached as right child of 10. Parent (10) is black: no red-red violation. Black-height: both paths have one black node.

Insert 30: red node attached as right child of 20. Parent (20) is red: violation. Uncle (left of 10) is null (black). Insertion is on the **outside** (right-right) relative to grandparent (10). Solution: left-rotate at grandparent (10), then recolor. After rotation: 20 is root (black), 10 is left child (red), 30 is right child (red). Black-height: root-to-leaf paths each have one black node.

Insert 15: red node attached as left child of 30. Parent (30) is red: violation. Uncle (10) is red. Solution: recolor parent, uncle, and grandparent — parent (30) becomes black, uncle (10) becomes black, grandparent (20) becomes red. Grandparent is root, recolor it black. Final tree: 20 (black), 10 (black), 30 (black), 15 (red) as left child of 30. Black-height: 2 black nodes on every root-to-leaf path.

The red-black cases require a state diagram covering three subcases (uncle red, uncle black with outside insertion, uncle black with inside insertion). Each case preserves black-height and resolves the red-red violation locally, potentially propagating the redness upward.

## Ordered operations

Balanced BSTs support more than exact lookup. Minimum and maximum follow extreme paths. Predecessor and successor use child subtrees or ancestor relationships. Range reporting finds a split point and traverses only relevant subtrees plus logarithmic search overhead.

Augment a node with subtree size to support order statistics: select the $k$th smallest by comparing $k$ with the left subtree size, and rank a key by accumulating sizes while descending.

<MathStatement
  id="augmentation-invariant"
  kind="definition"
  title="Subtree-size augmentation"
>
For a node $x$, define $\text{size}(x) = 1 + \text{size}(\text{left}(x)) + \text{size}(\text{right}(x))$. This field supports $\text{select}(k)$ and $\text{rank}(k)$ in $O(\log n)$ time on a balanced tree. After any rotation, $\text{size}$ must be recomputed bottom-up for the two affected nodes.
</MathStatement>

An augmented field that is not updated during every mutation can silently return plausible but incorrect answers. After a right rotation, recompute the old root ($y$) first from its new children, then the new root ($x$) from its children.

## Deletion and rebalancing discipline

Deletion is where intuitive reasoning about insertion fails. In an AVL tree, replacing a two-child node by its successor preserves BST order, but the physical deletion occurs in the successor's subtree and can reduce its height. Walking upward to recompute height and rebalance every affected ancestor is necessary — stopping after the first rotation is a documented source of implementation bugs because a height reduction can propagate to the root.

In a red-black tree, deletion can remove a black node and create a **black-height deficit**. The repair cases move, recolor, or eliminate the deficit while preserving the inorder sequence. A sentinel null leaf simplifies the case analysis, but it is a real participant in the color and black-height invariant. Implementations that treat null as "not a node" in one case and as a black leaf in another produce subtle violations that tests often miss.

For any balanced tree implementation, use a validation routine that checks ordering bounds, parent links, cached heights or colors, subtree sizes, and root conditions. Run it after every mutation in tests. Validation is not a proof, but it localizes which operation first violated the invariant.

## Failure modes

**Stale metadata after rotation.** Forgetting to recompute height or size after a rotation invalidates all subsequent operations. A rank query returns a plausible-looking integer that is wrong. Repair: always recompute bottom-up metadata for the two nodes directly affected by any rotation.

**Parent pointer corruption.** A rotation changes which node is the child of the parent pointer. If the rotation function updates the child-parent links but forgets to update the parent of the rotated subtree, subsequent upward traversals follow a dangling link. The tree appears correct under inorder traversal but fails during rebalancing walks.

**Inconsistent sentinel treatment.** In red-black deletion, treating null as "no node" in one branch and "black leaf" in another produces missing rotation or double-black elimination. The sentinel must be a uniform black leaf with no parent or key — never a conditional absence.

**Key mutation after insertion.** If a key field changes while the node is in the tree, all ancestor bounds are violated. Search fails to find it at its stored position. Either make keys immutable while stored, or remove and reinsert. In languages without immutable references, enforce this by interface design, not convention.

## Choice among ordered structures

AVL trees keep tighter height and favor read-heavy workloads. Red-black trees tolerate more structural slack and bound update rotations well. Treaps assign random priorities and maintain heap order by rotations, giving expected logarithmic height without deterministic balance cases. B-trees widen nodes to reduce height and I/O operations, appropriate for external memory.

Always state whether the bound is worst-case or expected, whether keys are adversarial, and whether comparison itself is constant. If keys are strings of length $\ell$, a comparison costs $O(\ell)$, so $O(\log n)$ comparisons do not imply $O(\log n)$ character work. This distinction is critical in real dictionaries and indexing systems.

**Treap random-priority analysis.** Expected-height proof via random priorities. Treaps combine BST order with heap-order on random priorities; the proof that expected height is $O(\log n)$ relies on a permutation model that is instructive but duplicates analysis better covered in a probability review. The randomized nature means worst-case height is $O(n)$ with negligible probability, which matters in adversarial settings.

**Optimal static BST (Knuth's DP) and dynamic optimality conjecture.** Constructing a BST with minimum expected search cost for a known key-access distribution, and the splay-tree conjecture that it is dynamically optimal. These are advanced data-structure theory topics that require amortized analysis and competitive analysis frameworks beyond the sequential invariant approach of this lesson.

**Concurrent balanced trees.** Lock-free or transactional BSTs that support concurrent readers and writers. These require memory-ordering and synchronization reasoning that assumes a different correctness model (linearizability, serializability) and is covered in concurrent-data-structures material.

**B-tree variant details.** The exact split-merge invariants of B-trees, the choice of minimum degree $t$, and the I/O complexity analysis belong to the B-tree lesson, which builds on the rotation and node-splitting concepts introduced here.
