# Applications of Integration

Prerequisites: the definite integral, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and elementary derivatives. The central modeling decision is to choose a thin element, express its measure in terms of the integration variable, and sum the elements in a limit.

## Area Between Curves

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f(x)\ge g(x)$ there, the region between their graphs has area

$$A=\int_a^b\bigl(f(x)-g(x)\bigr)\,dx.$$

The order is not optional: if the graphs cross, split the interval at every intersection or integrate $|f-g|$. When horizontal slices are simpler, solve for $x$ as a function of $y$ and use $A=\int_c^d (x_{\mathrm{right}}(y)-x_{\mathrm{left}}(y))\,dy$.

The shaded region is the geometric object represented by the integrand:

For $y=x$ and $y=x^2$, the intersections are $x=0,1$, and the line is above the parabola on that interval. Hence

$$A=\int_0^1(x-x^2)\,dx=\left[\frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1=\frac16.$$

## Volumes by Slicing

Let a solid have cross-sectional area $A(x)$ perpendicular to the $x$-axis. If $A$ is integrable, Cavalieri's slicing principle gives

<MathStatement id="volume-by-slices" kind="theorem" title="Volume by Slicing" number="1">

$$V=\int_a^b A(x)\,dx.$$

</MathStatement>

Revolving a region about the $x$-axis produces disks of area $\pi [R(x)]^2$ when there is no hole, and washers of area $\pi([R(x)]^2-[r(x)]^2)$ when the inner radius is $r(x)$. Shells instead use cylindrical strips: revolving a vertical strip of radius $x$ and height $h(x)$ about the $y$-axis gives

$$V=2\pi\int_a^b xh(x)\,dx.$$

For the region under $y=2x-x^2$ on $[0,2]$, rotation about the $x$-axis gives

$$V=\pi\int_0^2(2x-x^2)^2\,dx=\frac{64\pi}{15}.$$

## Arc Length and Surface Area

Approximate a smooth graph by line segments. For $y=f(x)$, the distance element satisfies

$$ds=\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}=\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx.$$

Therefore:

<MathStatement id="arc-length" kind="definition" title="Arc Length of a Graph" number="2">

If $f$ is continuously differentiable on $[a,b]$, its graph has length

$$L=\int_a^b\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx.$$

</MathStatement>

Rotating the graph about the $x$-axis sweeps out a band with circumference $2\pi f(x)$, so for a nonnegative $f$,

$$S=2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx.$$

These integrals frequently have no elementary antiderivative; numerical quadrature is then part of the mathematical solution, not a failure of the model.

## Mass, Work, and Average Value

For a one-dimensional object with linear density $\rho(x)$, a slice of width $dx$ has mass $dm=\rho(x)\,dx$, giving

$$m=\int_a^b\rho(x)\,dx.$$

If a variable force $F(x)$ moves an object from $a$ to $b$ in the positive direction, the work is

$$W=\int_a^bF(x)\,dx.$$

The same accumulation logic yields the average value of a continuous function:

$$f_{\mathrm{avg}}=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx.$$
