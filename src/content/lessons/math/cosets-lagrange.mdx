# Cosets and Lagrange's Theorem

If you know the size of a group, does that restrict the sizes of the subgroups it can contain? For example, can a group of order 12 have a subgroup of order 5? The answer is **no**, and proving this requires partitioning a group into equal-sized pieces using a subgroup. These pieces are **cosets**.

## The Motivating Idea: Shifting a Subgroup

Let $G = (\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}, +) = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ and $H = \{0, 3\}$. "Shift" $H$ by adding each element of $G$:

| Shift | Result |
|-------|--------|
| $0 + H$ | $\{0, 3\}$ |
| $1 + H$ | $\{1, 4\}$ |
| $2 + H$ | $\{2, 5\}$ |
| $3 + H$ | $\{3, 0\} = \{0, 3\}$ |
| $4 + H$ | $\{4, 1\} = \{1, 4\}$ |
| $5 + H$ | $\{5, 2\} = \{2, 5\}$ |

Two facts emerge:

1. Every element of $G$ appears in exactly one of the three distinct shifted sets $\{0,3\}, \{1,4\}, \{2,5\}$ — they form a **partition** of $G$.
2. All three sets have the same size (2), which is $|H|$.

This is not a coincidence — it is a general theorem.

## Formalization: Cosets

Let $G$ be a group and $H \le G$. For $g \in G$:
- The **left coset** of $H$ containing $g$ is $gH = \{ g \cdot h \mid h \in H \}$.
- The **right coset** of $H$ containing $g$ is $Hg = \{ h \cdot g \mid h \in H \}$.

$g$ is called the **coset representative**.

### Lemma: Cosets Partition the Group

> **Lemma 1:** For any $a, b \in G$, either $aH = bH$ or $aH \cap bH = \emptyset$.

*Proof:* Suppose $aH \cap bH \neq \emptyset$. Then there exist $h_1, h_2 \in H$ such that $a h_1 = b h_2$. Multiply on the right by $h_2^{-1}$: $a h_1 h_2^{-1} = b$. Since $h_1 h_2^{-1} \in H$, we have $b = a h$ for some $h \in H$. Then $bH = a h H = a H$ (because $hH = H$). So overlapping cosets coincide. $\square$

### Lemma: All Cosets Have the Same Size

> **Lemma 2:** For any $g \in G$, the map $f: H \to gH$ defined by $f(h) = g h$ is a bijection. Hence $|gH| = |H|$.

*Proof:* **Injective:** If $g h_1 = g h_2$, multiply on the left by $g^{-1}$ to get $h_1 = h_2$. **Surjective:** Every element of $gH$ is of the form $g h$ for some $h \in H$ by definition, so $f$ hits all of $gH$. $\square$

## Lagrange's Theorem

> **Theorem (Lagrange):** If $G$ is a finite group and $H \le G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$. Moreover,
> $$|G| = [G : H] \cdot |H|$$
> where $[G : H]$, the **index** of $H$ in $G$, is the number of distinct left cosets of $H$ in $G$.

*Proof:* By Lemma 1, the left cosets of $H$ form a partition of $G$. By Lemma 2, each coset has exactly $|H|$ elements. If there are $[G : H]$ distinct cosets, then $|G| = [G : H] \cdot |H|$. Since $[G : H]$ is an integer, $|H|$ divides $|G|$. $\square$

### Key Corollaries

1. **Order of an element divides group order:** $|a| = |\langle a \rangle|$ divides $|G|$ for any $a \in G$.

2. **Prime order groups are cyclic:** If $|G| = p$ is prime, pick any $x \neq e$. Then $|\langle x \rangle|$ divides $p$ and exceeds 1, so $|\langle x \rangle| = p = |G|$. Hence $G = \langle x \rangle$ is cyclic.

3. **Subgroup containment by index:** If $H \le K \le G$, then $[G : H] = [G : K] \cdot [K : H]$.

## The Lattice of Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$

The additive group $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$ has order 12. Its subgroups correspond to the divisors of 12:

```
        Z/12Z
       /  |   \
      /   |    \
   Z/6Z  Z/4Z  Z/3Z
    |     |     |
   Z/3Z  Z/2Z  {0}
    |     |
   {0}  {0}
```

Each subgroup's order divides 12: orders 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.

## Worked Example: Left vs Right Cosets in $D_3$

Let $H = \{R_0, S_1\} \le D_3$:

- **Left cosets:** $R_0 H = S_1 H = \{R_0, S_1\}$; $R_{120} H = S_3 H = \{R_{120}, S_3\}$; $R_{240} H = S_2 H = \{R_{240}, S_2\}$.
- **Right cosets:** $H R_0 = H S_1 = \{R_0, S_1\}$; $H R_{120} = \{R_{120}, S_2\}$; $H R_{240} = \{R_{240}, S_3\}$.

Notice the left coset $R_{120} H = \{R_{120}, S_3\}$ differs from the right coset $H R_{120} = \{R_{120}, S_2\}$. In non-Abelian groups, left and right cosets need not coincide.

## Deep Practice: The Converse of Lagrange is False

Lagrange tells us that if $H \le G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$. But the converse is **false**: a divisor of $|G|$ does not guarantee the existence of a subgroup of that order.

The smallest counterexample is $A_4$ (the even permutations of 4 elements), which has order $12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$. It has subgroups of orders $1, 2, 3, 4$, but **no subgroup of order 6**. This is why the Sylow theorems (coming later) are needed — they guarantee subgroups of prime-power order.

### Challenge Problem

Let $G$ be a group of order 35.

1. What are the possible orders of subgroups of $G$ according to Lagrange?
2. Use the corollary about prime-order groups to argue that any subgroup of order 5 or 7 is cyclic.
3. If $G$ has a unique subgroup of order 5 and a unique subgroup of order 7, and these subgroups intersect only at $\{e\}$, what is the minimum possible size of $G$?

<details>

    Reveal Solution
  </summary>

    (1) The divisors of 35 are 1, 5, 7, 35. (2) 5 and 7 are prime, so any group of order 5 or 7 is cyclic. (3) The product of the two cyclic subgroups would contain 5·7 = 35 elements if they intersect trivially, so G would have at least 35 elements.

## Retrieval & Synthesis

<details>
  <summary>
    Prove: In any group, a left coset gH and the subgroup H have the same number of elements.
  </summary>
  <div>
    Define f: H → gH by f(h) = gh. It is injective (gh₁ = gh₂ ⇒ h₁ = h₂ by left cancellation) and surjective (every element of gH is gh for some h by definition). Hence |gH| = |H|.
  </div>
</details>
