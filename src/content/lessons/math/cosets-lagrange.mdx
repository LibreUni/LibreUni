# Cosets and Lagrange's Theorem

If you know the size of a group, does that restrict the sizes of the subgroups it can contain? 

For example, can a group of order 12 have a subgroup of order 5? The answer is a definitive **no**, and the proof of this fact relies on a geometric way of partitioning a group into equal-sized pieces using a subgroup. This partitioning process creates what we call **cosets**.

## The Problem: Partitioning a Group

Let's look at the additive group $G = (\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}, +) = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ and its subgroup:
$$H = \{0, 3\}$$

If we "shift" this entire subgroup by adding different elements of $G$ to all elements of $H$, what sets do we get?
- Shift by $0$: $0 + H = \{0, 3\}$
- Shift by $1$: $1 + H = \{1, 4\}$
- Shift by $2$: $2 + H = \{2, 5\}$
- Shift by $3$: $3 + H = \{3, 0\} = \{0, 3\}$
- Shift by $4$: $4 + H = \{4, 1\} = \{1, 4\}$
- Shift by $5$: $5 + H = \{5, 2\} = \{2, 5\}$

Notice two remarkable facts:
1. Every element in $G$ belongs to exactly one of the three distinct shifted sets $\{0, 3\}$, $\{1, 4\}$, or $\{2, 5\}$. They form a partition of $G$.
2. All three of these sets have exactly the same size ($2$).

## Formalization: Cosets and Lagrange's Theorem

Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. For any element $g \in G$:
- The **left coset** of $H$ containing $g$ is the set:
  $$gH = \{ g \cdot h \mid h \in H \}$$
- The **right coset** of $H$ containing $g$ is the set:
  $$Hg = \{ h \cdot g \mid h \in H \}$$

The element $g$ is called the **coset representative**.

### Properties of Cosets

1. **Partitioning:** The set of left cosets of $H$ forms a partition of $G$. That is, every element of $G$ lies in exactly one left coset, and two left cosets $aH$ and $bH$ are either identical or disjoint. (The same holds for right cosets).
2. **Equal Size:** There is a bijection between $H$ and any of its cosets $gH$ (given by $h \mapsto g \cdot h$). Therefore, every coset has exactly the same number of elements as the subgroup $H$ itself.

### Lagrange's Theorem

Because the cosets partition $G$ into pieces of equal size $|H|$, we arrive at one of the most fundamental theorems of group theory:

> **Lagrange's Theorem:** If $G$ is a finite group and $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then the order of $H$ divides the order of $G$.
> $$|G| = [G : H] \cdot |H|$$
> Where $[G : H]$ is the number of distinct left cosets of $H$ in $G$, called the **index** of $H$ in $G$.

### Key Corollaries

1. **Order of an Element:** The order of any element $a \in G$ divides the order of $G$ (since $|a| = |\langle a \rangle|$, and $\langle a \rangle \le G$).
2. **Prime Order Groups:** If $G$ is a group of prime order $p$, then $G$ must be cyclic, and any non-identity element generates $G$. (Proof: Let $x \in G$ be a non-identity element. The order of $H = \langle x \rangle$ must divide $p$. Since $p$ is prime and $H$ has more than 1 element, $|H| = p$, so $H = G$).

## Worked Example: Left vs Right Cosets in $D_3$

Let's compute all left and right cosets of $H = \{R_0, S_1\}$ in $D_3$ to see if they are the same.
- **Left Cosets:**
  - $R_0 H = S_1 H = \{R_0, S_1\}$
  - $R_{120} H = S_3 H = \{R_{120}, S_3\}$
  - $R_{240} H = S_2 H = \{R_{240}, S_2\}$
- **Right Cosets:**
  - $H R_0 = H S_1 = \{R_0, S_1\}$
  - $H R_{120} = \{R_0 \circ R_{120}, S_1 \circ R_{120}\} = \{R_{120}, S_2\}$
  - $H R_{240} = \{R_0 \circ R_{240}, S_1 \circ R_{240}\} = \{R_{240}, S_3\}$

Notice that the left coset $R_{120} H = \{R_{120}, S_3\}$ is **NOT** equal to the right coset $H R_{120} = \{R_{120}, S_2\}$. 
In non-Abelian groups, left and right cosets of a subgroup do not necessarily coincide!

Let's write a Python script to verify this behavior and compute the index of subgroups.

elements = {
    'R0': (0, 1, 2), 'R120': (1, 2, 0), 'R240': (2, 0, 1),

def compose(p1, p2):
    return tuple(p1[p2[i]] for i in range(3))

# Subgroup H = {R0, S1}
H = ['R0', 'S1']

def get_left_coset(g, subgroup):
    coset = set()
    for h in subgroup:
        res = compose(elements[g], elements[h])
        res_name = [name for name, perm in elements.items() if perm == res][0]
        coset.add(res_name)
    return frozenset(coset)

def get_right_coset(g, subgroup):
    coset = set()
    for h in subgroup:
        res = compose(elements[h], elements[g])
        res_name = [name for name, perm in elements.items() if perm == res][0]
        coset.add(res_name)
    return frozenset(coset)

left_coset = get_left_coset('R120', H)
right_coset = get_right_coset('R120', H)

print(f"Left Coset R120*H:  {set(left_coset)}")
print(f"Right Coset H*R120: {set(right_coset)}")
print(f"Do they match? {left_coset == right_coset}")
