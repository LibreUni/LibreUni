# Derivatives: Rates of Change

The derivative measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function. Geometrically, it represents the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function at a given point.

---

## Differentiability and the Derivative

We formally define the derivative of a function at a point as a limit of the difference quotient.

<MathStatement id="derivative-definition" kind="definition" title="The Derivative" number="1">

Let $f: I \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined on an open interval $I$ containing $c$. The function $f$ is said to be **differentiable** at $c$ if the limit

$$f'(c) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h}$$

exists. If this limit exists, we denote it by $f'(c)$ or $\frac{df}{dx}(c)$.

</MathStatement>

Differentiability is a stronger condition than continuity. A function can be continuous but not differentiable, but the converse is always true.

<MathStatement id="diff-implies-cont" kind="theorem" title="Differentiability Implies Continuity" number="1" proofId="diff-implies-cont-proof">

If $f$ is differentiable at $c$, then $f$ is continuous at $c$.

</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="diff-implies-cont-proof" for="diff-implies-cont">

To prove $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$, it suffices to show that $\lim_{x \to c} (f(x) - f(c)) = 0$. For $x \neq c$, we can write:

$$f(x) - f(c) = \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} \cdot (x - c).$$

Taking the limit as $x \to c$:

$$\lim_{x \to c} (f(x) - f(c)) = \lim_{x \to c} \left( \frac{f(x) - f(c)}{x - c} \cdot (x - c) \right).$$

Since $f$ is differentiable at $c$, the limit of the first term is $f'(c)$. Since $\lim_{x \to c} (x - c) = 0$, we have:

$$\lim_{x \to c} (f(x) - f(c)) = f'(c) \cdot 0 = 0.$$

Therefore, $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$, and $f$ is continuous at $c$.

</MathProof>

A classic counterexample of a function that is continuous but not differentiable is $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$. The difference quotient as $h \to 0^+$ is $1$, but as $h \to 0^-$ is $-1$, so the limit does not exist.

## Fundamental Rules of Differentiation

<TangentPlayground client:load />

To compute derivatives efficiently, we establish algebraic rules derived from the definition.

<MathStatement id="differentiation-rules" kind="theorem" title="Differentiation Rules" number="2" proofId="product-rule-proof">

Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable at $x$.
1. **Sum Rule**: $(f + g)'(x) = f'(x) + g'(x)$.
2. **Product Rule**: $(fg)'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)$.
3. **Quotient Rule**: If $g(x) \neq 0$, $\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}$.

</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="product-rule-proof" for="differentiation-rules">

We prove the Product Rule. Let $F(x) = f(x)g(x)$. Using the definition:

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}.$$

Subtract and add the term $f(x)g(x+h)$ in the numerator:

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x+h) + f(x)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}$$
$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \left( \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \cdot g(x+h) + f(x) \cdot \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} \right).$$

Since $f$ and $g$ are differentiable, their individual limits exist. Since $g$ is differentiable, it is continuous, so $\lim_{h \to 0} g(x+h) = g(x)$. Applying limit laws:

$$F'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x).$$

</MathProof>

### The Chain Rule

The Chain Rule allows us to differentiate composite functions. It is the mathematical engine behind backpropagation in deep neural networks.

<MathStatement id="chain-rule" kind="theorem" title="The Chain Rule" number="3">

If $g$ is differentiable at $x$, and $f$ is differentiable at $g(x)$, then the composite function $y = (f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ is differentiable at $x$, and:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x).$$

</MathStatement>

## Linear Approximation

The derivative provides the best linear approximation of a function near a point. Geometrically, this is the tangent line equation.

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a).$$

The tangent line is the limit of secant lines as the second point approaches $(a,f(a))$:

Let us visualize how the linear approximation matches $f(x) = \sin(x)$ around $a = 1.0$, and verify the error increases as we move away from $a$.

## Implicit Differentiation

When variables are defined implicitly (e.g., $x^2 + y^2 = 1$), we differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, applying the Chain Rule to $y$ (treating $y$ as a function of $x$).

For the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, differentiating yields:

$$2x + 2y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}.$$
