# Field Extensions and Algebraic Elements

How do we construct a field that contains the root of an irreducible polynomial? 

For example, the polynomial $x^2 + 1$ has no roots in the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$. To solve this, we invent a symbol $i$ satisfying $i^2 = -1$ and append it to $\mathbb{R}$. This construction yields the complex numbers $\mathbb{C} = \{ a + bi \mid a, b \in \mathbb{R} \}$. 

Notice that $\mathbb{C}$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$ with basis $\{1, i\}$. The dimension of this vector space is $2$. In abstract algebra, we generalize this construction to any field, viewing larger fields as vector spaces over smaller ones. These structures are called **field extensions**.

## The Motivating Problem: Arithmetic in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$

Let's adjoin the symbol $\sqrt{2}$ (a root of $x^2 - 2$) to the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. This forms a new field:
$$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{ a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \mathbb{Q} \}$$

Addition in this field is straightforward, but how do we divide? If we have an element:
$$\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{2}$$
what is its multiplicative inverse $\alpha^{-1} = \frac{1}{1 + \sqrt{2}}$ in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$?

We can rationalize the denominator:
$$\frac{1}{1 + \sqrt{2}} = \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{(1 + \sqrt{2})(1 - \sqrt{2})} = \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{1 - 2} = -1 + \sqrt{2}$$

Notice that the inverse, $-1 + \sqrt{2}$, is still an element of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ (with $a = -1, b = 1$). 
This is not a coincidence: adjoining a root of any irreducible polynomial to a field always yields a field!

### Try It Yourself: Extension Dimension

Because $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$ with basis $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$, its dimension as a vector space is $2$. We say the degree of the extension is $2$.

## Formalization: Field Extensions and Degrees

If $K$ and $L$ are fields such that $K \subseteq L$, we say $L$ is a **field extension** of $K$, denoted $L/K$.
- Because $K$ is closed under its field operations, the larger field $L$ can be viewed as a **vector space** over $K$ (where elements of $L$ are vectors, and elements of $K$ are scalars).
- The **degree** of the extension $L/K$ (written $[L:K]$) is the dimension of $L$ as a vector space over $K$.
- An extension is **finite** if $[L:K]$ is finite.

### The Tower Rule

Field extensions can be stacked. If we have a chain of field extensions $K \subseteq L \subseteq M$:

> **Theorem (The Tower Rule):** The degree of the overall extension $M/K$ is the product of the intermediate degrees:
> $$[M : K] = [M : L] \cdot [L : K]$$

## Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

Let $L/K$ be a field extension, and let $\alpha \in L$.
- $\alpha$ is **algebraic** over $K$ if it is the root of some non-zero polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$.
- $\alpha$ is **transcendental** over $K$ if it is not algebraic (meaning no such polynomial exists).

### Minimal Polynomials

If $\alpha$ is algebraic over $K$, there exists a unique monic, irreducible polynomial $m_{\alpha, K}(x) \in K[x]$ of minimum degree that has $\alpha$ as a root. We call this the **minimal polynomial** of $\alpha$ over $K$.
- The degree of the simple extension $[K(\alpha) : K]$ is exactly equal to the degree of the minimal polynomial of $\alpha$ over $K$.

## Worked Example: Calculating Inverses in Extensions

To compute inverses in a simple extension $K(\alpha)$, we can use the Extended Euclidean Algorithm on polynomials. 
Let's find the inverse of $\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{2}$ in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$.
The minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{2}$ is $p(x) = x^2 - 2$. The element we want to invert is $f(x) = x + 1$.
Since $p(x)$ is irreducible, $\gcd(f(x), p(x)) = 1$. By Bezout's identity, there exist polynomials $a(x), b(x)$ such that:
$$a(x)f(x) + b(x)p(x) = 1$$

Evaluating this at $x = \sqrt{2}$ (where $p(\sqrt{2}) = 0$):
$$a(\sqrt{2})f(\sqrt{2}) = 1 \implies f(\sqrt{2})^{-1} = a(\sqrt{2})$$

Let's run polynomial division:
$$x^2 - 2 = (x - 1)(x + 1) - 1 \implies 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1) - (x^2 - 2)$$
So $a(x) = x - 1$. 
Evaluating at $x = \sqrt{2}$:
$$(1 + \sqrt{2})^{-1} = \sqrt{2} - 1$$
This is the same inverse we found earlier!

## Contrast: Algebraic vs Transcendental Numbers

- **Algebraic Number:** $\sqrt{2}$ is algebraic over $\mathbb{Q}$ because it is the root of $x^2 - 2 \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$. The degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}]$ is 2.
- **Transcendental Number:** $\pi$ is transcendental over $\mathbb{Q}$ (proven by Lindemann in 1882). This means there is no non-zero polynomial with rational coefficients that has $\pi$ as a root. The extension $[\mathbb{Q}(\pi):\mathbb{Q}]$ has **infinite** degree.
