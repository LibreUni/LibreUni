# Field Extensions and Algebraic Elements

How do we construct a field that contains the root of an irreducible polynomial?

For example, the polynomial $x^2 + 1$ has no roots in the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$. To resolve this, we invent a symbol $i$ satisfying $i^2 = -1$ and append it to $\mathbb{R}$. This yields the complex numbers $\mathbb{C} = \{ a + bi \mid a, b \in \mathbb{R} \}$.

Notice that $\mathbb{C}$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$ with basis $\{1, i\}$, so its dimension is $2$. In abstract algebra, this construction generalizes: larger fields can be viewed as vector spaces over smaller ones. These structures are called **field extensions**.

## The Motivating Problem: Arithmetic in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$

Adjoin the symbol $\sqrt{2}$ (a root of $x^2 - 2$) to the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. This forms a new field:
$$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{ a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \mathbb{Q} \}$$

Addition is straightforward, but how do we divide? Consider $\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{2}$ and compute its multiplicative inverse $\alpha^{-1}$ in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$.

Rationalize the denominator:
$$\frac{1}{1 + \sqrt{2}} = \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{(1 + \sqrt{2})(1 - \sqrt{2})} = \frac{1 - \sqrt{2}}{1 - 2} = -1 + \sqrt{2}$$

The inverse $-1 + \sqrt{2}$ is still an element of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ (with $a = -1, b = 1$). This is not a coincidence: adjoining a root of any irreducible polynomial to a field always yields a field.

## Formalization: Field Extensions and Degrees

If $K$ and $L$ are fields such that $K \subseteq L$, we say $L$ is a **field extension** of $K$, denoted $L/K$.
- Because $K$ is closed under its field operations, the larger field $L$ can be viewed as a **vector space** over $K$ (where elements of $L$ are vectors, and elements of $K$ are scalars).
- The **degree** of the extension $L/K$ (written $[L:K]$) is the dimension of $L$ as a vector space over $K$.
- An extension is **finite** if $[L:K]$ is finite.

### The Tower Rule

Field extensions can be stacked. If we have a chain of field extensions $K \subseteq L \subseteq M$:

> **Theorem (The Tower Rule):** The degree of the overall extension $M/K$ is the product of the intermediate degrees:
> $$[M : K] = [M : L] \cdot [L : K]$$

*Proof.* Let $\{u_1, \dots, u_d\}$ be a basis for $L$ over $K$ ($d = [L:K]$) and $\{v_1, \dots, v_e\}$ a basis for $M$ over $L$ ($e = [M:L]$). We claim $\{u_i v_j \mid 1 \le i \le d,\, 1 \le j \le e\}$ is a basis for $M$ over $K$.

*Spanning:* Any $x \in M$ can be written $x = \sum_{j=1}^e a_j v_j$ with $a_j \in L$. Each $a_j = \sum_{i=1}^d b_{ij} u_i$ with $b_{ij} \in K$. Substituting gives $x = \sum_{i,j} b_{ij} (u_i v_j)$.

*Linear independence:* Suppose $\sum_{i,j} c_{ij} (u_i v_j) = 0$ with $c_{ij} \in K$. Regroup as $\sum_{j=1}^e (\sum_{i=1}^d c_{ij} u_i) v_j = 0$. Since the $v_j$ are $L$-independent, each inner sum $\sum_{i=1}^d c_{ij} u_i = 0$ in $L$. Since the $u_i$ are $K$-independent, all $c_{ij}=0$. Hence the $de$ products form a basis, so $[M:K] = de = [M:L][L:K]$. $\square$

## Algebraic and Transcendental Elements

Let $L/K$ be a field extension, and let $\alpha \in L$.
- $\alpha$ is **algebraic** over $K$ if it is the root of some non-zero polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$.
- $\alpha$ is **transcendental** over $K$ if it is not algebraic (meaning no such polynomial exists).

### Minimal Polynomials

If $\alpha$ is algebraic over $K$, there exists a unique monic, irreducible polynomial $m_{\alpha, K}(x) \in K[x]$ of minimum degree that has $\alpha$ as a root. This is the **minimal polynomial** of $\alpha$ over $K$.

> **Theorem:** $[K(\alpha):K] = \deg(m_{\alpha, K})$.

*Proof.* Let $n = \deg(m_{\alpha, K})$. The evaluation homomorphism $\phi: K[x] \to K(\alpha)$, $f(x) \mapsto f(\alpha)$, has kernel $\langle m_{\alpha, K} \rangle$ because $m_{\alpha, K}$ generates the principal ideal of all polynomials vanishing at $\alpha$. By the First Isomorphism Theorem, $K[x]/\langle m_{\alpha, K} \rangle \cong K(\alpha)$. The quotient is an $n$-dimensional vector space over $K$ with basis $\{1, x, \dots, x^{n-1}\}$, and this basis maps to $\{1, \alpha, \dots, \alpha^{n-1}\}$. Hence $\{1, \alpha, \dots, \alpha^{n-1}\}$ spans $K(\alpha)$ and is linearly independent (a dependence would give a polynomial of degree $< n$ vanishing at $\alpha$, contradicting minimality). Therefore $[K(\alpha):K] = n$. $\square$

### Computing a Minimal Polynomial Over $\mathbb{Q}$

Find the minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.

*Step 1.* Set $\alpha = \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$. Square: $\alpha^2 = 2 + 2\sqrt{6} + 3 = 5 + 2\sqrt{6}$.

*Step 2.* Isolate the radical: $\alpha^2 - 5 = 2\sqrt{6}$. Square again: $(\alpha^2 - 5)^2 = 24$.

*Step 3.* Expand: $\alpha^4 - 10\alpha^2 + 25 = 24$, so $\alpha^4 - 10\alpha^2 + 1 = 0$.

*Step 4.* Check irreducibility. The polynomial $x^4 - 10x^2 + 1$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ (by the rational root test: possible roots $\pm1$ are not zeros) and has no quadratic factor with rational coefficients (one can check modulo $p$ or note its Galois group has order $4$). Hence $m_{\sqrt{2}+\sqrt{3},\,\mathbb{Q}}(x) = x^4 - 10x^2 + 1$, and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}+\sqrt{3}):\mathbb{Q}] = 4$.

## The Tower Rule Combined with Simple Extensions

A powerful consequence of the Tower Rule is the **tower law for simple extensions**: if $\alpha$ is algebraic over $K$ and $K(\alpha) \subseteq L \subseteq M$, then $[M:K] = [M:K(\alpha)] \cdot [K(\alpha):K]$.

This lets us compute degrees of composite extensions. For instance, $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})$ is $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 2 = 4$.

## Existence of Algebraic Closure

> **Theorem (Existence of Algebraic Closure).** Every field $K$ has an algebraic closure $\overline{K}$ — an algebraic extension of $K$ that is algebraically closed.

*Proof sketch.* Let $S$ be the set of all monic irreducible polynomials in $K[x]$. For each $f \in S$, introduce variables $u_{f,1}, \dots, u_{f,\deg f}$. Form the polynomial ring $R = K[\{u_{f,i}\}]$ and the ideal $I$ generated by the coefficients of $f - \prod_i (x - u_{f,i})$ for each $f \in S$. Then $I \neq R$ (since otherwise finitely many polynomials would produce a contradiction), so by Zorn's Lemma there is a maximal ideal $M \supseteq I$. The field $K_1 = R/M$ splits every polynomial in $K[x]$. Iterate to obtain $K_2, K_3, \dots$ splitting polynomials over $K_1, K_2, \dots$ respectively. The union $\overline{K} = \bigcup_{n \ge 1} K_n$ is algebraically closed and algebraic over $K$. $\square$

## Primitive Element Theorem

> **Theorem (Primitive Element Theorem).** Let $E/F$ be a finite separable extension. Then $E = F(\alpha)$ for some $\alpha \in E$ — a **primitive element**.

*Proof (characteristic zero case).* Write $E = F(a, b)$. Let $p(x), q(x)$ be minimal polynomials for $a, b$ over $F$, with roots $a = a_1, \dots, a_m$ and $b = b_1, \dots, b_n$ in some extension. Choose $c \in F$ such that $c \neq (a_i - a)/(b - b_j)$ for all $i \ge 1, j > 1$. Set $\alpha = a + cb$. Then $F(\alpha) \subseteq F(a, b)$. Consider $h(x) = p(\alpha - cx) \in F(\alpha)[x]$. Since $h(b) = p(a) = 0$ and $q(b) = 0$, both $h$ and $q$ share the root $b$. For $j > 1$, $h(b_j) = p(\alpha - cb_j) = p(a + c(b - b_j)) \neq 0$ by choice of $c$. Hence the minimal polynomial of $b$ over $F(\alpha)$ divides both $h$ and $q$, but $b$ is its only common root in the separable closure, so $b \in F(\alpha)$. Thus $a = \alpha - cb \in F(\alpha)$, giving $F(a,b) = F(\alpha)$. Induction extends this to finitely many generators. $\square$

{/* Source tracking: Primitive element theorem proof synthesized from Dummit & Foote, Chapter 13.4, and Artin's "Algebra", Chapter 15. */}

Consequence: every finite separable extension is simple. For example, $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3})$.

## Worked Example: Computing Inverses via the Extended Euclidean Algorithm

To compute inverses in $K(\alpha)$, use the Extended Euclidean Algorithm on polynomials. Find the inverse of $\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{2}$ in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$.

The minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{2}$ is $p(x) = x^2 - 2$. The element to invert is $f(x) = x + 1$ evaluated at $\sqrt{2}$. Since $p(x)$ is irreducible, $\gcd(f(x), p(x)) = 1$. By Bezout's identity, there exist $a(x), b(x)$ such that:
$$a(x)f(x) + b(x)p(x) = 1$$

Perform polynomial division:
$$x^2 - 2 = (x - 1)(x + 1) - 1 \implies 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1) - (x^2 - 2)$$

So $a(x) = x - 1$. Evaluating at $x = \sqrt{2}$:
$$(1 + \sqrt{2})^{-1} = \sqrt{2} - 1$$

## Contrast: Algebraic vs Transcendental Numbers

- **Algebraic Number:** $\sqrt{2}$ is algebraic over $\mathbb{Q}$ because it is a root of $x^2 - 2 \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$. The degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}]$ is $2$.
- **Transcendental Number:** $\pi$ is transcendental over $\mathbb{Q}$ (proved by Lindemann in 1882). No non-zero polynomial with rational coefficients has $\pi$ as a root. The extension $[\mathbb{Q}(\pi):\mathbb{Q}]$ is infinite.
- **Open Problem:** It is unknown whether $\pi + e$ is algebraic or transcendental.
