# Functional Analysis

Functional analysis is the study of vector spaces endowed with topological structures (such as norms, metrics, or inner products) and the linear mappings (operators) acting between them. It generalizes linear algebra to infinite-dimensional spaces, providing the rigorous mathematical foundation for quantum mechanics, signal processing, and partial differential equations.

---

## Normed Spaces and Banach Spaces

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $\mathbb{F}$ (where $\mathbb{F}$ is $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$).

<MathStatement id="normed-space" kind="definition" title="Normed Space and Banach Space" number="1">

A **norm** on $V$ is a function $\|\cdot\|: V \to [0, \infty)$ satisfying:
1. **Positive Definiteness**: $\|v\| = 0 \iff v = 0$.
2. **Absolute Homogeneity**: $\|\alpha v\| = |\alpha| \|v\|$ for all $v \in V$, $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}$.
3. **Triangle Inequality**: $\|u + v\| \le \|u\| + \|v\|$ for all $u, v \in V$.

The pair $(V, \|\cdot\|)$ is a **normed space**.
A **Banach space** is a normed space that is **complete** with respect to the metric induced by the norm (i.e., every Cauchy sequence in $V$ converges to a limit in $V$).

</MathStatement>

## Hilbert Spaces and Inner Products

Hilbert spaces are Banach spaces where the norm is induced by an inner product, allowing for the concepts of angle, orthogonality, and projections.

<MathStatement id="hilbert-space" kind="definition" title="Inner Product and Hilbert Space" number="2">

An **inner product** on $V$ is a mapping $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: V \times V \to \mathbb{F}$ satisfying:
1. **Conjugate Symmetry**: $\langle u, v \rangle = \overline{\langle v, u \rangle}$.
2. **Linearity in the First Argument**: $\langle \alpha u + \beta v, w \rangle = \alpha \langle u, w \rangle + \beta \langle v, w \rangle$.
3. **Positive Definiteness**: $\langle v, v \rangle \ge 0$, with equality if and only if $v = 0$.

An inner product induces a norm $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v, v \rangle}$.
A **Hilbert space** $H$ is a complete inner product space.

</MathStatement>

A classic example of an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space is $L^2([a, b])$, the space of square-integrable functions with the inner product:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x) \overline{g(x)} \, dx.$$

## Bounded Linear Operators and the Dual Space

Linear mappings between normed spaces are called operators.

<MathStatement id="bounded-operator" kind="definition" title="Bounded Linear Operator" number="3">

Let $X$ and $Y$ be normed spaces. A linear operator $T: X \to Y$ is **bounded** if there exists a constant $M \ge 0$ such that:

$$\|Tx\|_Y \le M \|x\|_X \quad \text{for all } x \in X.$$

The **operator norm** of $T$, denoted $\|T\|$, is defined as:

$$\|T\| = \sup \left\{ \frac{\|Tx\|_Y}{\|x\|_X} \;\middle|\; x \in X, x \neq \mathbf{0} \right\} = \sup \{ \|Tx\|_Y \mid \|x\|_X = 1 \}.$$

</MathStatement>

An operator is bounded if and only if it is continuous.
If $Y = \mathbb{F}$ (the scalar field), a bounded linear operator is called a **bounded linear functional**. The space of all bounded linear functionals on $X$ is the **dual space** $X^*$.

For Hilbert spaces, the dual space is structurally isomorphic to the space itself.

<MathStatement id="riesz-representation" kind="theorem" title="Riesz Representation Theorem" number="1">

For every bounded linear functional $f$ on a Hilbert space $H$, there exists a unique vector $y \in H$ such that:

$$f(x) = \langle x, y \rangle \quad \text{for all } x \in H.$$

Furthermore, the norm of the functional equals the norm of the vector: $\|f\|_{H^*} = \|y\|_H$.

</MathStatement>

## Spectral Theory of Compact Operators

Spectral theory generalizes eigenvalues and eigenvectors to operators on infinite-dimensional spaces.

<MathStatement id="spectral-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Spectral Theorem for Compact Self-Adjoint Operators" number="2">

Let $T: H \to H$ be a compact, self-adjoint linear operator on a Hilbert space $H$. Then $H$ has an orthonormal basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$. The eigenvalues of $T$ are real, and the only possible limit point of the eigenvalues is 0.

</MathStatement>

Let us write a Python script using Legendre polynomials to demonstrate how functions in $L^2([-1, 1])$ can be projected orthogonally onto a finite-dimensional subspace spanned by orthonormal Legendre polynomials.

<CodeRunner client:load
  code={`import numpy as np
from scipy.special import legendre

# Legendre polynomials P_0, P_1, P_2 are orthogonal on [-1, 1]
# Normalization: ||P_n||^2 = 2 / (2n + 1).
# Orthonormal basis: phi_n(x) = sqrt((2n + 1)/2) * P_n(x)

def phi(n, x):
    coeff = np.sqrt((2*n + 1) / 2.0)
    return coeff * legendre(n)(x)

# Let's project f(x) = x^3 onto the span of {phi_0, phi_1, phi_2, phi_3}
# The projection is sum( c_n * phi_n ) where c_n = <f, phi_n>
x_grid = np.linspace(-1, 1, 1000)
dx = x_grid[1] - x_grid[0]
f = x_grid ** 3

c_coeffs = []
for n in range(4):
    integrand = f * phi(n, x_grid)
    c_n = np.trapz(integrand, x_grid) # L2 inner product
    c_coeffs.append(c_n)

# Reconstruct
projection = np.zeros_like(x_grid)
for n in range(4):
    projection += c_coeffs[n] * phi(n, x_grid)

# L2 error should be near zero since x^3 is in the span of polynomials up to degree 3
l2_error = np.sqrt(np.trapz((f - projection)**2, x_grid))
print(f"Legendre projection coefficients: {c_coeffs}")
print(f"Reconstruction L2 error: {l2_error:.10f}")
`}
