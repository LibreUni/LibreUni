# Group Actions and Orbit-Stabilizer

How do we study symmetries of structures that are not themselves groups? When a group of rotations acts on the vertices of a square, or when we color the faces of a cube, we see a group interacting with a set. This interaction is formalized as a **group action**.

## Formalization: Group Actions, Orbits, and Stabilizers

A **group action** of $G$ on a set $X$ is a function $G \times X \to X$, $(g, x) \mapsto g \cdot x$, satisfying:

1. **Identity:** $e \cdot x = x$ for all $x \in X$.
2. **Compatibility:** $(g h) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)$ for all $g, h \in G$, $x \in X$.

### Orbits

The **orbit** of $x \in X$ is $G \cdot x = \{ g \cdot x \mid g \in G \}$. Orbits partition $X$.

### Stabilizers

The **stabilizer** of $x \in X$ is $G_x = \{ g \in G \mid g \cdot x = x \}$. $G_x$ is always a subgroup of $G$.

---

## The Orbit-Stabilizer Theorem

> **Theorem (Orbit-Stabilizer):** Let $G$ be a finite group acting on a set $X$. For any $x \in X$:
> $$|G \cdot x| \cdot |G_x| = |G|$$
> Equivalently, $|G \cdot x| = [G : G_x]$, the index of the stabilizer.

*Proof:* Define a map $\phi: G/G_x \to G \cdot x$ by $\phi(g G_x) = g \cdot x$. We prove $\phi$ is a bijection.

1. **Well-defined:** If $g_1 G_x = g_2 G_x$, then $g_2 = g_1 h$ for some $h \in G_x$. Then $g_2 \cdot x = (g_1 h) \cdot x = g_1 \cdot (h \cdot x) = g_1 \cdot x$. So $\phi(g_1 G_x) = \phi(g_2 G_x)$.

2. **Injective:** If $\phi(g_1 G_x) = \phi(g_2 G_x)$, then $g_1 \cdot x = g_2 \cdot x$, so $(g_2^{-1} g_1) \cdot x = x$, hence $g_2^{-1} g_1 \in G_x$, so $g_1 G_x = g_2 G_x$.

3. **Surjective:** Every element of $G \cdot x$ is $g \cdot x = \phi(g G_x)$ by definition.

Since $|G/G_x| = [G : G_x] = |G|/|G_x|$, we have $|G \cdot x| = |G|/|G_x|$. $\square$

### Worked Example: Verifying on the Square

For $D_4$ acting on vertices, $|D_4| = 8$. For vertex 1:
- Orbit: $\{1,2,3,4\}$, size 4.
- Stabilizer: $\{R_0, D_1\}$, size 2.
- Check: $4 \cdot 2 = 8 = |D_4|$.

## Burnside's Lemma: Counting Orbits

How many distinct colorings of a square's vertices with 2 colors exist up to rotation?

> **Burnside's Lemma:** The number of orbits of a finite group $G$ acting on a finite set $X$ is:
> $$\text{Orbits} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |X^g|$$
> where $X^g = \{ x \in X \mid g \cdot x = x \}$.

---

## The Class Equation: Conjugation Action

An important action is $G$ acting on itself by **conjugation**: $g \cdot x = g x g^{-1}$.
- Orbit: the **conjugacy class** $Cl(x) = \{ g x g^{-1} \mid g \in G \}$.
- Stabilizer: the **centralizer** $C_G(x) = \{ g \in G \mid g x = x g \}$.

Partitioning $G$ into conjugacy classes gives the **Class Equation**:
$$|G| = |Z(G)| + \sum_{i=1}^r [G : C_G(g_i)]$$
where $Z(G)$ is the center (elements with singleton conjugacy classes), and $g_i$ represent classes of size $> 1$.

This equation is the bridge to the Sylow theorems: it lets us prove the existence of subgroups of prime-power order.

## Contrast: Transitive vs Non-Transitive Actions

- **Transitive:** A single orbit. $D_4$ acting on vertices is transitive.
- **Non-Transitive:** Multiple orbits. $D_4$ acting on 2-subsets of vertices has orbits $\{\{1,2\}, \{2,3\}, \{3,4\}, \{4,1\}\}$ and $\{\{1,3\}, \{2,4\}\}$.

Prove **Cauchy's Theorem for Abelian groups** using group actions: if a prime $p$ divides $|G|$ and $G$ is Abelian, then $G$ contains an element of order $p$.
