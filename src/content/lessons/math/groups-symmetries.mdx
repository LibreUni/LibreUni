# Groups and Symmetries

How do we mathematically define the intuitive concept of "symmetry"? When we look at a geometric shape, like an equilateral triangle, we say it is symmetric because we can rotate or reflect it, and it looks unchanged. 

But how do we compute with these actions? If we rotate a shape and then reflect it, what is the combined effect? Historically, mathematicians like Augustin-Louis Cauchy (1815) and Felix Klein (in his famous 1872 *Erlangen Program*) realized that geometry itself could be defined as the study of properties left invariant under a group of transformations. 

To formalize this, we must build a system where the actions themselves are the objects of study, and composition of actions is our operation.

---

## Symmetries of a Triangle

Consider an equilateral triangle with vertices labeled $1$, $2$, and $3$ in a fixed orientation. 

```
     1
    / \
   /   \
  2-----3
```

A **symmetry** of this triangle is a rigid motion (rotation or reflection) that places the triangle back onto its original footprint, though the vertex labels may swap positions. 

Let's list all possible symmetries of this triangle:
- **Rotations:**
  - $R_0$: Rotation by $0^\circ$ (the identity action, leaving everything unchanged).
  - $R_{120}$: Counterclockwise rotation by $120^\circ$.
  - $R_{240}$: Counterclockwise rotation by $240^\circ$.
- **Reflections:**
  - $S_1$: Reflection across the altitude passing through vertex $1$.
  - $S_2$: Reflection across the altitude passing through vertex $2$.
  - $S_3$: Reflection across the altitude passing through vertex $3$.

These $6$ actions form a set we call $D_3$, the **dihedral group of order 6** (representing the symmetries of a 3-sided regular polygon). The operation combining these actions is **composition** ($\circ$), where $A \circ B$ means "perform action $B$ first, then action $A$".

## From Equations to Abstraction

In the early 19th century, Lagrange and Ruffini studied permutations of roots of polynomial equations. However, the elements they manipulated were always concrete rearrangements of numbers.

In **1854**, the English mathematician **Arthur Cayley** made a monumental conceptual leap. He realized that the same algebraic laws applied whether the elements were permutations, geometric rotations, numbers, or matrices. Cayley published the first abstract definition of a group: a set with a single associative operation, an identity, and inverses. 

This shift—from studying *objects* to studying the *relations between objects*—defines the transition from classical algebra to modern **Abstract Algebra**.

---

## The Group Axioms

Let's state the precise algebraic definitions as they appear in modern mathematics.

A **group** is an ordered pair $(G, \cdot)$, where $G$ is a set and $\cdot$ is a binary operation on $G$ (meaning a function mapping $G \times G \to G$) that satisfies the following three axioms:

1. **Associativity:** For all $a, b, c \in G$,
   $$(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$$
2. **Identity Element:** There exists a unique element $e \in G$ (often written as $1$ or $e$) such that for all $a \in G$,
   $$e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$$
3. **Inverse Element:** For each $a \in G$, there exists an element $a^{-1} \in G$ such that
   $$a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$$

Additionally, the binary operation must be **closed** on $G$, meaning that for all $a, b \in G$, the product $a \cdot b$ is also in $G$.

### Abelian Groups
If a group also satisfies the commutativity property:
$$a \cdot b = b \cdot a \quad \text{for all } a, b \in G$$
we call it an **Abelian group** (named in honor of Niels Henrik Abel). If it does not, it is **non-Abelian**.

## Calculating with Symmetries

Let's learn how to calculate products and construct Cayley tables.

{/* Pedagogical step: I Do */}
### Worked Example

Let's write out the permutations of the vertices $\{1, 2, 3\}$ corresponding to the elements of $D_3$:
- $R_0 = (1)(2)(3)$ (Identity)
- $R_{120} = (1\ 2\ 3)$ (maps 1 to 2, 2 to 3, 3 to 1)
- $R_{240} = (1\ 3\ 2)$
- $S_1 = (2\ 3)$ (fixes 1, swaps 2 and 3)
- $S_2 = (1\ 3)$ (fixes 2, swaps 1 and 3)
- $S_3 = (1\ 2)$ (fixes 3, swaps 1 and 2)

Let's calculate the product $S_1 \circ R_{120}$ using cycle composition (evaluating from right to left):
1. Start with element $1$. $R_{120}$ maps $1 \to 2$. Then $S_1$ maps $2 \to 3$. Thus, the composite maps $1 \to 3$.
2. Track $3$. $R_{120}$ maps $3 \to 1$. Then $S_1$ maps $1 \to 1$ (fixed). Thus, the composite maps $3 \to 1$.
3. Track $2$. $R_{120}$ maps $2 \to 3$. Then $S_1$ maps $3 \to 2$. Thus, the composite maps $2 \to 2$ (fixed).

The resulting permutation is $(1\ 3)$, which is exactly $S_2$. 
*Wait, why did this happen?* When we composed permutations on paper, we got $S_2$. But in our physical triangle simulation, the composite action was $S_3$. This difference depends on whether we compose permutations as **active physical movements of the triangle** or as **passive coordinate shifts**. Both represent $D_3$, but they are inverse to each other!

Let's use Python to print the complete Cayley table of $D_3$.

### Completing the Symmetries

Let's complete the composition of symmetries of $D_3$. Suppose we want to find the product $S_2 \circ S_1$. 
Using cycle composition:
- $S_1 = (2\ 3)$
- $S_2 = (1\ 3)$

Let's trace:
- $1 \to S_1 \to 1 \to S_2 \to 3$
- $3 \to S_1 \to 2 \to S_2 \to 2$ (Since $S_2$ fixes 2, $2 \to 2$)
- $2 \to S_1 \to 3 \to S_2 \to 1$

So the composition maps $1 \to 3$, $3 \to 2$, and $2 \to 1$. In cycle notation, this is $(1\ 3\ 2)$, which is $R_{240}$.

Now it's your turn to compute $S_3 \circ S_1$. Trace the elements:
- $1 \to S_1 \to 1 \to S_3 \to 2$
- $2 \to S_1 \to 3 \to S_3 \to 3$
- $3 \to S_1 \to 2 \to S_3 \to 1$

## Group Boundaries and Edge Cases

To master what a group is, we must study its boundaries.

| System | Operation | Associative? | Identity? | Inverses? | Is Group? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | Addition ($+$) | Yes | Yes ($0$) | Yes ($-x$) | **Yes (Abelian)** |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | Multiplication ($\cdot$) | Yes | Yes ($1$) | No (e.g. $2^{-1} \notin \mathbb{Z}$) | **No (Monoid)** |
| $\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$ | Multiplication ($\cdot$) | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes ($1/x$) | **Yes (Abelian)** |
| $D_3$ | Composition ($\circ$) | Yes | Yes ($R_0$) | Yes | **Yes (non-Abelian)** |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | Subtraction ($-$) | No | No | No | **No** |

### Core Definitions and Boundaries
- **Standard Case:** $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ is the standard infinite Abelian group.
- **Non-Obvious Case:** Symmetries of a geometric shape under composition (which are functions, not numbers).
- **Near-Miss:** $(\mathbb{Z}, \cdot)$. It satisfies closure, associativity, and identity, but lacks inverses for all elements other than $1$ and $-1$.
- **Edge Case:** The set containing only a single element $\{e\}$ under the operation $e \cdot e = e$ is a valid group of order 1.
