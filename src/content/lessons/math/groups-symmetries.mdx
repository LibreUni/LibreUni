# Groups and Symmetries

How do we mathematically define symmetry? When we look at an equilateral triangle, we say it is symmetric because certain rigid motions—rotations and reflections—map the triangle back onto itself. The set of all such motions, together with the operation of composing them one after another, forms an algebraic structure called a **group**.

This insight—treating *actions* as the objects of study and *composition* as the operation—originated with Augustin-Louis Cauchy (1815) and was crystallized by Felix Klein in his 1872 *Erlangen Program*, which defined geometry as the study of properties invariant under a group of transformations.

## A Concrete Problem: Symmetries of a Triangle

Consider an equilateral triangle whose corners are labeled with the numbers $1$, $2$, and $3$ in fixed positions. A **symmetry** of this triangle is a rigid motion (rotation or reflection) that places the triangle back onto its original outline, though the labels may move to different positions.

List all possible symmetries:

- **Rotations:**
  - $R_0$: Rotation by $0^\circ$ (the identity — nothing moves).
  - $R_{120}$: Counterclockwise rotation by $120^\circ$.
  - $R_{240}$: Counterclockwise rotation by $240^\circ$.
- **Reflections:**
  - $S_1$: Reflection across the altitude through vertex $1$.
  - $S_2$: Reflection across the altitude through vertex $2$.
  - $S_3$: Reflection across the altitude through vertex $3$.

These six actions form the **dihedral group** $D_3$ (order 6: the symmetry group of a regular 3-gon). The operation is composition ($\circ$), where $A \circ B$ means "do $B$ first, then $A$".

### Predicting a Composition

## From Concrete Actions to Abstract Axioms

Through the early 19th century, mathematicians like Lagrange and Ruffini manipulated specific permutations of polynomial roots. In **1854**, Arthur Cayley made a conceptual leap: the same algebraic laws held whether the elements were permutations, geometric rotations, or matrices. He published the first abstract definition of a group.

### The Group Axioms

A **group** is an ordered pair $(G, \cdot)$, where $G$ is a set and $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ is a binary operation satisfying:

1. **Associativity:** For all $a, b, c \in G$,
   $$(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$$
2. **Identity:** There exists an element $e \in G$ such that for all $a \in G$,
   $$e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a$$
3. **Inverses:** For each $a \in G$, there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that
   $$a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$$

These axioms imply two immediate consequences that are worth proving directly:

> **Proposition (Uniqueness of Identity):** The identity element of a group is unique.
>
> *Proof:* Suppose $e$ and $e'$ are both identities. Then $e = e \cdot e'$ (since $e'$ is an identity) and $e \cdot e' = e'$ (since $e$ is an identity). Hence $e = e'$.

> **Proposition (Uniqueness of Inverses):** Each element of a group has a unique inverse.
>
> *Proof:* Suppose $b$ and $c$ are both inverses of $a$. Then $b = b \cdot e = b \cdot (a \cdot c) = (b \cdot a) \cdot c = e \cdot c = c$.

### Abelian Groups

If a group additionally satisfies commutativity:
$$a \cdot b = b \cdot a \quad \text{for all } a, b \in G$$
we call it an **Abelian group** (for Niels Henrik Abel). Otherwise it is **non-Abelian**. $D_3$ is non-Abelian: for instance, $S_1 \circ R_{120} = S_2$ but $R_{120} \circ S_1 = S_3 \neq S_2$.

## Computing with Symmetries

Let us translate each symmetry of $D_3$ into a permutation of the three vertex positions $\{1, 2, 3\}$ (the **passive** convention: we permute the labels while the triangle outline stays fixed):

- $R_0 = (1)(2)(3)$ (identity)
- $R_{120} = (1\ 2\ 3)$
- $R_{240} = (1\ 3\ 2)$
- $S_1 = (2\ 3)$ (fixes 1, swaps 2 and 3)
- $S_2 = (1\ 3)$
- $S_3 = (1\ 2)$

### Cayley Table of $D_3$

The Cayley table of a finite group lists every product $a \circ b$ (row $\circ$ column). Here it is for $D_3$:

| $\circ$ | $R_0$ | $R_{120}$ | $R_{240}$ | $S_1$ | $S_2$ | $S_3$ |
|---------|-------|-----------|-----------|-------|-------|-------|
| $R_0$   | $R_0$ | $R_{120}$ | $R_{240}$ | $S_1$ | $S_2$ | $S_3$ |
| $R_{120}$ | $R_{120}$ | $R_{240}$ | $R_0$ | $S_2$ | $S_3$ | $S_1$ |
| $R_{240}$ | $R_{240}$ | $R_0$ | $R_{120}$ | $S_3$ | $S_1$ | $S_2$ |
| $S_1$   | $S_1$ | $S_3$ | $S_2$ | $R_0$ | $R_{240}$ | $R_{120}$ |
| $S_2$   | $S_2$ | $S_1$ | $S_3$ | $R_{120}$ | $R_0$ | $R_{240}$ |
| $S_3$   | $S_3$ | $S_2$ | $S_1$ | $R_{240}$ | $R_{120}$ | $R_0$ |

Observe: every row and column is a permutation of the six elements. This is always true for finite groups — it follows from the cancellation law.

### Pattern Recognition

Notice a recurring structure in the Cayley table:

1. **Any reflection composed with itself is the identity:** $S_k \circ S_k = R_0$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$.
2. **Two distinct reflections compose to a rotation:** $S_i \circ S_j$ with $i \neq j$ is always one of $\{R_{120}, R_{240}\}$.
3. **The rotation subset $\{R_0, R_{120}, R_{240}\}$ is closed:** any product of two rotations is another rotation.

These patterns are not coincidental — they reflect the underlying geometry of $D_3$ and will generalize to all dihedral groups $D_n$.

## Boundaries and Edge Cases

Knowing what a group is requires knowing what is *not* a group.

| System | Operation | Associative? | Identity? | Inverses? | Is Group? | Reason |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $+$ | Yes | Yes ($0$) | Yes ($-x$) | **Yes (Abelian)** | |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $\cdot$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | No (e.g. $2^{-1} \notin \mathbb{Z}$) | **No (Monoid)** | Fails inverses |
| $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ | $\cdot$ | Yes | Yes ($1$) | Yes ($1/x$) | **Yes (Abelian)** | |
| $D_3$ | $\circ$ | Yes | Yes ($R_0$) | Yes | **Yes (non-Abelian)** | |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | $-$ | No ($1-(2-3) \neq (1-2)-3$) | No | No | **No** | Fails associativity |
| $\mathbb{N} = \{0,1,2,\dots\}$ | $+$ | Yes | Yes ($0$) | No ($1$ has no inverse in $\mathbb{N}$) | **No** | Fails inverses |

A few more instructive examples:

- **The trivial group:** $(\{e\}, \cdot)$ with $e \cdot e = e$ is a valid group of order $1$. Every axiom holds trivially.
- **A non-example with two failures:** $(\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}, +)$ contains the identity $0$ but lacks inverses for any positive element. It satisfies only associativity and identity.
- **Why associativity matters:** Subtraction on $\mathbb{Z}$ fails associativity: $(1 - 2) - 3 = -4$ but $1 - (2 - 3) = 2$.
