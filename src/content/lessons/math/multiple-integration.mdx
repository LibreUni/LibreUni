# Multiple Integration

Multiple integration extends the concept of Riemann integration to functions of several variables, allowing for the calculation of volumes, masses, centroids, and moments of inertia in two- and three-dimensional domains.

---

## Double and Triple Integrals

We define double integrals by partitioning a 2D domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ into small subrectangles of area $\Delta A_i = \Delta x_i \Delta y_i$.

<MathStatement id="double-integral" kind="definition" title="Double Integral" number="1">

Let $f(x, y)$ be a bounded function defined on a closed, bounded region $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$. The **double integral** of $f$ over $D$ is defined as the limit of Riemann sums:

$$\iint_D f(x, y) \, dA = \lim_{\Delta \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*, y_i^*) \Delta A_i,$$

provided this limit exists and is independent of the partition choice and sample point selection.

</MathStatement>

Evaluating a double integral directly from the definition is rarely done. Instead, we compute them as iterated single-variable integrals using Fubini's Theorem.

<MathStatement id="fubinis-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Fubini's Theorem" number="1">

If $f(x, y)$ is continuous on the rectangular region $R = [a, b] \times [c, d]$, then:

$$\iint_R f(x, y) \, dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x, y) \, dy \, dx = \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x, y) \, dx \, dy.$$

</MathStatement>

If the region is non-rectangular, we define it using functional bounds (e.g., $y \in [g_1(x), g_2(x)]$), and evaluate:

$$\iint_D f(x, y) \, dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x, y) \, dy \, dx.$$

## Change of Variables and the Jacobian

When integrating over circular, cylindrical, or spherical regions, cartesian coordinates are often highly inefficient. Changing variables requires scaling the differential volume element using the Jacobian determinant.

<MathStatement id="change-of-variables" kind="theorem" title="Change of Variables in Double Integrals" number="2">

Let $T: D^* \to D$ be a continuously differentiable, one-to-one transformation mapping a region $D^*$ in the $uv$-plane to a region $D$ in the $xy$-plane, defined by $x = g(u, v)$ and $y = h(u, v)$.
Then:

$$\iint_D f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = \iint_{D^*} f(g(u, v), h(u, v)) \left| \frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)} \right| \, du \, dv,$$

where the **Jacobian determinant** (or simply Jacobian) is defined as:

$$\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(u, v)} = \det \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{pmatrix}.$$

</MathStatement>

## Coordinate Transformations and their Jacobians

Let us compute the Jacobians for the three classical coordinate transformations.

### 1. Polar Coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^2$

Let $x = r \cos(\theta)$ and $y = r \sin(\theta)$. Differentiating:

$$\frac{\partial(x, y)}{\partial(r, \theta)} = \det \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -r\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & r\cos(\theta) \end{pmatrix} = r(\cos^2(\theta) + \sin^2(\theta)) = r.$$

Thus, the area element in polar coordinates is $dA = r \, dr \, d\theta$.

### 2. Cylindrical Coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $x = r \cos(\theta)$, $y = r \sin(\theta)$, and $z = z$. The volume element is:

$$dV = r \, dr \, d\theta \, dz.$$

### 3. Spherical Coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $x = \rho \sin(\phi) \cos(\theta)$, $y = \rho \sin(\phi) \sin(\theta)$, and $z = \rho \cos(\phi)$, where $\phi$ is the polar angle and $\theta$ is the azimuthal angle. The volume element is:

$$dV = \rho^2 \sin(\phi) \, d\rho \, d\theta \, d\phi.$$

Let us write a Python script using `scipy.integrate.dblquad` to evaluate the double integral of $f(x, y) = x^2 y$ over the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 \le 1$ using polar coordinates, where $x^2 y = r^3 \cos^2(\theta) \sin(\theta)$, and compare it to cartesian integration.

<CodeRunner client:load

cartesian_val, _ = dblquad(lambda y, x: x**2 * y, -1.0, 1.0,

                       lambda theta: 0.0, lambda theta: 1.0)

print(f"Cartesian result: {cartesian_val:.10f}")
print(f"Polar result: {polar_val:.10f}")
print("Both integrals yield 0 because the function x^2*y is odd in y and the circle is symmetric.")
