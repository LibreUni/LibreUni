# Multivariable Calculus

Multivariable calculus extends single-variable analysis to functions mapping $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{R}^m$, enabling the study of multidimensional spaces, physical fields, and optimization surfaces.

---

## Limits and Continuity in $\mathbb{R}^n$

For multivariable functions, limits are more complex than in single variable because $x$ can approach $c$ from infinitely many directions.

<MathStatement id="multivariable-limit" kind="definition" title="Limit of a Multivariable Function" number="1">

Let $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function, and let $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a limit point of $D$. We say:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{c}} f(\mathbf{x}) = L$$

if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that for all $\mathbf{x} \in D$:

$$0 < \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}\| < \delta \implies |f(\mathbf{x}) - L| < \epsilon$$

where $\|\cdot\|$ denotes the standard Euclidean norm in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

</MathStatement>

For the limit to exist, $f(\mathbf{x})$ must approach $L$ along *every* possible continuous path approaching $\mathbf{c}$. If two different paths yield different limit values, the limit does not exist.

## Partial Derivatives and Differentiability

A partial derivative is a derivative taken with respect to one variable, treating all other variables as constants.

<MathStatement id="partial-derivative" kind="definition" title="Partial Derivative" number="2">

Let $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$. The **partial derivative** of $f$ with respect to $x$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0, y_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0+h, y_0) - f(x_0, y_0)}{h}.$$

Similarly, the partial derivative with respect to $y$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0) = \lim_{k \to 0} \frac{f(x_0, y_0+k) - f(x_0, y_0)}{k}.$$

</MathStatement>

Unlike single-variable calculus, the existence of partial derivatives does *not* imply differentiability or even continuity. Differentiability in $\mathbb{R}^n$ requires the existence of a linear transformation that locally approximates the function.

<MathStatement id="multivariable-differentiability" kind="definition" title="Differentiability in R^n" number="3">

Let $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $\mathbf{a}$ be an interior point of $D$. We say $f$ is **differentiable** at $\mathbf{a}$ if there exists a linear transformation $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ (represented by a vector $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ as $T(\mathbf{h}) = \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{h}$) such that:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{h} \to \mathbf{0}} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{h}) - f(\mathbf{a}) - \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{h}}{\|\mathbf{h}\|} = 0.$$

If $f$ is differentiable, then $\mathbf{A}$ is uniquely the **gradient** vector:

$$\mathbf{A} = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{a}), \dots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{a}) \right).$$

</MathStatement>

## Tangent Planes and the Multivariable Chain Rule

For a differentiable function $f(x, y)$ at $(a, b)$, the best linear approximation defines a **tangent plane**:

$$z = f(a, b) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a, b)(x - a) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(a, b)(y - b).$$

### The Multivariable Chain Rule

If $z = f(x, y)$ is differentiable, and $x = g(t)$, $y = h(t)$ are differentiable functions of $t$, then $z$ is a differentiable function of $t$, and:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{dy}{dt}.$$

## Directional Derivatives and the Gradient

The gradient vector points in the direction of steepest ascent on the surface defined by $f$.

<MathStatement id="directional-derivative" kind="definition" title="Directional Derivative" number="4">

Let $f$ be differentiable at $\mathbf{x}$, and let $\mathbf{u}$ be a unit vector. The **directional derivative** of $f$ in the direction of $\mathbf{u}$ is:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{x}) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{x} + t\mathbf{u}) - f(\mathbf{x})}{t} = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{u}.$$

Since $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{u} = \|\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\| \cos(\theta)$, the directional derivative is maximized when $\theta = 0$, meaning $\mathbf{u}$ is parallel to $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$.

</MathStatement>

Level curves are perpendicular to the gradient: the gradient points across contours rather than along them.

Let us write a Python script to compute the gradient of $f(x, y) = x^2 y + \sin(x)$ at $(1.0, 2.0)$ numerically and verify the directional derivative is maximized along the gradient direction.

<CodeRunner client:load
  code={`import numpy as np

def f(x, y):
    return x**2 * y + np.sin(x)

# Numerical gradient using central difference
def numerical_gradient(f, x, y, h=1e-5):
    dfdx = (f(x+h, y) - f(x-h, y)) / (2*h)
    dfdy = (f(x, y+h) - f(x, y-h)) / (2*h)
    return np.array([dfdx, dfdy])

x0, y0 = 1.0, 2.0
grad = numerical_gradient(f, x0, y0)
print(f"Gradient vector at (1, 2): {grad}")

# Let's test two directions: along gradient and perpendicular to it
u_along = grad / np.linalg.norm(grad)
u_perp = np.array([-u_along[1], u_along[0]])

deriv_along = np.dot(grad, u_along)
deriv_perp = np.dot(grad, u_perp)

print(f"Directional derivative (along gradient): {deriv_along:.6f}")
print(f"Directional derivative (perpendicular): {deriv_perp:.6f} (Should be 0)")

## Second Derivative Test (Hessian Matrix)

To identify local extrema in multivariable functions, we use the eigenvalues or determinant of the matrix of second partial derivatives.

<MathStatement id="hessian-test" kind="theorem" title="Second Derivative Test in R^2" number="1">

Let $f(x, y)$ have continuous second partial derivatives on a neighborhood of a critical point $(a, b)$ (where $\nabla f(a, b) = \mathbf{0}$). Define the **Hessian matrix** $H$ and its determinant $D$:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{yx} & f_{yy} \end{pmatrix}, \quad D = \det(H) = f_{xx} f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2.$$

Then:
1. If $D > 0$ and $f_{xx}(a, b) > 0$, then $f(a, b)$ is a local minimum.
2. If $D > 0$ and $f_{xx}(a, b) < 0$, then $f(a, b)$ is a local maximum.
3. If $D < 0$, then $(a, b)$ is a **saddle point**.
4. If $D = 0$, the test is inconclusive.

</MathStatement>
