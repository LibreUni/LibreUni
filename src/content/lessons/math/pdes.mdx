# Partial Differential Equations

Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) describe physical systems where states depend on multiple independent variables (typically space coordinates $x, y, z$ and time $t$). Unlike ODEs, which govern rates of change along a single trajectory, PDEs govern fields across continuous domains.

---

## Classification of Second-Order Linear PDEs

A second-order linear PDE in two variables has the general form:

$$A \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + B \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x \partial y} + C \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} + D \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + E \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} + Fu = G(x, y).$$

<MathStatement id="pde-classification" kind="definition" title="PDE Classification" number="1">

The classification of a second-order linear PDE is determined by the discriminant $B^2 - 4AC$:
1. **Elliptic**: If $B^2 - 4AC < 0$. (Example: Laplace's Equation $\Delta u = 0$). Represents steady-state or equilibrium systems.
2. **Parabolic**: If $B^2 - 4AC = 0$. (Example: The Heat Equation $u_t = \alpha \Delta u$). Represents dissipative or diffusion systems.
3. **Hyperbolic**: If $B^2 - 4AC > 0$. (Example: The Wave Equation $u_{tt} = c^2 \Delta u$). Represents propagation or wave systems.

</MathStatement>

## Boundary and Initial Conditions

For a PDE to have a unique solution, it must be constrained by conditions on the boundary of the spatial domain $\partial \Omega$ and initial states at $t = 0$.

- **Dirichlet Boundary Condition**: The value of the function is specified on the boundary: $u(\mathbf{x}, t) = g(\mathbf{x})$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \partial \Omega$.
- **Neumann Boundary Condition**: The normal derivative (flux) is specified on the boundary: $\frac{\partial u}{\partial n}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \nabla u \cdot \mathbf{n} = g(\mathbf{x})$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \partial \Omega$.

## Analytical Method: Separation of Variables

For simple geometries, we solve PDEs by assuming the solution is a product of single-variable functions.

Let us solve the 1D Heat Equation on $[0, L]$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions:

$$u_t = k u_{xx}, \quad u(0, t) = u(L, t) = 0, \quad u(x, 0) = f(x).$$

We assume a product solution: $u(x, t) = X(x)T(t)$. Substituting into the PDE:

$$X(x)T'(t) = k X''(x)T(t) \iff \frac{T'(t)}{k T(t)} = \frac{X''(x)}{X(x)} = -\lambda$$

where $-\lambda$ is a separation constant (must be constant because the left side depends only on $t$ and the right only on $x$). This yields two coupled ODEs:

1. $X''(x) + \lambda X(x) = 0, \quad X(0) = X(L) = 0$.
2. $T'(t) + \lambda k T(t) = 0$.

For non-trivial solutions of the spatial ODE, we must have $\lambda > 0$. Solving $X''(x) + \lambda X(x) = 0$ yields:

$$X(x) = A \cos(\sqrt{\lambda} x) + B \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} x).$$

Applying boundary conditions:
- $X(0) = 0 \implies A = 0$.
- $X(L) = 0 \implies B \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} L) = 0 \implies \sqrt{\lambda} L = n\pi \implies \lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

The corresponding temporal solution is $T_n(t) = C_n e^{-\lambda_n k t}$. By superposition, the general solution is:

$$u(x, t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty B_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-\left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2 k t},$$

where $B_n$ are the Fourier coefficients of the initial condition $f(x)$.

## Numerical Solution: Finite Difference Method (FTCS)

We discretize space and time on a grid. Let $u_i^n \approx u(i\Delta x, n\Delta t)$. Using forward difference in time and central difference in space (FTCS scheme):

$$\frac{u_i^{n+1} - u_i^n}{\Delta t} = k \frac{u_{i+1}^n - 2u_i^n + u_{i-1}^n}{\Delta x^2} \implies u_i^{n+1} = u_i^n + \frac{k \Delta t}{\Delta x^2} (u_{i+1}^n - 2u_i^n + u_{i-1}^n).$$

For numerical stability, the time step must satisfy the Von Neumann stability criterion: $\frac{k \Delta t}{\Delta x^2} \le \frac{1}{2}$.

Let us write a Python script to simulate 1D heat diffusion in a rod of length $L = 1.0$ with thermal diffusivity $k = 0.05$ and verify that the hot spot in the center decays over time.

L = 1.0
nx = 21
dx = L / (nx - 1)
k = 0.05
dt = 0.001 # dt <= 0.5 * dx^2 / k = 0.5 * 0.0025 / 0.05 = 0.025 (stable)

# Initial condition: hot spot in the middle
u = np.zeros(nx)
u[nx//2] = 100.0

# Integrate 50 steps

    u_new = u.copy()
    for i in range(1, nx - 1):

    u = u_new

print(f"Temperature at endpoints: {u[0]:.4f}, {u[-1]:.4f}")
print(f"Temperature at rod center: {u[nx//2]:.4f}")
`}
