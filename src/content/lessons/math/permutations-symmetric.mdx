# Permutations and Symmetric Groups

Can every finite group structure, no matter how abstract, be understood simply as a set of permutations?

In early group theory, mathematicians studied concrete rearrangements of objects, which we call **permutations**. This concrete view is entirely general: every finite group behaves exactly like a collection of permutations.

## Permutation Composition

Suppose you have three cups in a row, labeled $1$, $2$, and $3$.
- Action $\sigma$ swaps cups $1$ and $2$.
- Action $\tau$ shifts all cups one position to the right ($1 \to 2$, $2 \to 3$, $3 \to 1$).

Performing $\tau$ first then $\sigma$: trace cup $1$:
1. Start: Cup 1 at position 1.
2. Apply $\tau$: Cup 1 moves to position 2.
3. Apply $\sigma$: Position 2 swapped with position 1. Cup 1 returns to position 1.

We compose permutations right-to-left: $(\sigma \circ \tau)(x) = \sigma(\tau(x))$.

## The Symmetric Group

Let $\Omega$ be a non-empty set. A **permutation** of $\Omega$ is a bijection $\sigma: \Omega \to \Omega$. The set of all permutations of $\{1, \dots, n\}$ forms a group under composition, the **Symmetric Group of degree $n$**, denoted $S_n$. Identity is the trivial permutation fixing every element; inverses exist because each $\sigma$ is bijective. The order of $S_n$ is $n!$.

### Cycle Decomposition Theorem

**Theorem 1.** Every permutation $\sigma \in S_n$ can be expressed uniquely as a product of disjoint cycles, up to the order of the cycles.

*Proof.* Pick $a \in \{1,\dots,n\}$ and apply $\sigma$ repeatedly. Because the set is finite, the sequence $a, \sigma(a), \sigma^2(a), \dots$ must eventually repeat. Let $k > 0$ be minimal with $\sigma^k(a) = a$. The $k$ distinct elements $\{a, \sigma(a), \dots, \sigma^{k-1}(a)\}$ form the cycle $(a\; \sigma(a)\; \dots\; \sigma^{k-1}(a))$.

Repeat on any element not yet in a cycle. Each orbit becomes a disjoint cycle. Uniqueness follows because cycles are exactly the orbits of $\sigma$, independent of the chosen starting element. $\square$

**Example.** $\sigma \in S_6$ defined by $\sigma(1)=3$, $\sigma(2)=5$, $\sigma(3)=1$, $\sigma(4)=4$, $\sigma(5)=6$, $\sigma(6)=2$.
- Orbit of $1$: $1 \to 3 \to 1$ → cycle $(1\ 3)$
- Orbit of $2$: $2 \to 5 \to 6 \to 2$ → cycle $(2\ 5\ 6)$
- Orbit of $4$: $4 \to 4$ → fixed point, omitted

Thus $\sigma = (1\ 3)(2\ 5\ 6)$.

### Computing Products in Cycle Notation

Composing permutations written in cycle notation requires tracking each element's image through the composition, working right-to-left.

**Problem.** Compute $\sigma\tau$ in $S_5$ where $\sigma = (1\ 2\ 3)$ and $\tau = (1\ 4)(2\ 5\ 3)$.

*Solution.* Compose right-to-left: $(\sigma\tau)(x) = \sigma(\tau(x))$.

| $x$ | $\tau(x)$ | $\sigma(\tau(x))$ |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| 1   | 4         | 4                 |
| 4   | 1         | 2                 |
| 2   | 5         | 5                 |
| 5   | 3         | 1                 |
| 3   | 2         | 3                 |

Element $3$ is fixed. The orbit $1 \to 4 \to 2 \to 5 \to 1$ gives $\sigma\tau = (1\ 4\ 2\ 5)$.

**Problem.** Compute $\tau\sigma$ with the same permutations.

*Solution.* $(\tau\sigma)(x) = \tau(\sigma(x))$.

| $x$ | $\sigma(x)$ | $\tau(\sigma(x))$ |
|-----|-------------|-------------------|
| 1   | 2           | 5                 |
| 5   | 5           | 3                 |
| 3   | 1           | 4                 |
| 4   | 4           | 1                 |
| 2   | 3           | 2                 |

Thus $\tau\sigma = (1\ 5\ 3\ 4)$. Since $\sigma\tau \neq \tau\sigma$, $S_n$ is non-abelian for $n \ge 3$.

## Transpositions and Parity

A **transposition** is a cycle of length $2$, $(a\ b)$. Transpositions are the elementary building blocks of all permutations.

**Theorem 2 (Generation by Transpositions).** Every permutation can be expressed as a product of transpositions. In particular,
$$(a_1\; a_2\; \dots\; a_k) = (a_1\; a_k)(a_1\; a_{k-1})\cdots(a_1\; a_2).$$

*Proof.* Compose right-to-left. The rightmost factor $(a_1\; a_2)$ sends $a_1 \to a_2$ and $a_2 \to a_1$. Each subsequent $(a_1\; a_j)$ sends $a_1$ to $a_j$ while placing the displaced element back into the cycle. Tracking confirms the composite map equals the original $k$-cycle. $\square$

**Corollary.** $S_n$ is generated by transpositions. Moreover, the $n-1$ adjacent transpositions
$$(1\; 2), (2\; 3), \dots, (n-1\; n)$$
suffice to generate $S_n$.

*Proof.* Any transposition $(a\; b)$ with $a < b$ decomposes as a product of adjacent transpositions: $(a\; b) = (a\; a+1)(a+1\; a+2)\cdots(b-1\; b)\cdots(a+1\; a+2)(a\; a+1)$. $\square$

**Example.** Decompose $(1\ 2\ 3\ 4)$ into transpositions.
$$(1\ 2\ 3\ 4) = (1\ 4)(1\ 3)(1\ 2)$$

Verification by composition:
- $1 \xrightarrow{(1\ 2)} 2 \xrightarrow{(1\ 3)} 2 \xrightarrow{(1\ 4)} 2$ → $1 \to 2$ ✓
- $2 \xrightarrow{(1\ 2)} 1 \xrightarrow{(1\ 3)} 3 \xrightarrow{(1\ 4)} 3$ → $2 \to 3$ ✓
- $3 \xrightarrow{(1\ 2)} 3 \xrightarrow{(1\ 3)} 1 \xrightarrow{(1\ 4)} 4$ → $3 \to 4$ ✓
- $4 \xrightarrow{(1\ 2)} 4 \xrightarrow{(1\ 3)} 4 \xrightarrow{(1\ 4)} 1$ → $4 \to 1$ ✓

### Parity and the Sign Homomorphism

A permutation admits many different transposition factorizations, but the parity of the number of transpositions is invariant.

**Theorem 3 (Parity Invariance).** If $\sigma = \tau_1\tau_2\cdots\tau_k = \tau'_1\tau'_2\cdots\tau'_\ell$ are two expressions of $\sigma$ as products of transpositions, then $k \equiv \ell \pmod{2}$.

*Proof.* Consider the discriminant polynomial
$$D(x_1,\dots,x_n) = \prod_{1\le i<j\le n} (x_i - x_j).$$
For $\sigma \in S_n$, define
$$\sigma(D)(x_1,\dots,x_n) = D(x_{\sigma(1)},\dots,x_{\sigma(n)}) = \prod_{i<j} (x_{\sigma(i)} - x_{\sigma(j)}).$$

Each factor $(x_{\sigma(i)} - x_{\sigma(j)})$ equals $\pm(x_p - x_q)$ for some $p < q$, hence $\sigma(D) = \pm D$.

For a transposition $\tau = (a\ b)$ with $a < b$:
- The factor $(x_a - x_b)$ becomes $(x_b - x_a) = -(x_a - x_b)$.
- For any $k \neq a,b$: the pair $(x_a - x_k)(x_b - x_k)$ maps to $(x_b - x_k)(x_a - x_k)$, identical as a product.
Thus $\tau(D) = -D$.

If $\sigma = \tau_1\cdots\tau_k$, then $\sigma(D) = (-1)^k D$. If also $\sigma = \tau'_1\cdots\tau'_\ell$, then $(-1)^k = (-1)^\ell$, so $k \equiv \ell \pmod{2}$. $\square$

**Definition.** The **sign** of $\sigma$ is $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = (-1)^k$ where $\sigma$ is a product of $k$ transpositions. A permutation is **even** if $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = 1$ and **odd** if $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = -1$.

**Theorem 4 (Sign Homomorphism).** The map $\operatorname{sgn}: S_n \to \{\pm 1\}$ is a surjective group homomorphism. For any $\sigma,\tau \in S_n$,
$$\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma\tau) = \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\operatorname{sgn}(\tau).$$

*Proof.* Write $\sigma = \tau_1\cdots\tau_k$ and $\tau = \tau_{k+1}\cdots\tau_{k+\ell}$. Then $\sigma\tau = \tau_1\cdots\tau_{k+\ell}$, so
$$\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma\tau) = (-1)^{k+\ell} = (-1)^k(-1)^\ell = \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\operatorname{sgn}(\tau).$$
Surjectivity: the transposition $(1\ 2)$ maps to $-1$. $\square$

The kernel of $\operatorname{sgn}$ is the **Alternating Group** $A_n$ — the set of all even permutations.

**Corollary.** $A_n$ is a normal subgroup of $S_n$ of index $2$, hence $|A_n| = n!/2$.

*Proof.* $A_n = \ker(\operatorname{sgn})$ is a subgroup. Since $\operatorname{sgn}$ is surjective, $S_n/A_n \cong \{\pm 1\}$, so $[S_n : A_n] = 2$ and $|A_n| = n!/2$. Normality follows because kernels of homomorphisms are always normal. $\square$

### Worked Example: Computing Sign

**Problem.** Find the sign of $\sigma = (1\ 3\ 5)(2\ 4)(6\ 7\ 8\ 9) \in S_9$.

*Solution (method 1 — transposition count).*
- $(1\ 3\ 5) = (1\ 5)(1\ 3)$ — 2 transpositions
- $(2\ 4)$ — 1 transposition
- $(6\ 7\ 8\ 9) = (6\ 9)(6\ 8)(6\ 7)$ — 3 transpositions
Total: $2 + 1 + 3 = 6$, so $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = (-1)^6 = 1$ (even).

*Solution (method 2 — cycle parity formula).* A $k$-cycle has sign $(-1)^{k-1}$.
- $(1\ 3\ 5)$: $k=3$, sign $(-1)^2 = 1$
- $(2\ 4)$: $k=2$, sign $(-1)^1 = -1$
- $(6\ 7\ 8\ 9)$: $k=4$, sign $(-1)^3 = -1$
Product: $1 \cdot (-1) \cdot (-1) = 1$. $\sigma$ is even.

## Cayley's Theorem: Symmetries as Universal Building Blocks

Arthur Cayley proved that symmetric groups are universal.

**Theorem 5 (Cayley).** Every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of the symmetric group $S_G$ (the group of permutations of the set $G$). If $G$ is finite of order $n$, then $G$ embeds into $S_n$.

*Proof.* For each $g \in G$, define the left-multiplication map $\phi_g: G \to G$ by $\phi_g(x) = gx$. Because group elements have inverses, $\phi_g$ is bijective — a permutation of $G$. Define $\Phi: G \to S_G$ by $\Phi(g) = \phi_g$.

Check $\Phi$ is a homomorphism: $\Phi(gh)(x) = (gh)x = g(hx) = \phi_g(\phi_h(x)) = (\Phi(g) \circ \Phi(h))(x)$, so $\Phi(gh) = \Phi(g)\Phi(h)$.

Injectivity: if $\Phi(g) = \operatorname{id}$, then $gx = x$ for all $x \in G$, so $g = e$. Hence $G \cong \operatorname{im}(\Phi) \le S_G$. $\square$

**Example.** The Klein four-group $V_4 = \{e, a, b, c\}$ with $a^2 = b^2 = c^2 = e$ and $ab = c$. Cayley's theorem embeds $V_4$ into $S_4$ via left multiplication:
- $\phi_e = (e)(a)(b)(c)$ — identity
- $\phi_a = (e\; a)(b\; c)$
- $\phi_b = (e\; b)(a\; c)$
- $\phi_c = (e\; c)(a\; b)$

These four permutations form a subgroup of $S_4$ isomorphic to $V_4$.

**Problem 2 (Generation by 3-cycles).** Prove that $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles (cycles of length 3). *Hint:* $(a\ b)(b\ c) = (a\ b\ c)$ and $(a\ b)(c\ d) = (a\ b\ c)(a\ d\ c)$.

<details>
  <summary>Solution sketch</summary>
  <div>
    Every even permutation is a product of an even number of transpositions. Pair adjacent transpositions. If they share an element, their product is a 3-cycle. If disjoint, their product decomposes into two 3-cycles via $(ab)(cd) = (abc)(adc)$.
  </div>
</details>

**Problem 3 (Maximum order in $S_7$).** Find an element of $S_7$ with the largest possible order. Generalize: given a partition $n = \sum n_i$, the order equals $\operatorname{lcm}(n_1, n_2, \dots)$ of the cycle lengths. What partition of $7$ maximizes this?

<details>
  <summary>Answer</summary>
  <div>
    The partition $7 = 3 + 4$ gives order $\operatorname{lcm}(3,4) = 12$. Other candidates: $7$ gives $7$, $2+5$ gives $10$, $2+2+3$ gives $6$. The maximum order in $S_7$ is $12$.
  </div>
</details>

**Problem 4 (Sign of a product formula).** Prove directly from the definition that $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma\tau) = \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\operatorname{sgn}(\tau)$ without using the transposition counting argument of Theorem 4. Use the inversion-counting definition: $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = (-1)^{I(\sigma)}$ where $I(\sigma) = \#\{(i,j) : i < j \text{ and } \sigma(i) > \sigma(j)\}$.

    visited = set()
    cycles = []
    for x in perm:
        if x not in visited:
            cycle = []
            curr = x
            while curr not in visited:
                visited.add(curr)
                cycle.append(curr)
                curr = perm[curr]
            if len(cycle) > 1:
                cycles.append(cycle)
    return cycles

def is_even_permutation(perm):
    cycles = disjoint_cycles(perm)
    sign = 1
    for c in cycles:
        sign *= (-1) ** (len(c) - 1)
    return sign == 1

## Retrieval Check

Without looking back, state the three equivalent conditions for a permutation to be even, and give the formula for the sign of a $k$-cycle.

<details>
<summary>Reveal Answer</summary>
<div>
A permutation $\sigma \in S_n$ is even iff:
<ol>
<li>It can be expressed as a product of an even number of transpositions.</li>
<li>$\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma) = 1$ where $\operatorname{sgn}$ is the sign homomorphism.</li>
<li>The discriminant polynomial $D(x_1,\dots,x_n)$ is fixed by $\sigma$ (i.e., $\sigma(D) = D$).</li>
</ol>
The sign of a $k$-cycle is $(-1)^{k-1}$.
