# Permutations and Symmetric Groups

Can every finite group structure, no matter how abstract, be understood simply as a set of rules for rearranging objects? 

In early group theory, mathematicians did not study abstract sets with axioms. Instead, they studied concrete rearrangements of objects, which we call **permutations**. It turns out that this concrete view is entirely general: every finite group behaves exactly like a collection of permutations.

## The Motivating Problem: Permutation Composition

Suppose you have three cups in a row, labeled $1$, $2$, and $3$. 
- Action $\sigma$ swaps cups $1$ and $2$.
- Action $\tau$ shifts all cups one position to the right (cup 1 goes to 2, 2 to 3, 3 to 1).

If we perform action $\tau$ first, and then action $\sigma$, where do the cups end up? 

Let's trace cup $1$:
1. Start: Cup 1 is at position 1.
2. Apply $\tau$: Cup 1 moves to position 2.
3. Apply $\sigma$: The cup at position 2 is swapped with the cup at position 1. So the cup ends up at position 1.

By tracking all three cups, we can determine the net result. In mathematics, we compose permutations from right to left: $(\sigma \circ \tau)(x) = \sigma(\tau(x))$.

## Formalization: Symmetric Group $S_n$ and Cycle Notation

Let $\Omega$ be a non-empty set. A **permutation** of $\Omega$ is a bijective function $\sigma: \Omega \to \Omega$. The set of all permutations of a set of size $n$ (let's say $\{1, 2, \dots, n\}$) forms a group under function composition. We call this the **Symmetric Group of degree $n$**, denoted $S_n$. The order of $S_n$ is $n!$.

### Cycle Notation

Instead of writing permutations as functions, we use **cycle notation**:
- A **cycle** $(a_1\ a_2\ \dots\ a_k)$ represents the permutation that maps $a_1 \to a_2$, $a_2 \to a_3$, $\dots$, $a_k \to a_1$, and leaves all other elements fixed.
- Two cycles are **disjoint** if they share no elements.
- **Theorem:** Every permutation in $S_n$ can be uniquely written as a product of disjoint cycles (up to the order of the cycles).

For example, the permutation $\sigma$ mapping $1 \to 3$, $2 \to 4$, $3 \to 5$, $4 \to 2$, $5 \to 1$ is decomposed as:
$$\sigma = (1\ 3\ 5)(2\ 4)$$

## Transpositions and Parity

A cycle of length 2, $(a\ b)$, is called a **transposition** (it simply swaps two elements). Every cycle (and thus every permutation) can be written as a product of transpositions:
$$(a_1\ a_2\ \dots\ a_k) = (a_1\ a_k)(a_1\ a_{k-1})\dots(a_1\ a_2)$$

While the decomposition into transpositions is not unique, the **number of transpositions** is always either always even or always odd. 
- A permutation is **even** if it can be written as a product of an even number of transpositions.
- A permutation is **odd** if it can be written as a product of an odd number of transpositions.

The set of all even permutations in $S_n$ forms a subgroup called the **Alternating Group of degree $n$**, denoted $A_n$. Its order is exactly $n!/2$.

Let's use Python to compute cycle decompositions and determine the parity of permutations.

  code={`def disjoint_cycles(perm):
    # perm is a dictionary representing the mapping, e.g. {1: 3, 2: 4, 3: 5, 4: 2, 5: 1}
    visited = set()
    cycles = []
    for x in perm:
        if x not in visited:
            cycle = []
            curr = x
            while curr not in visited:
                visited.add(curr)
                cycle.append(curr)
                curr = perm[curr]
            if len(cycle) > 1:
                cycles.append(cycle)
    return cycles

def is_even_permutation(perm):
    cycles = disjoint_cycles(perm)
    # The parity of a cycle of length L is (-1)^(L-1).
    # The total parity is the product of cycle parities.
    sign = 1
    for c in cycles:
        sign *= (-1) ** (len(c) - 1)
    return sign == 1

# Let's check: (1 3 5)(2 4)

## Cayley's Theorem: Symmetries as Universal Building Blocks

Why are symmetric groups so central? Arthur Cayley proved that they are universal:

> **Cayley's Theorem:** Every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of the symmetric group $S_G$ (the group of permutations of the set $G$ itself).

If $G$ is a finite group of order $n$, then $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_n$.

*Sketch of Proof:* For each $g \in G$, define a mapping $\phi_g: G \to G$ by left-multiplication: $\phi_g(x) = g \cdot x$. Because group elements have inverses, $\phi_g$ is a bijection, i.e., a permutation of the set $G$. The mapping $g \mapsto \phi_g$ is a group injection, embedding $G$ into $S_G$.

---

## Contrast: Even vs. Odd Permutations

- **Subgroup Status:** The set of even permutations $A_n$ is a subgroup of $S_n$. The identity permutation is even (it can be written as $(1\ 2)(1\ 2) = e$), and products/inverses of even permutations remain even.
- **Non-Subgroup Status:** The set of *odd* permutations is **NOT** a subgroup of $S_n$. The identity is even, so it is not in the set. Furthermore, the product of two odd permutations is always even (e.g. $(1\ 2) \circ (1\ 2) = e$), failing closure.
