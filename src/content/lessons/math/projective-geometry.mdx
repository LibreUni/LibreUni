# Projective Geometry

Projective geometry studies geometric properties that are invariant under projective transformations. It extends Euclidean geometry by adding "points at infinity."

## Homogeneous Coordinates

In $n$-dimensional projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$, a point is represented by $(n+1)$ coordinates $[x_0 : x_1 : \dots : x_n]$, where not all are zero. Two sets of coordinates represent the same point if they are scalar multiples of each other:
$$[x] = [\lambda x] \quad \text{for } \lambda \neq 0$$

For the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$, the point $[1 : 0]$ represents the point at infinity.

## Duality

One of the most profound features of projective geometry is the principle of duality: for any theorem in two-dimensional projective geometry, there is a dual theorem obtained by interchanging the roles of "points" and "lines."

## Cross-Ratio

The cross-ratio is the fundamental invariant of projective geometry. For four collinear points $A, B, C, D$, the cross-ratio is defined as:
$$(A, B; C, D) = \frac{AC \cdot BD}{BC \cdot AD}$$
where $AC$ denotes the signed distance from $A$ to $C$.

<CodeRunner client:load 
  code={`def cross_ratio(a, b, c, d):
    return ((c - a) * (d - b)) / ((c - b) * (d - a))

print(f"Cross ratio of 10, 11, 12, 15: {cross_ratio(10, 11, 12, 15):.2f}")
# Note how translation preserves the ratio!`}
