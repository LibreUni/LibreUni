# Splitting Fields and Separability

Adjoining a single root of a polynomial to a field does not guarantee containment of all other roots. The field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$ contains one root of $x^3 - 2$, but the remaining two roots $\sqrt[3]{2}\omega$ and $\sqrt[3]{2}\omega^2$ (where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3} = (-1 + i\sqrt{3})/2$) lie outside it. A field that contains every root of a given polynomial is called a **splitting field**.

## Existence of Splitting Fields

**Theorem (Existence).** For any field $K$ and any non-constant polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$, there exists an extension field $L/K$ such that $f(x)$ splits into linear factors in $L[x]$.

*Proof.* Proceed by induction on $\deg f$. If $\deg f = 1$, then $f$ is already linear and $L = K$ works. Assume the statement holds for all polynomials of degree $< n$. Let $f$ have degree $n \ge 2$. Choose an irreducible factor $p(x)$ of $f(x)$ (possible since $K[x]$ is a UFD). Construct the simple extension $K_1 = K[x]/(p(x))$, in which $p(x)$ has a root $\alpha$. Then $f(x) = (x - \alpha)g(x)$ in $K_1[x]$ where $\deg g = n - 1$. By the induction hypothesis, there exists an extension $L/K_1$ splitting $g(x)$, and $L$ is also a splitting field for $f(x)$. $\square$

The construction adjoins roots one at a time, factoring the polynomial after each adjunction. The field $L$ obtained is finite-dimensional over $K$; in fact $[L:K] \le n!$.

## Uniqueness up to Isomorphism

**Theorem (Uniqueness).** Let $\sigma: K \to K'$ be a field isomorphism, and let $f(x) \in K[x]$ be a polynomial with splitting field $L/K$. If $L'/K'$ is a splitting field for $\sigma(f)(x) \in K'[x]$, then $\sigma$ extends to an isomorphism $\tilde{\sigma}: L \to L'$.

*Proof.* Proceed by induction on $[L:K]$. If $[L:K] = 1$, then $L = K$ and $f$ splits over $K$, so $\sigma(f)$ splits over $K'$ and $L' = K'$; $\sigma$ itself suffices. For $[L:K] > 1$, $f$ has an irreducible factor $p(x)$ of degree $> 1$. Let $\alpha \in L$ be a root of $p$ and $\alpha' \in L'$ a root of $\sigma(p)$. Then $K(\alpha) \cong K[x]/(p(x)) \cong K'(\alpha')$ via an isomorphism $\tau$ extending $\sigma$. Now $L$ is a splitting field for $f$ over $K(\alpha)$ and $L'$ is a splitting field for $\sigma(f)$ over $K'(\alpha')$, with $[L:K(\alpha)] < [L:K]$. By the induction hypothesis, $\tau$ extends to an isomorphism $L \to L'$. $\square$

Taking $\sigma = \operatorname{id}_K$, any two splitting fields of $f$ over $K$ are $K$-isomorphic. Hence we speak of **the** splitting field of $f$ over $K$, unique up to isomorphism.

## Formal Definition

Let $K$ be a field and $f(x) \in K[x]$. An extension field $L/K$ is a **splitting field** for $f(x)$ over $K$ if:

1. $f(x)$ factors into linear factors in $L[x]$:
   $$f(x) = c(x - \alpha_1)(x - \alpha_2)\dots(x - \alpha_n) \quad \text{for some } \alpha_i \in L,\ c \in K$$
2. $L$ is generated over $K$ by the roots: $L = K(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dots, \alpha_n)$.

An extension $L/K$ is called a **normal extension** if it is the splitting field of some family of polynomials in $K[x]$. Equivalently, if a normal extension contains one root of an irreducible polynomial in $K[x]$, it must contain all of its roots.

## Worked Example: Splitting Field of $x^4 - 2$

Let $f(x) = x^4 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. The roots are $\sqrt[4]{2},\ i\sqrt[4]{2},\ -\sqrt[4]{2},\ -i\sqrt[4]{2}$, so $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}, i)$.

**Step 1: Degree of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$.** The polynomial $x^4 - 2$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ by Eisenstein's criterion with $p = 2$. Thus $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 4$.

**Step 2: Adjoining $i$.** The element $i$ satisfies $i^2 + 1 = 0$. This quadratic remains irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2})$ because $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}) \subset \mathbb{R}$ and $i \notin \mathbb{R}$. Hence $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}, i) : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2})] = 2$.

**Step 3: Tower Rule.** $[L : \mathbb{Q}] = [L : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 4 = 8$.

**Step 4: Subfield lattice.** The extension $L/\mathbb{Q}$ is Galois with group $D_4$ of order 8. The intermediate fields are displayed below.

## Worked Example: Splitting Field of $x^3 - 2$

Let $f(x) = x^3 - 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. The roots are $\sqrt[3]{2},\ \sqrt[3]{2}\omega,\ \sqrt[3]{2}\omega^2$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3} = (-1 + i\sqrt{3})/2$, so $L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$.

**Step 1: Degree of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})/\mathbb{Q}$.** Since $x^3 - 2$ is irreducible by Eisenstein ($p=2$), $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 3$.

**Step 2: Adjoining $\omega$.** The primitive cube root of unity $\omega$ satisfies $\omega^2 + \omega + 1 = 0$, an irreducible quadratic. Since $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}) \subset \mathbb{R}$ and $\omega \notin \mathbb{R}$, the polynomial $x^2 + x + 1$ remains irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})$. Thus $[L : \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})] = 2$.

**Step 3: Tower Rule.** $[L : \mathbb{Q}] = 2 \cdot 3 = 6$.

**Step 4: Verify that $L$ is the splitting field.** Over $L$, the polynomial factors completely:
$$x^3 - 2 = (x - \sqrt[3]{2})(x - \sqrt[3]{2}\omega)(x - \sqrt[3]{2}\omega^2)$$
and $L$ is generated by the roots, so it is a splitting field.

## Separability and Multiple Roots

A polynomial $f(x) \in K[x]$ is **separable** if it has no repeated roots in its splitting field. Over fields of characteristic 0 or over finite fields, every irreducible polynomial is separable. Fields with this property are called **perfect fields**. Inseparability occurs only in infinite fields of prime characteristic $p$.

### Derivative Test for Multiple Roots

**Theorem.** Let $f(x) \in K[x]$. Then $f$ has a multiple root in its splitting field if and only if $\gcd(f, f') \neq 1$, where $f'$ is the formal derivative.

*Proof.* Let $L$ be the splitting field of $f$ and $\alpha \in L$ a root. Write $f(x) = (x - \alpha)^m g(x)$ with $g(\alpha) \neq 0$ and $m \ge 1$. Then
$$f'(x) = m(x - \alpha)^{m-1} g(x) + (x - \alpha)^m g'(x).$$
If $m \ge 2$, then $(x - \alpha)$ divides $f'(x)$, so $\gcd(f, f') \neq 1$.

Conversely, suppose $\gcd(f, f')$ has a root $\alpha$. Then $f(\alpha) = 0$ and $f'(\alpha) = 0$. Substituting into the formula above gives $0 = m \cdot 0^{m-1} g(\alpha) + 0$. If $m = 1$, the term $0^{m-1} = 0^0$ is interpreted as 1, yielding $f'(\alpha) = g(\alpha) \neq 0$, a contradiction. Hence $m \ge 2$ and $\alpha$ is a multiple root. $\square$

### Perfect Fields

A field $K$ is **perfect** if every irreducible polynomial over $K$ is separable.

**Theorem.** Fields of characteristic 0 are perfect. Finite fields are perfect.

*Proof for characteristic 0.* Let $f \in K[x]$ be irreducible. If $f$ had a multiple root, then $\gcd(f, f') \neq 1$ by the derivative test. Since $f$ is irreducible, $\gcd(f, f') = f$, so $f \mid f'$. But $\deg f' < \deg f$, which forces $f' = 0$. In characteristic 0, this implies every coefficient of $f'$ is zero, so $f$ is constant — a contradiction. Hence $f$ is separable. $\square$

*Proof for finite fields.* Let $K = \mathbb{F}_q$ and $f \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$ be irreducible. If $f$ were inseparable, then $f' = 0$, forcing every exponent of $f$ to be a multiple of $p = \operatorname{char}(K)$. Write $f(x) = g(x^p)$ for some $g \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$. The Frobenius map $a \mapsto a^p$ is an automorphism of $\mathbb{F}_q$, so every element has a $p$-th root. Thus $g(x^p) = (h(x))^p$ for some $h \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$, contradicting the irreducibility of $f$. Therefore $f$ is separable. $\square$

### Contrast: Separable vs Inseparable Extension

- **Separable Extension:** $L/K$ is separable if the minimal polynomial of every element in $L$ is separable. Over $\mathbb{Q}$, every algebraic extension is separable because $\mathbb{Q}$ has characteristic 0.
- **Inseparable Extension:** Exists only in characteristic $p$. In $\mathbb{F}_p(t^{1/p}) / \mathbb{F}_p(t)$, the minimal polynomial of $t^{1/p}$ is $x^p - t = (x - t^{1/p})^p$, so the root has multiplicity $p$ and the extension is purely inseparable.

### Worked Example: Testing $x^3 - 2$ for Separability

Check whether $x^3 - 2$ is separable over $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Step 1:** $f'(x) = 3x^2$.

**Step 2:** Compute $\gcd(x^3 - 2, 3x^2)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. Since $x^3 - 2$ is irreducible and $x \nmid (x^3 - 2)$, the only possible common factor is a unit. Hence $\gcd = 1$.

**Step 3:** By the derivative test, $x^3 - 2$ is separable. Its splitting field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$ indeed contains three distinct roots.
