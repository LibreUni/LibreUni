# The Fundamental Theorems of Vector Calculus

In single-variable calculus, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus states that integrating a derivative over an interval depends only on the boundary values at the endpoints. In vector calculus, this principle generalizes to higher dimensions: the integral of a "derivative-like" operator (gradient, curl, divergence) over a region is determined entirely by the values of the field on the boundary of that region.

---

## Parametric Surfaces and Surface Integrals

To extend integrals to 3D curves and surfaces, we define parametric surfaces and their area elements.

<MathStatement id="parametric-surface" kind="definition" title="Parametric Surface and Surface Integral" number="1">

A **parametric surface** $S$ is defined by a vector-valued function:

$$\mathbf{r}(u, v) = x(u, v)\mathbf{i} + y(u, v)\mathbf{j} + z(u, v)\mathbf{k}, \quad (u, v) \in D.$$

The tangent vectors are $\mathbf{r}_u = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u}$ and $\mathbf{r}_v = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}$. The standard normal vector is $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$.
The **surface area** of $S$ is:

$$A(S) = \iint_D \|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\| \, dA.$$

The **surface integral** of a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ over $S$ (oriented by unit normal $\mathbf{n}$) is the flux of $\mathbf{F}$ through $S$:

$$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u, v)) \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v) \, du \, dv.$$

</MathStatement>

## Green's Theorem (2D Boundary Connection)

Green's Theorem connects a line integral around a closed curve in the plane to a double integral over the enclosed region.

The orientation is part of the theorem. For a positively oriented boundary, the region remains on the left as the curve is traversed:

<TikZ code={String.raw`\draw[fill=blue!10, draw=blue, thick] (0,0) rectangle (5,3);
\draw[->, red, thick] (0,0) -- (5,0) -- (5,3) -- (0,3) -- (0,0);
\node at (2.5,1.5) {$D$};
\node[red] at (2.5,-.35) {$C=\partial D$};`} />

<MathStatement id="greens-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Green's Theorem" number="1" proofId="greens-theorem-proof">

Let $C$ be a positively oriented, piecewise smooth, simple closed curve in the plane, and let $D$ be the region bounded by $C$. If $P$ and $Q$ have continuous partial derivatives on an open region containing $D$, then:

$$\oint_C (P \, dx + Q \, dy) = \iint_D \left( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right) dA.$$

</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="greens-theorem-proof" for="greens-theorem">

We prove the theorem for a **simple region** $D$ that is simultaneously Type I and Type II:
$$D = \{ (x, y) \mid a \le x \le b, \, g_1(x) \le y \le g_2(x) \}.$$

We show $\oint_C P \, dx = -\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dA$.
Evaluate the double integral on the right:

$$\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dy \, dx.$$

By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 2), the inner integral is $P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x))$, so:

$$\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dA = \int_a^b [P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x))] \, dx.$$

Now, evaluate the line integral $\oint_C P \, dx$. The boundary curve $C$ consists of two paths:
- $C_1$ (lower boundary): $y = g_1(x)$, $x$ goes from $a$ to $b$.
- $C_2$ (upper boundary): $y = g_2(x)$, $x$ goes from $b$ to $a$ (for positive orientation).

$$\oint_C P \, dx = \int_{C_1} P \, dx + \int_{C_2} P \, dx = \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x)) \, dx + \int_b^a P(x, g_2(x)) \, dx$$
$$= \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x)) \, dx - \int_a^b P(x, g_2(x)) \, dx = -\int_a^b [P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x))] \, dx.$$

Comparing the two results:

$$\oint_C P \, dx = -\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dA.$$

A similar proof shows $\oint_C Q \, dy = \iint_D \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} \, dA$. Adding the two yields Green's Theorem.

</MathProof>

## Stokes' Theorem (3D Boundary Connection)

Stokes' Theorem generalizes Green's Theorem to 3D surfaces bounded by a closed loop.

<MathStatement id="stokes-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Stokes' Theorem" number="2" dependsOn={[{ id: "greens-theorem", label: "Green's Theorem" }]}>

Let $S$ be an oriented piecewise smooth surface bounded by a simple, closed, piecewise smooth boundary curve $C$ with positive orientation. If $\mathbf{F}$ is a vector field with continuous partial derivatives, then:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}.$$

</MathStatement>

## Gauss' Divergence Theorem (Volume Connection)

Gauss' Theorem relates the net expansion of a field inside a volume to the flux out of the boundary.

<MathStatement id="divergence-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Gauss' Divergence Theorem" number="3">

Let $E$ be a simple solid region, and let $S = \partial E$ be the boundary surface of $E$, oriented outward. If $\mathbf{F}$ is a vector field with continuous partial derivatives on $E$, then:

$$\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_E (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) \, dV.$$

</MathStatement>

## Connection to Maxwell's Equations

The transition between differential forms (local laws) and integral forms (global laws) in electromagnetism is performed using these boundary theorems:

| Differential Law (Local) | Integral Law (Global) | Theorem Used | Physical Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$ | $\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{Q_{\text{enclosed}}}{\epsilon_0}$ | Divergence Theorem | Gauss's Law (electric charge creates diverging fields) |
| $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$ | $\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$ | Stokes' Theorem | Faraday's Law (changing magnetic fields induce EMF) |

Let us write a Python script to verify Green's Theorem for $\mathbf{F}(x, y) = -y\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j}$ over the unit square $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$. The boundary line integral should equal the double integral of $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1 - (-1) = 2$ over the square (Area = 1.0, so integral = 2.0).

<CodeRunner client:load
  code={`# Green's Theorem verification over unit square
# F = [-y, x] -> dQ/dx - dP/dy = 1 - (-1) = 2.0
# Double integral over square is 2.0 * Area = 2.0 * 1.0 = 2.0

# Line integral: 4 segments:
# L1: (0,0) -> (1,0) [y=0, dy=0] -> F = [0, x]. F.dr = 0*dx = 0
# L2: (1,0) -> (1,1) [x=1, dx=0] -> F = [-y, 1]. F.dr = 1*dy -> int(1) dy [0,1] = 1.0
# L3: (1,1) -> (0,1) [y=1, dy=0] -> F = [-1, x]. F.dr = -1*dx -> int(-1) dx [1,0] = 1.0
# L4: (0,1) -> (0,0) [x=0, dx=0] -> F = [-y, 0]. F.dr = 0*dy = 0
# Total line integral = 0 + 1.0 + 1.0 + 0 = 2.0

line_integral_sum = 0.0 + 1.0 + 1.0 + 0.0
double_integral_val = 2.0 * 1.0 # (curl * Area)

print(f"Line Integral around boundary: {line_integral_sum}")
print(f"Double Integral of Curl: {double_integral_val}")
print(f"Match? {line_integral_sum == double_integral_val}")
`}
