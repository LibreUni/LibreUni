# Subgroups and Cyclic Groups

When studying a large, complex group, it is often useful to look for smaller groups contained within it. For example, within the symmetries of a triangle $D_3$, the set of rotations $\{R_0, R_{120}, R_{240}\}$ forms a group on its own under the same composition operation.

How do we determine if a subset of a group is itself a group, and how do we construct groups using a single generator?

## The Problem: Classifying Subsets

Consider $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$. Inspect three subsets:
1. $2\mathbb{Z} = \{ \dots, -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, \dots \}$ (even integers).
2. $2\mathbb{Z} + 1 = \{ \dots, -3, -1, 1, 3, 5, \dots \}$ (odd integers).
3. $\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0} = \{ 0, 1, 2, 3, \dots \}$ (non-negative integers).

Only one forms a group under addition.

## Formalization: Subgroups and the Subgroup Criterion

Let $(G, \cdot)$ be a group. A subset $H \subseteq G$ is a **subgroup** of $G$ (denoted $H \le G$) if $H$ forms a group under the binary operation of $G$ restricted to $H$.

Associativity and the operation itself are inherited from $G$, so only three checks are needed:

> **Theorem (Subgroup Criterion):** A subset $H$ of a group $G$ is a subgroup if and only if:
> 1. $H$ is non-empty ($e \in H$).
> 2. For all $x, y \in H$, $x \cdot y \in H$ (closure under the operation).
> 3. For all $x \in H$, $x^{-1} \in H$ (closure under inverses).

These three conditions collapse into a single combined check:
$$H \le G \iff H \neq \emptyset \quad\text{and}\quad \forall x, y \in H,\; x \cdot y^{-1} \in H.$$

*Proof.* If $H \le G$, then $e \in H$ so $H \neq \emptyset$, and closure gives $x y^{-1} \in H$. Conversely, pick $x \in H$ (non-empty). With $y = x$, we get $e = x x^{-1} \in H$. With $e$ and any $y \in H$, we get $y^{-1} = e y^{-1} \in H$. Finally, $x y = x (y^{-1})^{-1} \in H$. ∎

### Worked Example: Upper-Triangular Matrices

Let $G = \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$, the group of $2\times 2$ invertible real matrices under multiplication. Determine whether
$$H = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & c \end{pmatrix} \;\Big|\; a, b, c \in \mathbb{R},\; ac \neq 0 \right\}$$
is a subgroup.

**Step 1 — Identity.** $I_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$ has $a = c = 1$, $b = 0$, and $ac = 1 \neq 0$, so $I_2 \in H$.

**Step 2 — Closure.** Take $X = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\0&c\end{pmatrix}$, $Y = \begin{pmatrix}d&e\\0&f\end{pmatrix}$ in $H$; then $ac \neq 0$ and $df \neq 0$. Compute
$$XY = \begin{pmatrix}ad & ae+bf \\ 0 & cf\end{pmatrix}.$$
Since $ad \cdot cf = (ac)(df) \neq 0$, the product lies in $H$.

**Step 3 — Inverses.** For $X$ with $ac \neq 0$,
$$X^{-1} = \frac{1}{ac}\begin{pmatrix}c & -b \\ 0 & a\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/a & -b/(ac) \\ 0 & 1/c\end{pmatrix}.$$
The $(2,1)$ entry is $0$, and $(1/a)(1/c) = 1/(ac) \neq 0$, so $X^{-1} \in H$.

Thus $H$ is a subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{R})$ — the **upper-triangular** matrices.

## The Center of a Group

For any group $G$, the **center** is the set of elements that commute with every element of $G$:
$$Z(G) = \{ z \in G \mid zg = gz \text{ for all } g \in G \}.$$

**Theorem.** $Z(G)$ is a subgroup of $G$.

*Proof.*
- **Identity:** $e g = g e$ for all $g$, so $e \in Z(G)$.
- **Closure:** If $z_1, z_2 \in Z(G)$, then for any $g \in G$,
  $$(z_1 z_2) g = z_1 (z_2 g) = z_1 (g z_2) = (z_1 g) z_2 = (g z_1) z_2 = g (z_1 z_2),$$
  so $z_1 z_2 \in Z(G)$.
- **Inverses:** If $z \in Z(G)$, then for any $g \in G$,
  $$z^{-1} g = z^{-1} g (z z^{-1}) = z^{-1} (g z) z^{-1} = z^{-1} (z g) z^{-1} = (z^{-1} z) g z^{-1} = g z^{-1},$$
  so $z^{-1} \in Z(G)$. ∎

The center is always abelian. A group is abelian iff $Z(G) = G$.

### Worked Example: Center of $D_3$

$D_3 = \{R_0, R_{120}, R_{240}, r_1, r_2, r_3\}$ (rotations and reflections of a triangle). Rotations commute with each other, but no reflection commutes with a nontrivial rotation: $R_{120} r_1 = r_3 \neq r_2 = r_1 R_{120}$. Hence $Z(D_3) = \{R_0\}$ — the center is trivial.

## Cyclic Subgroups and Element Order

Take a single element $a \in G$ and repeatedly apply the group operation. Define powers:
- $a^n = a \cdot a \cdot \dots \cdot a$ ($n$ times) for $n > 0$.
- $a^0 = e$.
- $a^{-n} = (a^{-1})^n$ for $n > 0$.

The set of all powers
$$\langle a \rangle = \{ a^n \mid n \in \mathbb{Z} \}$$
is a subgroup of $G$ — the **cyclic subgroup generated by $a$**. If $G = \langle g \rangle$ for some $g$, $G$ is **cyclic**.

The **order** $|a|$ is the smallest positive $n$ with $a^n = e$ (or $\infty$ if none exists).
- If $|a| = n$, then $\langle a \rangle = \{ e, a, a^2, \dots, a^{n-1} \}$ has exactly $n$ elements.
- If $|a| = \infty$, then $\langle a \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

## Subgroups of Cyclic Groups

Every subgroup of a cyclic group is itself cyclic.

> **Theorem.** Let $G = \langle a \rangle$ be a cyclic group and $H \le G$. Then $H$ is cyclic. Moreover, if $|G| = n$ and $H \neq \{e\}$, then $H = \langle a^d \rangle$ where $d$ is the smallest positive integer with $a^d \in H$, and $|H| = n/d$.

*Proof.* If $H = \{e\}$, it is trivially cyclic. Otherwise, let $d$ be the smallest positive integer such that $a^d \in H$. Claim: $H = \langle a^d \rangle$.

Take any $x \in H$. Since $G = \langle a \rangle$, $x = a^k$ for some $k \in \mathbb{Z}$. Apply the division algorithm: $k = qd + r$ with $0 \le r < d$. Then $a^k = a^{qd + r} = (a^d)^q a^r$, so $a^r = (a^d)^{-q} a^k$. Both $(a^d)^{-q}$ and $a^k$ belong to $H$, therefore $a^r \in H$. By minimality of $d$, $r = 0$, so $d \mid k$ and $x = (a^d)^q \in \langle a^d \rangle$.

If $|G| = n$, then $a^n = e \in H$, so the same argument gives $d \mid n$. Hence $|a^d| = n/d$ and $|H| = n/d$. ∎

### Corollary: Classification for $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$

For $G = \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, subgroups correspond bijectively to divisors $d \mid n$:
$$\{ \langle d \rangle \} \longleftrightarrow \{ \text{subgroups of } \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \}.$$
Concretely, $\langle d \rangle$ has order $n/d$, and every subgroup arises uniquely this way.

## Contrast: A Non-Cyclic Group

Not all groups are cyclic. The **Klein four-group** $V_4 = \{e, a, b, c\}$ satisfies $a^2 = b^2 = c^2 = e$ and $ab = ba = c$, $bc = cb = a$, $ca = ac = b$. Every non-identity element has order $2$; no element has order $4$, so $V_4$ cannot be generated by a single element.
