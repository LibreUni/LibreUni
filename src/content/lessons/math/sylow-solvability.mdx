# Sylow Theorems and Solvability

Lagrange's Theorem states that the order of any subgroup of a finite group $G$ must divide the order of $G$. But is the converse true? If $d$ divides $|G|$, is there always a subgroup of order $d$ in $G$?

The answer is **no**. The alternating group $A_4$, which has order 12, contains no subgroup of order 6. To find subgroups when the converse of Lagrange's theorem fails, we must turn to the **Sylow Theorems**. These theorems guarantee the existence of subgroups of prime-power order and provide tools to count them.

## The Motivating Counterexample: Subgroups of $A_4$

The group $A_4$ consists of the 12 even permutations of $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$. The divisors of 12 are $1, 2, 3, 4, 6,$ and $12$.
If we search for subgroups:
- There is a trivial subgroup of order 1.
- There are subgroups of order 2 (generated by double transpositions like $(1\ 2)(3\ 4)$).
- There are subgroups of order 3 (generated by 3-cycles like $(1\ 2\ 3)$).
- There is a subgroup of order 4 (the Klein four-group $V_4 = \{e, (1\ 2)(3\ 4), (1\ 3)(2\ 4), (1\ 4)(2\ 3)\}$).

But if you check every possible subset of size 6, you will find that none of them forms a subgroup under composition.

### Try It Yourself: Sylow Subgroups of $A_4$

While $A_4$ has no subgroup of order 6, it does have subgroups of order $4 = 2^2$ (a power of the prime 2) and order $3$ (a prime). These are examples of **Sylow subgroups**.

## Formalization: Conjugacy and the Class Equation

Let a group $G$ act on itself by **conjugation**: $g \cdot x = g x g^{-1}$. 
- The orbit of $x \in G$ under this action is called the **conjugacy class** of $x$, denoted $Cl(x) = \{ g x g^{-1} \mid g \in G \}$.
- The stabilizer of $x \in G$ under conjugation is called the **centralizer** of $x$, denoted $C_G(x) = \{ g \in G \mid g x = x g \}$.

By partitioning $G$ into its conjugacy classes, we get the **Class Equation**:
$$|G| = |Z(G)| + \sum_{i=1}^r [G : C_G(g_i)]$$
Where $Z(G) = \{ z \in G \mid zg = gz \text{ for all } g \in G \}$ is the **center** of $G$ (the elements whose conjugacy class has size 1), and $g_1, \dots, g_r$ are representatives of the conjugacy classes of size greater than 1.

## The Sylow Theorems

Let $G$ be a finite group, and let $p$ be a prime. Suppose $|G| = p^\alpha m$, where $p$ does not divide $m$. A subgroup of $G$ of order $p^\alpha$ is called a **Sylow $p$-subgroup** of $G$.

> **Sylow's First Theorem (Existence):** $G$ contains at least one Sylow $p$-subgroup.

> **Sylow's Second Theorem (Conjugacy):** Any two Sylow $p$-subgroups of $G$ are conjugate to each other (i.e., if $P$ and $Q$ are Sylow $p$-subgroups, then $Q = g P g^{-1}$ for some $g \in G$).
> *Corollary:* A Sylow $p$-subgroup $P$ is **normal** in $G$ if and only if it is the *unique* Sylow $p$-subgroup of $G$.

> **Sylow's Third Theorem (Number):** Let $n_p$ be the number of Sylow $p$-subgroups of $G$. Then:
> 1. $n_p \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
> 2. $n_p$ divides $m$.

## Worked Example: Classifying Groups of Order 15

Let's prove that any group $G$ of order 15 must be cyclic (isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$).
Here, $|G| = 15 = 3 \cdot 5$.
1. **Find $n_5$ (number of Sylow 5-subgroups):**
   - $n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod 5 \implies n_5 \in \{1, 6, 11, \dots\}$
   - $n_5$ must divide $3 \implies n_5 \in \{1, 3\}$
   - The only number satisfying both is $n_5 = 1$. Thus, there is a unique, normal Sylow 5-subgroup $P_5 \trianglelefteq G$ of order 5.
2. **Find $n_3$ (number of Sylow 3-subgroups):**
   - $n_3 \equiv 1 \pmod 3 \implies n_3 \in \{1, 4, 7, \dots\}$
   - $n_3$ must divide $5 \implies n_3 \in \{1, 5\}$
   - The only number satisfying both is $n_3 = 1$. Thus, there is a unique, normal Sylow 3-subgroup $P_3 \trianglelefteq G$ of order 3.
3. **Analyze the intersection:** Since $P_3$ and $P_5$ have prime orders, their intersection must be trivial: $P_3 \cap P_5 = \{e\}$.
4. **Form the product:** Since both subgroups are normal and intersect trivially, their direct product $P_3 \times P_5$ is isomorphic to $G$. Since $\gcd(3, 5) = 1$, the product of cyclic groups $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}$ is isomorphic to the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$.

Thus, $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic!

## Solvable Groups

The structure of groups is also studied through their composition series. A group $G$ is called **solvable** if it has a chain of subgroups:
$$\{e\} = G_0 \trianglelefteq G_1 \trianglelefteq \dots \trianglelefteq G_k = G$$
such that each factor group $G_{i+1}/G_i$ is **Abelian**.

Solvable groups are directly linked to the solvability of polynomial equations by radicals, which we will see in the Galois Theory module. The alternating group $A_5$ is the smallest non-solvable group because it is a **simple group** (it contains no non-trivial normal subgroups), and it is non-Abelian.
