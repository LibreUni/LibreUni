# Sylow Theorems and Solvability

Lagrange's Theorem says that the order of any subgroup divides the order of the group. But the converse is false: $A_4$ (order 12) has no subgroup of order 6. The **Sylow Theorems** salvage a partial converse: they guarantee subgroups of prime-power order and tell us how many such subgroups exist.

## The Motivating Counterexample: $A_4$

$A_4$ (even permutations of 4 elements) has order $12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$. Divisors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Subgroups exist of orders 1, 2, 3, 4 — but **none of order 6**. Why? Because 6 is not the highest power of any prime dividing 12.

The Sylow theorems guarantee:
- A subgroup of order $2^2 = 4$ (Sylow 2-subgroup).
- A subgroup of order $3^1 = 3$ (Sylow 3-subgroup).

## The Class Equation (Review from Group Actions)

Let $G$ act on itself by conjugation. The orbits are **conjugacy classes**, and the stabilizer of $x$ is its **centralizer** $C_G(x)$. Partitioning $G$:
$$|G| = |Z(G)| + \sum_{i=1}^r [G : C_G(g_i)]$$
where $Z(G)$ is the center and $g_i$ represent classes of size $>1$.

---

## The Sylow Theorems

Let $G$ be a finite group with $|G| = p^\alpha m$ where $p \nmid m$. A **Sylow $p$-subgroup** is a subgroup of order $p^\alpha$.

### Sylow I (Existence)

> **Theorem (Sylow I):** $G$ contains at least one Sylow $p$-subgroup.

*Proof Sketch (class equation method):* Induct on $|G|$. If $p \mid |Z(G)|$, Cauchy's theorem gives an element $z \in Z(G)$ of order $p$, then $G/\langle z \rangle$ has a Sylow $p$-subgroup by induction — lift it. If $p \nmid |Z(G)|$, the class equation $|G| = |Z(G)| + \sum [G:C_G(g_i)]$ implies $p \nmid [G:C_G(g_i)]$ for some $i$, so $p^\alpha \mid |C_G(g_i)| < |G|$. By induction $C_G(g_i)$ has a Sylow $p$-subgroup, which is also a Sylow $p$-subgroup of $G$.

### Sylow II (Conjugacy)

> **Theorem (Sylow II):** Any two Sylow $p$-subgroups of $G$ are conjugate. That is, if $P$ and $Q$ are Sylow $p$-subgroups, then $Q = g P g^{-1}$ for some $g \in G$.

*Proof Sketch:* Let $P$ act on the set $G/Q$ by left multiplication. The number of fixed points is $|N_G(Q) : Q| \cdot \delta$ (technical). Since $p \nmid |G/Q| = m$, there must be a fixed point $gQ$, which implies $g^{-1}Pg \subseteq Q$. Since both have the same order, they are equal.

### Sylow III (Number)

> **Theorem (Sylow III):** Let $n_p$ be the number of Sylow $p$-subgroups of $G$. Then:
> 1. $n_p \equiv 1 \pmod p$.
> 2. $n_p \mid m$.

*Proof Sketch:* $n_p = [G : N_G(P)]$ where $P$ is a Sylow $p$-subgroup. Since $P \le N_G(P)$, $n_p \mid [G:P] = m$. For the congruence, let $P$ act on the set of Sylow $p$-subgroups by conjugation; the only fixed point is $P$ itself (by Sylow II), and all other orbits have size divisible by $p$.

## Worked Example: Classifying Groups of Order 15

Prove any group $G$ of order $15 = 3 \cdot 5$ is cyclic.

1. **$n_5$:** $n_5 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$ and $n_5 \mid 3 \implies n_5 = 1$. So there is a unique (hence normal) Sylow 5-subgroup $P_5 \trianglelefteq G$ of order 5.
2. **$n_3$:** $n_3 \equiv 1 \pmod 3$ and $n_3 \mid 5 \implies n_3 = 1$. So $P_3 \trianglelefteq G$ of order 3.
3. $P_3 \cap P_5 = \{e\}$ (intersection of subgroups of distinct prime orders).
4. Both are normal with trivial intersection, so $G \cong P_3 \times P_5 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$.

  code={`def sylow_possibilities(order, p):
    alpha = 0
    m = order
    while m % p == 0:
        m //= p
        alpha += 1
    if alpha == 0:
        return []
    # n_p must divide m and satisfy n_p ≡ 1 (mod p)
    return [k for k in range(1, m+1) if m % k == 0 and k % p == 1]

## Solvable Groups

A group $G$ is **solvable** if it has a chain
$$\{e\} = G_0 \trianglelefteq G_1 \trianglelefteq \dots \trianglelefteq G_k = G$$
where each factor $G_{i+1}/G_i$ is **Abelian**. Solvability connects directly to Galois theory: a polynomial equation is solvable by radicals iff its Galois group is solvable.

$A_5$ is the smallest non-solvable group — it is **simple** (no non-trivial normal subgroups) and non-Abelian.
