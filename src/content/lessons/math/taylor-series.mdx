# Taylor Series

Many continuous functions (such as $\sin(x)$, $e^x$, or $\ln(x)$) cannot be calculated using simple algebraic operations. Power series, and specifically Taylor series, allow us to represent these transcendental functions as "infinite polynomials," which are easy to compute, differentiate, and integrate.

---

## Power Series and Convergence

Before defining Taylor series, we must understand the behavior of power series.

<MathStatement id="power-series" kind="definition" title="Power Series" number="1">

A **power series** centered at $a$ is an infinite series of the form:

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x - a) + c_2(x - a)^2 + \dots$$

where $x$ is a variable and $c_n$ are the coefficients.

</MathStatement>

Every power series converges at its center $x = a$. The set of all $x$ for which the series converges is an interval, governed by the radius of convergence.

<MathStatement id="radius-convergence" kind="theorem" title="Radius of Convergence" number="1">

For any power series $\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n (x - a)^n$, exactly one of the following holds:
1. The series converges only at $x = a$ (Radius of convergence $R = 0$).
2. The series converges absolutely for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (Radius of convergence $R = \infty$).
3. There exists a real number $R > 0$ such that the series converges absolutely for $|x - a| < R$ and diverges for $|x - a| > R$.

The value $R$ is the **radius of convergence**, and can be calculated using the Root or Ratio test:

$$\frac{1}{R} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n} \right| \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{1}{R} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|c_n|}.$$

</MathStatement>

## Taylor and Maclaurin Series

If a function can be represented by a power series, the coefficients are uniquely determined by the function's derivatives.

<MathStatement id="taylor-definition" kind="definition" title="Taylor and Maclaurin Series" number="2">

Let $f$ be a function with derivatives of all orders on an interval containing $a$. The **Taylor series** of $f$ centered at $a$ is:

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x - a)^n = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x - a)^2 + \dots$$

If $a = 0$, the series is called the **Maclaurin series** of $f$.

</MathStatement>

## Taylor's Theorem with Remainder

In practice, we can only compute finite sums. Taylor's Theorem provides a formula for the error (remainder) when approximating a function with a Taylor polynomial of degree $k$.

<MathStatement id="taylors-theorem" kind="theorem" title="Taylor's Theorem" number="2" proofId="taylors-theorem-proof">

Let $f$ have $k+1$ derivatives on an open interval $I$ containing $a$. For any $x \in I$, there exists a number $c$ between $a$ and $x$ such that:

$$f(x) = P_k(x) + R_k(x),$$

where $P_k(x)$ is the $k$-th degree Taylor polynomial:

$$P_k(x) = \sum_{n=0}^k \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x - a)^n,$$

and $R_k(x)$ is the remainder (error) term, expressed in the **Lagrange form**:

$$R_k(x) = \frac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!} (x - a)^{k+1}.$$

</MathStatement>

Increasing the degree adds local information: each Taylor polynomial agrees with the function and more derivatives at the center.

<MathProof id="taylors-theorem-proof" for="taylors-theorem">

We fix $x \neq a$. Let $M$ be the unique real number satisfying:

$$f(x) = P_k(x) + M(x - a)^{k+1}.$$

We want to prove that $M = \frac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}$ for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$.
Define an auxiliary function $g(t)$ for $t$ in the interval:

$$g(t) = f(t) - P_k(t) - M(t - a)^{k+1}.$$

1. Since $f$ has $k+1$ derivatives, $g$ is continuous and differentiable.
2. Evaluate $g$ at $t = a$:
   - $g(a) = f(a) - P_k(a) - M(0) = f(a) - f(a) = 0$.
   - By definition of $P_k(t)$, we also have $g'(a) = g''(a) = \dots = g^{(k)}(a) = 0$.
3. Evaluate $g$ at $t = x$:
   - $g(x) = f(x) - P_k(x) - M(x - a)^{k+1} = 0$, by our choice of $M$.

Since $g(a) = g(x) = 0$, by Rolle's Theorem, there exists $c_1$ between $a$ and $x$ such that $g'(c_1) = 0$.
Now, since $g'(a) = 0$ and $g'(c_1) = 0$, by Rolle's Theorem again, there exists $c_2$ between $a$ and $c_1$ such that $g''(c_2) = 0$.
Repeating this process $k+1$ times, we find that there exists $c = c_{k+1}$ between $a$ and $x$ such that:

$$g^{(k+1)}(c) = 0.$$

Differentiating $g(t)$ exactly $k+1$ times:
- The $(k+1)$-th derivative of $f(t)$ is $f^{(k+1)}(t)$.
- The $(k+1)$-th derivative of the polynomial $P_k(t)$ of degree $k$ is $0$.
- The $(k+1)$-th derivative of $M(t - a)^{k+1}$ is $M \cdot (k+1)!$.

Thus:

$$g^{(k+1)}(t) = f^{(k+1)}(t) - 0 - M(k+1)!$$

Evaluating at $t = c$:

$$0 = f^{(k+1)}(c) - M(k+1)! \implies M = \frac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}.$$

Substituting $M$ back into our original equation yields the Lagrange remainder.

</MathProof>

Let us write a Python script to plot the Maclaurin polynomials of $\cos(x)$ and analyze how adding terms expands the interval where the approximation remains accurate.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def cos_approx(x, order):
    # P_n(x) for cos(x) around a=0
    val = 0.0
    for i in range(0, order + 1, 2):
        sign = (-1) ** (i // 2)
        term = (x ** i) / np.math.factorial(i)
        val += sign * term
    return val

x = np.linspace(-2 * np.pi, 2 * np.pi, 200)

plt.plot(x, np.cos(x), label='cos(x)', color='black', linewidth=2)

plt.plot(x, cos_approx(x, 4), '--', label='Order 4', color='g')
plt.plot(x, cos_approx(x, 8), '--', label='Order 8', color='b')
plt.ylim(-2, 2)
plt.legend()
plt.title("Maclaurin Approximations of cos(x)")
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

# Compute error at x = np.pi
exact = np.cos(np.pi)
print(f"Exact cos(pi): {exact}")
print(f"Error (order 2): {abs(cos_approx(np.pi, 2) - exact):.6f}")
print(f"Error (order 8): {abs(cos_approx(np.pi, 8) - exact):.6f}")

## Euler's Formula via Maclaurin Series

By substituting $ix$ into the Maclaurin series of $e^z$, we can derive the fundamental connection between complex exponentiation and trigonometry.

Let $z = ix$. The Maclaurin series for $e^{ix}$ is:

$$e^{ix} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(ix)^n}{n!} = 1 + ix - \frac{x^2}{2!} - \frac{ix^3}{3!} + \frac{x^4}{4!} + \frac{ix^5}{5!} - \dots$$

Grouping real and imaginary parts:

$$e^{ix} = \left( 1 - \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^4}{4!} - \dots \right) + i \left( x - \frac{x^3}{3!} + \frac{x^5}{5!} - \dots \right).$$

Recognizing the real part as the Maclaurin series for $\cos(x)$ and the imaginary part as the Maclaurin series for $\sin(x)$, we establish **Euler's Formula**:

$$e^{ix} = \cos(x) + i\sin(x).$$
