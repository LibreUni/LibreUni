# Vector Fields

A vector field maps a point in space to a vector. In physics and engineering, vector fields represent forces (gravity, electromagnetism), velocities (fluid flows, wind speeds), and gradients of scalar fields.

---

## Definitions and Conservative Fields

Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be an open set.

<MathStatement id="vector-field-def" kind="definition" title="Vector Field" number="1">

A **vector field** on $U$ is a function $\mathbf{F}: U \to \mathbb{R}^n$ that assigns a vector to each point in $U$. For $n = 3$, we write:

$$\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = P(x, y, z)\mathbf{i} + Q(x, y, z)\mathbf{j} + R(x, y, z)\mathbf{k}$$

where $P, Q, R$ are scalar-valued functions on $U$, called the component functions.

</MathStatement>

<VectorFieldPlayground client:load />

A special class of fields consists of those derived directly from scalar potentials.

<MathStatement id="conservative-field" kind="definition" title="Conservative Vector Field" number="2">

A vector field $\mathbf{F}$ is called **conservative** if it is the gradient of some scalar function $f$ (called the **potential function**), i.e.,

$$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\mathbf{k}.$$

</MathStatement>

Conservative fields are path-independent. Gravity and electrostatic force are conservative; friction is not.

## Divergence and Curl

Divergence and Curl represent the core differential operators in vector calculus, measuring expansion and rotation.

<MathStatement id="divergence-curl-def" kind="definition" title="Divergence and Curl" number="3">

Let $\mathbf{F} = P\mathbf{i} + Q\mathbf{j} + R\mathbf{k}$ be a differentiable vector field in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
1. The **divergence** of $\mathbf{F}$ is the scalar field defined by:
$$\operatorname{div} \mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}.$$
2. The **curl** of $\mathbf{F}$ is the vector field defined by:
$$\operatorname{curl} \mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \det \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ P & Q & R \end{pmatrix}$$
$$= \left( \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z} \right)\mathbf{i} + \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x} \right)\mathbf{j} + \left( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right)\mathbf{k}.$$

</MathStatement>

## Fundamental Vector Identities

We prove two identities that link conservative fields and rotational fields.

<MathStatement id="curl-gradient-zero" kind="theorem" title="Curl of a Gradient is Zero" number="1" proofId="curl-gradient-proof">

If $f(x, y, z)$ is a twice continuously differentiable scalar function, then:

$$\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}.$$

</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="curl-gradient-proof" for="curl-gradient-zero">

Let $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\mathbf{k}$.
Computing the curl of $\mathbf{F}$:

$$\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \det \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \end{pmatrix}$$
$$= \left( \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z \partial y} \right)\mathbf{i} + \left( \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial z} \right)\mathbf{j} + \left( \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} \right)\mathbf{k}.$$

Since $f$ has continuous second partial derivatives, by Clairaut's Theorem, the mixed partial derivatives are equal (e.g., $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial z} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z \partial y}$). Therefore, all components vanish:

$$\nabla \times (\nabla f) = 0\mathbf{i} + 0\mathbf{j} + 0\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{0}.$$

</MathProof>

This theorem implies that if a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative, its curl must be zero. The converse holds if the domain is simply connected.

<MathStatement id="div-curl-zero" kind="theorem" title="Divergence of a Curl is Zero" number="2" proofId="div-curl-proof">

If $\mathbf{F} = P\mathbf{i} + Q\mathbf{j} + R\mathbf{k}$ is a twice continuously differentiable vector field, then:

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0.$$

</MathStatement>

<MathProof id="div-curl-proof" for="div-curl-zero">

Let $\mathbf{G} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left( R_y - Q_z \right)\mathbf{i} + \left( P_z - R_x \right)\mathbf{j} + \left( Q_x - P_y \right)\mathbf{k}$.
Computing the divergence of $\mathbf{G}$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{G} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(R_y - Q_z) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(P_z - R_x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(Q_x - P_y)$$
$$= \left( \frac{\partial^2 R}{\partial x \partial y} - \frac{\partial^2 Q}{\partial x \partial z} \right) + \left( \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 R}{\partial y \partial x} \right) + \left( \frac{\partial^2 Q}{\partial z \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial z \partial y} \right).$$

Grouping terms by their function component:

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = \left( \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial y \partial z} - \frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial z \partial y} \right) + \left( \frac{\partial^2 Q}{\partial z \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 Q}{\partial x \partial z} \right) + \left( \frac{\partial^2 R}{\partial x \partial y} - \frac{\partial^2 R}{\partial y \partial x} \right).$$

By Clairaut's Theorem, the terms inside each parenthesis cancel out, yielding 0.

</MathProof>

The same field gives a concrete test case: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}=0$ and the scalar curl is $2$. Use the playground to inspect its geometry while keeping the calculation visible in the theorem statements above.
